# Bachelorette Party Planning Guide: Everything the MOH Needs to Know

*The timeline, the budget, the itinerary — and the logistics that trip up first-time MOHs*

**By Oscar Arana · July 1, 2026 · 12 min read**

---

If you just got asked to plan the bachelorette party, here is the first thing to know: the planning is the gift. The bride can tell the difference between a trip that was thought through and one that was thrown together two weeks before. What follows is the guide that gets you from zero to a fully planned bachelorette — in the right order, without the expensive surprises.

---

## Step 1: Have one conversation before you book anything

The most common bachelorette planning mistake is starting with the venue before confirming the basics. That one conversation with the bride should cover:

**What kind of experience does she actually want?** Not the Instagram version — the real version. Some brides want the Nashville bachelorette with a party bus and matching sashes. Others would genuinely prefer a wine country weekend or a beach house with their five closest people. Ask directly. "What would your perfect bachelorette look like?" gets you more useful information than any planning checklist.

**Who is on the guest list?** This determines everything from venue size to budget. The bachelorette guest list is typically the bridal party plus the bride's closest friends — often 6 to 14 people. Confirm who is included before doing any planning. Adding guests after the budget is set creates friction.

**What is the date range?** Bachelorettes typically happen four to eight weeks before the wedding. For destination trips, weekend availability depends on flights and everyone's work schedules. Get two or three candidate weekends from the bride before running a group poll.

**What is the budget range?** Ask the bride if she has a rough sense of what feels comfortable for the group. This is not a ceiling — it is a calibration. If she says "keep it reasonable" and you are planning a $600-per-person destination trip, you need to know that before deposits are made.

---

## Step 2: Run a clean group poll, collect commitments

Once you have the basics from the bride, send a quick poll to the guests covering:

1. **Dates** — Which weekends work? (Use a tool like When2Meet or just a simple poll in the group chat)
2. **Format** — Destination trip or local event?
3. **Budget range** — Give them three options with honest per-person estimates

Collect responses within five to seven days and move forward with what the majority can do. Bachelorette planning paralysis often comes from trying to accommodate everyone perfectly — you cannot. Make a decision, announce it clearly, and let people opt out gracefully if they genuinely cannot make it work.

**Collect deposits before booking anything.** This is the single most important rule. Do not book an Airbnb, pay a venue deposit, or make a restaurant reservation expecting reimbursement later. Collect money first, then book. Use Venmo, Zelle, or Splitwise. Be specific: "I need $150 from everyone by [date] to hold our reservation. Payments after that date may mean losing the spot."

---

## Step 3: Book the big pieces first

For a destination bachelorette, book in this order:

1. **The Airbnb or hotel block** — Availability for large groups is the scarcest resource, especially in popular bachelorette cities. Book this before flights.
2. **Flights** — Coordinate as a group but let people book independently. Trying to book everyone on the same flight creates unnecessary stress.
3. **The anchor activity** — Whatever the main event is: winery tour, spa day, dinner reservation, escape room, party bus. These book out fast.
4. **The dinner reservation** — Saturday night dinner for a large group of women needs four to eight weeks notice at most restaurants. OpenTable usually shows availability but call for groups over 8.
5. **Everything else** — Sashes, matching outfits, games, decorations. These can wait.

For a local bachelorette, the order is similar but compressed — book the venue and anchor activity first, coordinate everything else around them.

## The bachelorette itinerary that actually works

The mistake most MOHs make with the itinerary is over-scheduling. Every hour of a bachelorette weekend does not need an activity. The best itineraries have one anchor per half-day and breathing room between them.

### 3-Day Weekend Template (Friday – Sunday)

**Friday**
- Afternoon: Arrive, check in, settle
- Evening: Welcome dinner — somewhere easy, not the fancy reservation. This is the decompression meal, not the celebration meal.
- Night: Casual drinks at the house/hotel or one low-key bar

**Saturday**
- Morning: Sleep in, brunch, get ready at your own pace
- Afternoon: Anchor activity — spa, winery, beach, shopping, pool day, cooking class
- Late afternoon: Glam hour back at the house/hotel
- Evening: The dinner reservation — this is the actual celebration meal
- Night: Whatever the bride wants — could be clubs, could be one nice bar, could be back to the house for a fire pit and games

**Sunday**
- Morning: Brunch (the recovery meal — make it good)
- Mid-morning: Checkout, goodbyes, travel home

The unscheduled time on Saturday morning and between activities is where the real bonding happens. Do not fill it.

## Destination bachelorette: top 5 cities and what to expect

### Nashville, Tennessee
**Best for:** High-energy bar scenes, live music, line dancing, Honky Tonk Row  
**Budget:** $350–600/person (flights, Airbnb, activities)  
**Best time:** April–May, September–October  
**Skip if:** You want a relaxed or spa-focused weekend — Nashville's pace is relentless  

### New Orleans, Louisiana
**Best for:** Food culture, cocktails, French Quarter nights, slower mornings  
**Budget:** $300–500/person  
**Best time:** March–May, October–November (avoid summer heat and Mardi Gras crowds unless that's the point)  
**Best activity:** A private chef dinner or cocktail-making class before heading to Frenchmen Street

### Scottsdale, Arizona
**Best for:** Luxury spa weekends, desert pools, resort vibes, upscale dinners  
**Budget:** $500–900/person  
**Best time:** October–April (summer is legitimately dangerous heat)  
**Best activity:** A spa day at one of the resorts, followed by Old Town Scottsdale for dinner

### Charleston, South Carolina
**Best for:** Southern charm, historic walking tours, beach proximity, excellent restaurants  
**Budget:** $350–550/person  
**Best time:** March–May, September–November  
**Best activity:** A sunset boat tour on the harbor, followed by dinner on King Street

### Palm Springs, California
**Best for:** Pool weekends, desert modernism, chill vibes, mid-century style Airbnbs  
**Budget:** $400–700/person  
**Best time:** October–April  
**Best activity:** A private pool house Airbnb with a catered breakfast and a spa morning

## Bachelorette games that work (and which ones to skip)

### Games that consistently land

**"How well does she know her fiancé?"** — Before the trip, send the groom 10 questions about himself (favorite meal, most embarrassing moment, first thing he said to her, his dream vacation, etc.). Print his answers. At the party, the bride answers the same questions. Everyone scores how many she gets right. Easy setup, generates real conversation, genuinely funny.

**"Bingo through the night"** — Create a custom bingo card with things that might happen that night (someone buys us a drink, bride gets a compliment from a stranger, MOH cries, someone gets a phone number, etc.). First to complete a line wins a small prize. Keeps the group together even when the night gets scattered.

**"Advice for the bride"** — Give each guest a card. One side: "My best piece of marriage advice." Other side: "My most honest dating disaster." Share at dinner. Gets real, gets funny, gives the bride something to keep.

### Skip these

**Scavenger hunts with strangers** — Asking strangers to do things or having the bride approach strangers for dares creates uncomfortable situations and kills the vibe when someone says no.

**Elimination games** — Anything where guests get "out" and have to sit and watch others play is dead weight. Keep everyone in.

**Games that require sobriety to follow** — Complex rules are fine at hour one. By hour three, they fall apart.

## The budget tracker: how to split it so no one gets resentful

The cleanest bachelorette budget breakdown looks like this:

| Category | Estimate |
|----------|----------|
| Accommodation (Airbnb/hotel) | $800–1,800 |
| Activities (anchor event) | $50–150/person |
| Dinners | $60–120/person/night |
| Transportation | $100–200 total |
| Decorations & games | $80–150 total |
| Bride's share | Covered by group |

**Calculate the bride's portion of every shared cost, then divide that amount equally among the bridesmaids.** For example: if the total trip costs $800/person and there are 10 people including the bride, collect $889/person from the 9 paying guests to cover the bride's $800 share.

Use a shared spreadsheet to track who has paid, what has been booked, and what the running total is. A shared Google Sheet or a dedicated expense-tracker keeps everyone on the same page and eliminates the "I thought someone else handled that" moments that blow up bachelorette budgets.

## The day-of coordination list

The MOH's job on the day of the bachelorette is mostly invisible — keeping things on schedule, resolving small issues before the bride notices, and making sure the bride is having fun instead of managing logistics.

**The checklist:**
- Confirm the dinner reservation 48 hours in advance (large groups get released if not confirmed)
- Have cash for tips at the bar (apps don't work when there are 10 of you and the bartender is slammed)
- Keep the bride's bag charged — she will forget
- Have Tylenol, water bottles, and snacks at the house/hotel
- Know where the nearest Walgreens or CVS is (always needed for something)
- Set a departure time for every activity that is 15 minutes earlier than reality
- Have the Venmo requests sent before the trip so you are not collecting money poolside

## The one thing first-time MOHs get wrong

They start planning the experience before they know what experience the bride actually wants.

The best bachelorette is not the most elaborate one. It is the one where the bride felt like herself — not like she was performing someone's vision of a bachelorette. That starts with one honest conversation, not a Pinterest board.

Plan backward from the bride. Everything else is logistics.
