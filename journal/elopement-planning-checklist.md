# Elopement planning checklist: the 6-week and 6-month plans

*A 25-year wedding DJ on what makes a modern elopement work — the two timelines, the legal logistics, the four vendors that matter, and the trend of the 'elopement reception' that lets you have both.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 20, 2026 · 10 min read**

---

The reason elopements are growing is not the reason most people think. It is not that couples want to save money — many elopements end up costing $8,000 to $15,000 once the photographer, the destination, and the optional reception are added up. It is that couples want a *ceremony that is actually theirs* — not a logistical project that consumes eighteen months and pulls in a hundred people who half know each other.

I have been at a lot of large weddings. I have also been at a lot of small ones. The small ones, done well, are often the ones the couple talks about ten years later with the most love. The large ones are the ones they talk about with the most exhaustion.

This post is the checklist version of what to actually do — the timelines, the legal logistics, the four vendors that matter for an elopement, and the modern trend of having a separate reception weeks or months later so that family doesn't feel excluded.

## What counts as an elopement now

The old definition was *running off to a courthouse without telling anyone.* That version of elopement is now rare — fewer than 10 percent of modern elopements are secret.

The modern definition is a ceremony with **20 or fewer guests**, typically:

- No traditional wedding party (or a wedding party of two — one each)
- Outdoor, destination, or non-traditional location (national park, mountain overlook, beach, courthouse steps, backyard, friend's cabin)
- Officiant + couple + immediate family + a handful of closest friends
- No traditional reception immediately following — possibly a small dinner or family meal
- Optionally followed by a larger "elopement reception" weeks or months later

The line between "elopement" and "micro-wedding" is fuzzy and mostly about positioning. If the couple calls it an elopement, it is. If they call it a micro-wedding, that's a slightly different aesthetic. Both refer to the same general thing: a small intentional ceremony with intentionally few logistics.

## The 6-week timeline (domestic, minimal guests)

For a fast elopement — no destination travel, fewer than 10 guests, simple ceremony — six weeks is enough. The timeline:

### Week 6 (six weeks out)

- **Decide the location and date.** Outdoor location, courthouse, friend's backyard, hotel rooftop, public park (check permit requirements). The location and date constrain everything else, so lock both first.
- **Check the marriage license rules for the county.** Visit the county clerk's office website. Note: license cost, waiting period, validity window, witness requirements, officiant requirements. Most counties publish this clearly; if not, call.
- **Pick an officiant.** A friend can become a one-day officiant in most states by registering online (American Marriage Ministries, Universal Life Church). Allow two weeks for the registration to clear. Or hire a professional officiant for $250 to $600.
- **Tell immediate family.** Even if no family is attending, telling them now (rather than after the fact) usually goes over better.

### Week 4 (four weeks out)

- **Book a photographer.** For an elopement, the photographer is the most important vendor — the photos are the entire record of the day, and there is no second-best vendor here. Budget $1,200 to $3,500 for two to four hours of coverage. Look for photographers who specifically advertise elopements; their pricing structure is different from full-wedding photographers.
- **Buy rings.** If you don't already have them.
- **Decide on attire.** Most modern elopements skip the formal gown and tux. White dress, casual suit, all-white outfits, hiking clothes for a mountain elopement — anything goes. Allow alteration time if needed.

### Week 2 (two weeks out)

- **Get the marriage license.** This is the most-missed step. Many states have a validity window that means you can't get the license too early — California's is 90 days, but some are as short as 30. Get it close enough to the date that it will be valid, far enough that you have time to handle any paperwork issues.
- **Confirm the location permit, if required.** National Park ceremonies need permits well in advance — six weeks may not be enough for some parks (Yosemite and Zion regularly book six months out). State parks are usually faster.
- **Draft the ceremony script.** Even a five-minute ceremony benefits from a written script. The officiant says the legal words ("by the power vested in me…"); the couple can write their own vows or skip them.

### Week of

- **Witnesses confirmed.** Most states require two; the photographer often serves as one, and a family member or friend as the other.
- **Day-of attire and small details laid out the night before.** Skip the elaborate getting-ready morning that traditional weddings have. Most elopements get ready in 60 to 90 minutes.
- **Plan a meal after the ceremony.** Even if it's just a restaurant reservation for six, plan it. The "what do we do now" feeling after an elopement ceremony is real, and a destination meal frames the day.

## The 6-month timeline (destination, family flying in)

For a destination elopement — a different state, a national park, or a more orchestrated event — give yourself six months. Most of the additional time is travel logistics for guests, not ceremony complexity.

### Month 6

- **Pick the destination and date.** This is the slowest decision because it interacts with photographer availability, permit windows, season, and guest travel costs.
- **Photographer first.** Destination elopement photographers book up four to eight months in advance for peak seasons (May–October domestic, year-round for tropical destinations). Book this before anything else.
- **Officiant research.** Some destinations have specific officiant requirements (Hawaii, for example, requires a licensed Hawaii officiant). Some are easier (mainland U.S. with self-uniting options).

### Month 4

- **Guest communication.** If you are bringing immediate family, send the dates and rough plan. They need time to book flights and hotels. A simple email is fine — formal invitations are optional and arguably anti-elopement.
- **Lock travel for yourselves.** Flights and accommodation for the elopement location.
- **Permits.** National Park ceremony permits, public-land permits, beach permits — handle now. Most parks have a 4-6 month booking window for ceremony permits with more than two people.

### Month 2

- **Marriage license logistics.** For destination elopements, you usually need the license from the *destination* state, not your home state. Check waiting periods carefully — Hawaii requires the license to be picked up in person, with both parties present, at a designated location. Plan an extra day on the front of the trip.
- **Local vendor coordination.** If you are hiring local florals, hair and makeup, or a local meal venue, book now.
- **Attire finalized.** Especially for destination elopements where you can't easily run back for alterations.

### Month 1

- **Detailed day-of schedule.** Even a small elopement benefits from a one-page timeline — when you'll get to the location, when the ceremony starts, when the photographer needs you, when dinner is.
- **Witnesses confirmed.** If guests are coming, two of them serve as witnesses. If no guests, the photographer + a venue staff member or local hire can serve.

### Week of

- **Pick up the license.** Allow a half day for the in-person pickup if your destination requires it.
- **Confirm all vendors.** Photographer, officiant, hair and makeup, dinner reservation.
- **Print the license details and the ceremony script.** Yes, on paper. Phones die on hiking trails.

## The legal logistics (state-by-state)

Five variables that change by state — check the county clerk's website for the specific location of the ceremony, because some state rules have county-level interpretations.

### 1. License cost

Range: $35 (in some Texas counties) to $115 (in some California counties). The cost has no relationship to the wedding's significance — it's purely administrative. Most clerks accept cash or credit card; some require cash.

### 2. Waiting period

The time between getting the license and being legally allowed to use it.

- **Zero-day waiting period:** California, Texas, Florida, Nevada, Hawaii (for most counties), most others.
- **Three-day waiting period:** New York, Wisconsin (six business days), Pennsylvania (three days waivable).
- **Same-day allowed only with waiver:** Some states allow a judge to waive the waiting period for a fee.

Plan the license pickup around the waiting period. A Friday wedding with a three-day waiting period means picking up the license by Tuesday.

### 3. License validity window

How long the license is valid before it expires.

- **30 days:** Many states.
- **60-90 days:** California (90), Texas (90).
- **One year:** Pennsylvania.

Don't pick up the license too early. If you elope and forget to file the signed license before it expires, you're not legally married.

### 4. Witness requirements

- **Two witnesses required:** Most states.
- **One witness required:** A few (Vermont).
- **Zero witnesses required:** Colorado, Pennsylvania, Maine — these states allow self-uniting marriages, no officiant or witness required.

If you're at a public-land ceremony without a photographer-serving-as-witness, this matters.

### 5. Officiant requirements

- **Ordained or registered officiant required:** Most states.
- **One-day deputization available:** Massachusetts, California, others — a friend can become an officiant for the day.
- **Self-uniting ceremonies allowed:** Colorado, Pennsylvania, Maine — no officiant required.

For destination elopements, check whether your home-state-ordained friend is recognized in the ceremony state. Hawaii, for example, requires a Hawaii-licensed officiant; an out-of-state ordination doesn't work.

## The four vendors that actually matter

For an elopement, the vendor list is brutally short — and getting it right matters more than it does at a 150-guest wedding, because each vendor is a larger share of the day.

### 1. Photographer

The single most important vendor. The photos are the entire record of the day, the only thing you'll show family and friends who weren't there, and the artifact that lives on the wall for forty years. Elopement photographers are a specific category — they understand outdoor light, hiking-to-locations, small-group dynamics, and the rhythm of a 2-hour event. Budget $1,200 to $3,500.

### 2. Officiant

A wedding without a strong officiant feels rushed. A one-day-ordained friend can do this well — but they need a written script and a rehearsal. A professional officiant costs $250 to $600 and brings experience with the legal language, ceremony pacing, and the small touches that make a five-minute ceremony feel substantial.

### 3. Hair and makeup (if wanted)

Optional but worth considering. A mobile hair and makeup artist for the elopement morning costs $150 to $400 depending on location. The photos benefit, and the morning feels less stressful than DIY.

### 4. Meal after

A reservation at a restaurant, a private chef in a vacation rental, or a venue catering meal. Even for a 4-person elopement, the "what now" moment after the ceremony is real, and a planned meal frames the day. Budget $80 to $200 per person.

That's it. No DJ, no florist (usually), no transportation coordinator, no venue contract negotiation, no save-the-dates, no invitations, no caterer, no rental company, no bar service, no day-of coordinator (most elopement photographers serve this function). The vendor surface area is 5 percent of a traditional wedding.

## The "elopement reception" trend

The fastest-growing format in 2026 is the *elopement plus reception-later* combo. Couple elopes in May with immediate family. They throw a backyard reception in September with 50 to 80 friends. The reception is not a wedding — there is no ceremony, no first dance protocol, no formal toasts. It is a party with a clear announcement and a celebration.

The benefits:

- **Two intentional events instead of one rushed one.** The ceremony gets to be private and meaningful. The party gets to be public and fun.
- **Lower total cost.** A small elopement ($5,000) plus a backyard reception ($8,000) is still cheaper than a 150-guest traditional wedding ($35,000 average).
- **No vendor cascade.** The reception doesn't need a venue contract, transportation, a coordinator, or a DJ if you don't want one (a good playlist on speakers is enough).
- **Less stress.** Two manageable events vs. one massive one.

The downsides:

- **Some family takes the elopement personally.** Even with a reception planned, a parent who wasn't at the ceremony can feel excluded. Communicate the plan early — not after the elopement.
- **Two waves of planning.** Lower-intensity but spread out. Some couples find this freeing; others find it dragged out.

If you're considering this format, the planning stack is the same as for the ceremony — except the reception can be planned in 8 to 12 weeks once the ceremony is done.

## What you don't actually need

Things that elopement-niche Pinterest will tell you to buy that you almost certainly don't need:

- **A "first look" video crew.** The photographer can do this.
- **A bouquet preservation service.** Save the petals if you want, but most couples never look at the preserved version.
- **A formal "elopement dress" different from your normal nice clothes.** Wear what you'll feel good in.
- **A wedding website.** Skip it. For an elopement with 8 family members, a group text is fine.
- **An elopement-specific planner if the location is domestic.** Most domestic elopements need 4-6 hours of total planning from the couple. A planner is overkill.
- **Custom signage, place cards, programs.** None of these are required for an elopement and most photograph poorly in outdoor light.

## Where the printed elements fit in

For elopements with guests — 6 to 20 people — there are a few small printed items that earn their cost:

- **A one-page itinerary card** for guests flying in (the [wedding itinerary template](/journal/wedding-itinerary-template/) post covers the format)
- **A simple ceremony program** for guests at the ceremony (one folded card, 4-6 lines, the readings and the officiant name)
- **Thank-you notes** sent within 30 days to guests who traveled
- **A small "we eloped" announcement** if you want to send physical mail to family who wasn't there

Skip menus, escort cards, ceremony backdrop signage. The setting *is* the signage at an elopement.
