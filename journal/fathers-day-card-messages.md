# What to write in a Father's Day card (from a guy who prints a thousand of them every June)

*An honest guide to writing card messages that don't sound like the inside of a Hallmark — by archetype, by relationship, by what the dad in your life actually wants to read.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 9, 2026 · 10 min read**

---

The week before Father's Day, the mini-lab where I work runs about two hundred custom photo cards a day. Customers come in with a photograph and a one-line message they want printed on the back. Most of them ask me to read the message before I run the job, partly because they want a second opinion and partly because they do not entirely trust their own writing.

I have read a lot of Father's Day card messages this way. Most of them are bad. The bad ones are not bad because the writer does not love their dad — they almost always do — they are bad because the writer fell into the same three traps everyone falls into the moment they try to write something heartfelt under pressure.

This article is what I have learned about those three traps and how to avoid them. It is also a working set of message templates, by archetype and by relationship, that I have personally watched land at the counter when a customer reads them back to themselves and immediately tears up a little. If you are reading this two days before Father's Day with a blank card in front of you, this is the article I would hand you.

## The three traps everyone falls into

Almost every weak Father's Day card message I read at the counter falls into one of these three traps. Recognize them and you have already done 80% of the work of writing a card that lands.

**Trap one: Opening with "Happy Father's Day."** This is the universal default and it is also the universal flatline. "Happy Father's Day, Dad. Thank you for everything." Skip it. The card already has "Father's Day" printed on the front — opening the inside with the same words wastes the most valuable line in the message. Start with a specific memory instead. "I still remember the time you drove four hours to fix my flat tire." That is an opener.

**Trap two: Vague gratitude.** "Thank you for everything you do." "You're the best dad ever." "I appreciate everything about you." These all read as sincere and they all read as identical. The dad in your life will know it is from you, and he will be grateful, but the line is interchangeable with the line his coworker also wrote on a card that morning. *Specific* gratitude is the upgrade. "Thank you for never once telling me my college major was a waste of money." That sentence cannot be written by anyone else about anyone else.

**Trap three: Writing too much.** A panicked card-writer overcorrects by filling the entire inside of the card with text. The dad reads three sentences, eyes glaze, sets the card down. A tighter card — three to five sentences, hand-written, with white space around the writing — gets read all the way through and re-read. If you want to write more, write a separate letter and tuck it inside the card. The card itself stays short.

That is the entire framework. Open with specificity. Be specific in the gratitude. Keep it short. We are now going to look at the actual templates.

## The working formula (three sentences, every time)

The formula I have watched land at the counter every single time is the same three sentences, in the same order.

**Sentence one:** One specific memory or trait. Not "you're a great dad." Not "thank you for everything." A scene, a habit, an action, a moment. The smaller the better.

**Sentence two:** What that memory or trait meant to you — the "so what." This is the part most card messages skip. Without it, the memory is just a memory. With it, the memory becomes a piece of evidence for who he is.

**Sentence three:** Present-tense gratitude or affection. "I am lucky you are my dad." "I love you." "I am glad you are still here for this." One line. Resist the temptation to add a fourth.

Example, fully written:

> I still think about the way you used to pack the cooler before every road trip — the cold drinks at the top, the sandwiches in the middle, the ice block at the bottom so nothing got soggy. I never realized as a kid how much of growing up safe and fed was you quietly engineering the small things so the rest of us did not have to think about them. I am lucky to have you. Happy Father's Day, Dad.

Three sentences. Four if you count the closing. Reads in fifteen seconds. The cooler line is what makes it work — it is the kind of memory only the writer would have, written about the specific dad it is for. The "so what" line ("I never realized as a kid...") turns the cooler memory into evidence of a larger pattern. The closing is short and present-tense.

That is the entire structure. Memory → meaning → gratitude. From here it is a question of which memory and which meaning fit which dad.

> The cooler line is the gift. The Happy Father's Day line is the wrapping paper.

## Templates by dad archetype

These are starting points. The work is in the specificity — you have to swap in the actual memory, the actual trait, the actual line that fits your specific dad. But the structures below are working scaffolds.

### For the dad who never said "I love you" out loud

> I do not think you and I have said the words "I love you" out loud more than a handful of times in my whole life. That has never bothered me, because I have always known it from the way you show up — every game, every move, every call when I needed help with the car. I love you, Dad. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: name the thing you both know is true (that he is not a "say it out loud" dad) and then say it out loud anyway. This is the message that makes that dad cry quietly while reading the card alone in the kitchen. He will not show you he cried. He will, however, keep the card.

### For the dad who taught you a specific skill

> I think about you every time I parallel park — I still hear your voice saying "your steering wheel turn one full rotation, then ease it back." Twenty years later, that lesson is the one I use most weeks. Thank you for the small things you taught me that I did not realize were lessons at the time. I love you, Dad. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: name the specific lesson, name the specific situation where you still use it, then thank him for the *category* of lesson (small things he taught you) rather than just the one example. This message works for any practical skill — driving, fixing, cooking, throwing, fishing, hammering, the small mechanical or operational things dads tend to teach.

### For the dad who packed your lunch / made breakfast / handled the small daily things

> The image I have most clearly from being a kid is you in the kitchen at 6 am, making breakfast for all of us, before going to work an hour later. I did not understand at the time how tired you must have been. Thank you for showing me what showing up looks like, every day, on no sleep, without complaint. I love you, Dad.

The hook: a small daily scene that you only later understood as the thing it actually was. This is the message for the dad who quietly did the operational work of being a parent — the cooking, the driving, the laundry, the school pickups — and never asked for credit. He has been waiting his entire life to hear that you noticed.

### For the dad who taught you how to work

> Every job I have ever had, I have shown up early. Every project I have ever finished, I have finished it the right way. Both of those things are because of you. You did not lecture me about work ethic — you just lived one in front of me my entire childhood, and I absorbed it before I knew what it was. Thank you for that. I love you, Dad. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: name the trait you got from him, name how he transmitted it (by living it, not by talking about it), and acknowledge that you internalized it before you understood it. This is the message for the dad whose work ethic shaped you, even if you do not work in his field.

### For the dad who showed up after a hard time

> I have not told you this directly, but the night you drove out to get me when I called you from that bad situation in 2014 — that was the moment I knew I would never not be your kid, no matter how badly I messed something up. You did not lecture me. You did not even ask what happened. You just drove out and brought me home. Thank you. I love you, Dad.

The hook: a specific moment when he proved unconditional support and never made it about himself. This message is for the dad who showed up during a divorce, a breakup, a hospital stay, a financial disaster, an arrest, a hard year. Almost everyone has a moment like this with their dad; almost nobody has ever named it back to him out loud.

### For the dad who is becoming a grandfather (or already is)

> Watching you become a grandfather has been one of the gifts of my adult life. You with my kids is the version of you I did not get to see as a kid — slower, sillier, more present, easier with the small moments. They are going to remember you forever, in the way I do, and I hope they understand what they had. Thank you for being the dad you are to me and the grandfather you are to them. I love you, Dad. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: bridge two generations in one paragraph and acknowledge that he is a different (and better) version of himself with your kids than he was as a young dad with you — and that is OK and you are glad to watch it. This message is the one that lands hardest for dads in their sixties and seventies, because nobody else is writing it to them.

### For the dad you have not always gotten along with

> You and I have not always seen things the same way. We have had years that were harder than they needed to be, and there are conversations I wish we had handled differently — on both sides. What I want to say today is that I see how much you have done for me, even in the years where I did not always show that I saw it. Thank you for the things you gave me that I am only starting to understand now. I love you, Dad.

The hook: name the difficulty, name your own role in it, and shift to gratitude without demanding resolution. This is the hardest card to write and the one that has the largest impact. The dad reads it knowing it was hard for you to write, and that itself is the message.

### For a dad who is no longer here

> I have been carrying around a list of things I never said to you, and today I am writing the most important one down: I am the person I am because of you, and I think about you constantly. The way you laughed. The way you packed the trunk before every trip. The way you said my name. I miss you, Dad. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: this is a card for yourself, not for him — but you write it as if to him anyway. Tuck it into a notebook or a box. The act of writing it is the gift. Many people write the same card every year on Father's Day and read the previous years' cards alongside it. It becomes a quiet ritual.

## Templates by relationship (not just for biological dads)

### For a stepdad

> Thank you for stepping into a job you did not have to take. You did not have to drive me to soccer practice. You did not have to sit through the awkward years. You did not have to learn what my favorite cereal was. You did all of it anyway, and you did it without ever asking for credit. You are one of my dads now, and that is because of how you showed up. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: name what he did not have to do, name that he did it anyway, name that the relationship is now real because of his choices. Skip "Happy Father's Day" as the opening line; this opening lands harder.

### For a father-in-law

> Thank you for raising the person I married. I see you in [her/him/them] every day — in the way [she/he/they] [specific trait the partner has]. That trait is one of the reasons I built a life with [her/him/them], and I know it came from you. Thank you for shaping the person who is now my family. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: name a specific trait of the partner that came from him. This requires you to actually identify a trait and name where it came from — which is the kind of specific gratitude that makes a father-in-law put the card on his desk and keep it there.

### For a grandfather

> One of the things I am most grateful for as an adult is the memories I have of you from when I was small. The smell of your workshop. The way you used to let me ride on your shoulders. The specific way you said my name. I carry those with me. Thank you for being the kind of grandfather a kid still remembers as an adult. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: three specific sensory memories, tied to a single sentence of gratitude. Sensory memories (smell, sound, physical sensation) land harder than abstract memories for grandparent cards because the writer is often pulling from childhood and the senses are the parts that come back first.

### For your kids' dad (from a partner)

> Watching you be a dad has been one of the joys of my life. The patience you have with [child's name] in [specific recurring moment]. The way you [specific habit or ritual]. The version of you I see in those moments is the version I fell in love with, deepened. Thank you for being the dad you are to our kids. I love you. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: a single recurring scene from him being a dad, named specifically, framed as evidence of who he is. Avoid generic "you are a great dad" lines and pull from the kitchen, the bedtime routine, the school drop-off, the playground — whatever moment is recurring in your household.

### For a dad-figure who is not technically a dad (a mentor, an uncle, a coach)

> I do not have a card for this exact relationship, so I am writing one. You have been one of the men who shaped me — in [specific way he shaped you, like "the way you taught me to think about my career" or "the patience you had with me when I was an angry teenager"]. I do not always get the chance to tell the people in my life that they matter. Today I am taking the chance. Thank you. Happy Father's Day.

The hook: acknowledge that the card category does not quite fit, then name exactly why you are writing it anyway. This is the message that lands hardest for the uncle, the coach, the mentor, the family friend who quietly mattered. He has not received a Father's Day card before. He will keep this one for the rest of his life.

## The one-page letter, when the card is not enough

If the relationship deserves more than a three-sentence card message, write a separate one-page letter and tuck it inside the card. The card stays short and clean; the letter does the deeper work.

The letter structure is the same as the card structure, only longer. Three specific memories (one paragraph each), what each one meant to you (one or two sentences inside each paragraph), and a closing paragraph of present-tense gratitude. One page, hand-written if your handwriting is decent, typed and printed on real paper if it isn't.

The dad in your life will read the card first, set it down, then open the letter. He will read the letter twice. The letter will end up in the drawer with the other letters that mean something. The drawer is the only drawer in his house that does not get cleaned out.

I have written one of these letters before, to my own dad, and received one from each of my own kids. The drawer in our house has both. I look at them sometimes.

---

The card is small. The message is small. The dad in your life will not remember the design on the front of the card a month from now. He will, however, remember whether the inside named something specific that nobody else would have known to name. Write the line nobody else would write. That is the whole job.

If you are writing the card tonight and you have ten minutes, here is the working plan:

1. Pick one specific memory or trait — small, concrete, not "you're a great dad."
2. Write one sentence about what that memory or trait gave you or taught you.
3. Close with a one-line present-tense gratitude or affection.
4. Sign it.
5. Tuck the card into a wrapped gift, or leave it on the kitchen table for Sunday morning.

That is the entire workflow. Three sentences. Ten minutes. Lands harder than the card you spent an hour agonizing over with vague gratitude. The dad in your life is waiting to be seen specifically. Specificity is the gift.

## Frequently asked questions

**What should I write in a Father's Day card if I have no idea what to say?**

Skip "Happy Father's Day" and write one specific memory instead. Open with the memory: "I still think about the time you drove four hours to fix my flat tire." Add what it meant: "I never told you, but that was the moment I realized you would show up for me no matter what." Close with a single line of gratitude: "I am lucky you are my dad. Happy Father's Day." Three sentences. Twenty minutes. Lands harder than every pre-printed card on the shelf at the drugstore, because it names something only you know.

**What is the formula for a Father's Day card message that does not feel generic?**

Three parts: (1) one specific memory or trait — not "you're a great dad" but "the way you packed the cooler before every road trip." (2) what that memory or trait taught you, gave you, or meant to you — the "so what" line. (3) one sentence of present-tense gratitude or affection. The order matters: specific → meaning → present-tense. The generic Father's Day card opens with the gratitude line and never gets specific. Reversing that order is the trick.

**How long should a Father's Day card message be?**

Between three sentences and one page. Three sentences is the floor: specific memory, what it meant, gratitude line. One page is the ceiling — anything longer reads as a wedding speech and the dad will feel pressured to read it carefully. The sweet spot is three to five sentences, written by hand, on one side of the card. If you want to write more, write a separate letter and tuck it inside the card. The card itself stays tight.

**What should a son write versus what should a daughter write?**

Less than you would think. The "I have always wanted to tell you this" opening lands for both, the specific-memory structure lands for both, the present-tense gratitude lands for both. The one place there is a real difference: sons are more likely to skip the emotional close and a daughter is more likely to skip the practical opener. Cross the wires intentionally. A son writing "I love you" as the closing line lands hard precisely because he does not always say it; a daughter writing "thank you for teaching me how to change a tire" lands hard precisely because she names a specific lesson. Write the line your dad has not heard from you specifically.

**Is it weird to give my stepdad or my father-in-law a Father's Day card?**

Not at all — but the framing matters. Skip "Happy Father's Day" as the opening line, because depending on the relationship that line can read awkward or presumptuous. Instead open with the role you actually have with him: "thank you for showing up" for a stepdad, "thank you for raising the person I married" for a father-in-law, "I am glad you are part of our family" for either. Name the specific relationship and the gratitude becomes specific instead of awkward. End with "Happy Father's Day" as a closing line where it lands lighter.

**What should I write to a dad who has passed away?**

If you are writing it for yourself: write the letter you wish you had given him. Three specific things you appreciate, three things you wish you had said, one paragraph about the person you became because of him. Keep it. The act of writing it is the gift to yourself. If you are giving the card to someone whose dad has passed (a mother, sibling, friend), write one specific memory you have of the dad, name what he gave the recipient, and end with "I am thinking of you and of him today." Do not avoid mentioning him; the silence is the harder thing.
