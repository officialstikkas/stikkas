# What dad actually wants for Father's Day

*An honest gift guide from a print specialist who happens to also be a dad, and who spends his days printing other people's gifts.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 2, 2026 · 7 min read**

---

Every year around the first week of June a particular type of customer walks into the lab. They are holding their phone, on which is a photograph from 2014, and they want it printed before Father's Day. They are stressed, slightly apologetic, and asking me whether I think dad would prefer it in a frame or in an envelope. The honest answer to that question is "he would prefer that you spent two minutes thinking about it earlier," but I do not say that. I print the photograph.

I am also a dad. I have spent the last decade on the receiving end of Father's Day gifts. Some of them I still use. Most of them are in a drawer. A small handful I genuinely loved and remember years later, and the pattern of what those gifts had in common is interesting enough that I think it is worth writing down.

This is the gift guide I would give my own family if they were honest enough to ask. It is also the guide I would give anyone shopping for the dad in their life who already has — let's be honest — too many ties, mugs, and "World's Best Dad" novelty items.

## The five categories of Father's Day gift

After years of watching what gets printed, framed, wrapped, and ultimately used, I think every Father's Day gift falls into one of five categories. The category determines whether the gift lands or whether it goes in the drawer.

**Category one: the gag gift.** "World's Best Farter, I Mean Father." Funny once. Then it lives at the bottom of a sock drawer for a decade. Skip.

**Category two: the duty gift.** A tie, a wallet, a generic cologne. He will thank you sincerely. He will not use it. The gift signals that you remembered the day, which is meaningful, but the object itself is dead weight. The tie joins the other ties.

**Category three: the consumable.** Good whiskey, his coffee, a really nice steak from the butcher. These land *every time* because they get used inside of a week and the use is associated with you. The only downside is they're gone in a week.

**Category four: the experience.** A round of golf, tickets to a game, a dinner. Lands hard for the right dad. Lands wrong for the dad who would rather be home. You have to know the dad.

**Category five: the personal object.** A printed photograph, a framed memory, a hand-written letter, a small book of something only your family knows about. These land hardest. They also take the most thought, which is why most people skip them and end up at the tie.

The gift guide that follows is a tour through category five — the personal-object gift, which I think is the only category that consistently outlasts the day.

## The printed photograph (still the best gift)

I am biased because I print photographs for a living, but I am also biased because *I have eight printed photographs of my kids on my desk at the lab*, and none of them are from this year, and I look at them every day.

A printed photograph is a category-five gift because it does three things at once. It says "I thought about you." It says "I picked a specific memory." And it says "I made it real" — out of the phone, off the cloud, onto paper that lives somewhere visible.

What works:

- **An 8×10 in a simple frame.** Black or natural wood. The frame is not the gift; the photograph is. A nice 8×10 print on lustre paper costs about ten dollars at a real lab, and the frame is twenty at any home goods store. Total: thirty dollars, fifteen minutes of effort. The dad in your life will keep it on his desk forever.
- **A small print album.** Twelve to twenty photographs, three to four years of the family, printed as 4×6s in a small linen-cover album. A few hundred dollars at a real photo book service, eighty if you assemble the prints yourself with a sleeve album from a stationery store. Goes on the bookshelf. Comes off the bookshelf every Christmas.
- **A panoramic print of somewhere meaningful.** The trail he hikes, the porch of the house he grew up in, the view from the family cabin. Twelve dollars for an 8×24 lustre print. Lands harder than you'd expect because dads have specific places.

The mistake people make with photograph gifts is using their phone's screenshot. Phone screenshots are 72 dpi and they print fuzzy at 8×10. Use the original from the camera roll, or — better — the photograph you took with the camera you bought ten years ago and forgot about. The dpi math forgives you if the source is good.

> A printed photograph is the only gift in the entire category that goes on a desk and stays there.

## The printable gift (no shipping, lands the same day)

The hidden category of Father's Day gifts is the digital download. You buy it at 11 pm on June 14, you print it at 7 am on June 15 on the home printer, and it lands with the cup of coffee at breakfast. No shipping anxiety, no inventory, no waiting.

The printables that work for dads are not generic. They are specific.

A **goal-setting planner** for the dad who has been talking about starting the side project for two years. A **budget planner** for the dad who told you he wants to retire at sixty-five. A **habit tracker** for the dad trying to get back to the gym. The unifying thread is that the printable acknowledges a *specific thing the dad has been wanting to do.* That is the gift. The printable is the wrapper.

This is also why a bundle works better than a single planner for the dad you are not sure about. A bundle hedges across "he might be in goal-mode" and "he might be in budget-mode" and "he might be in habit-mode" — and it lets him decide which one to actually open. The act of choosing is part of the gift.

For Father's Day specifically, the bundles that work are the ones built around the *idea of a fresh start*. Goals, habits, finance. The vibe is not "you should be more disciplined." The vibe is "this is the year you actually do the thing." Sage and gold work better than navy and gray; the cover should look like a gift, not a textbook.

## The handwritten letter (the one thing nobody does)

If you are reading this two days before Father's Day and you have no plan, the best gift in this entire list is one you can do tonight in twenty minutes.

Write a one-page letter, by hand, on real paper, telling the dad in your life three specific things you appreciate about him. Not vague things — "you're a great dad" — but specific things. The way he packs the car for road trips. The way he answered the phone at 2 am when you called from college. The dumb joke he made at your brother's wedding that nobody else remembers.

Fold it. Put it in an envelope. Hand it to him on Sunday.

I have done this. I have had this done to me. The letter ends up in a drawer with the other letters that mean something, and the drawer is the only drawer in the house that does not get cleaned out. The letter outlasts every tie.

If your handwriting is bad, type it on a computer and print it on real paper. The format matters less than the specificity.

## The one thing dads do not want

I am going to be direct about this because someone needs to say it.

Dads do not want a mug that says "Best Dad Ever." We own twelve mugs. We received them. We use one. The other eleven live in the cabinet under the microwave.

Dads do not want a tie unless the dad specifically wears ties. If the dad in your life last wore a tie at a funeral in 2019, do not buy him a tie.

Dads do not want generic cologne. If you do not know which cologne the dad wears, the cologne is going in the drawer.

Dads do not want a Father's Day-themed novelty hat, t-shirt, socks, or apron. We will wear it exactly once, on June 15, to be polite, and then it will live in the back of the closet until you forget about it and we donate it.

The thing dads want is to be seen specifically. Every gift on the personal-object list — the photograph, the printable that matches the goal, the handwritten letter — is a specific gift. Every gift on the novelty list is a generic gift. The category of "Father's Day gift for dad" generates generic gifts because it is built on the assumption that "dad" is a single category instead of a specific person. Resist that assumption.

> The gift that lands is the gift that proves you noticed which dad you were buying for.

## The plan, if you are reading this on June 14

If you are reading this article the night before Father's Day, here is a working plan:

1. Pick the one specific thing your dad has been talking about doing this year. Side project, retirement, fitness goal, finally writing the book, getting better at the grill — whatever it is.
2. Find a printable that matches that thing. A goal-setting kit, a budget planner, a habit tracker. Spend ten minutes on Etsy.
3. Print it on the best paper you have in the house. If you have heavy white paper or matte presentation paper, use that. If you only have copy paper, that is fine.
4. Write a one-page letter, by hand, telling him you noticed the thing he's been talking about and you bought him a tool for it.
5. Fold the letter, slip it into the printed planner, hand it to him on Sunday.

Total cost: maybe ten dollars. Total time: about an hour. The gift lands harder than the eighty-dollar gift that arrives in a box.

If you have more time and want to add something tangible, print an 8×10 photograph at any real photo lab. They will have it ready in an hour. Total combined cost: about twenty-two dollars. Total time: maybe ninety minutes. You are done.

---

The dads in your life are not difficult to buy for. They are easy to buy for once you accept that the gift they actually want is not on the shelf at the department store. It is at the print shop, in a printable, or in your own handwriting.

The next Father's Day is going to come around at the same time as the last one. The dad in your life will still be specifically himself. Plan accordingly.
