# How to plan a bridal shower: the complete guide for MOH and co-hosts

*The timeline, the budget, the logistics, and the parts first-time planners always miss*

**By Oscar Arana · June 23, 2026 · 14 min read**

---

If you are the maid of honor planning your first bridal shower, the first thing to know is that there is no single right way to do it. The second thing to know is that the parts that go wrong — wrong venue booking, wrong timeline, wrong guest list, costs nobody agreed to — almost always go wrong in the first two weeks, before anything is confirmed.

What follows is the planning guide that gets you from "I said yes to MOH" to "the bride opened her last gift and it was perfect" — in the right order, without the common first-timer mistakes.

---

## The planning timeline: twelve weeks to shower day

### Twelve weeks out: get aligned before you spend a dollar

Before you book anything, before you even look at venues, have one conversation with the right people:

**Talk to the bride.** Specifically: guest count preference, venue style preference, and date range. Not every bride wants a large formal party. Not every bride wants a home gathering. Some brides have a best-friend group and a work group that should not overlap. Ask. The worst bridal shower is the one the MOH planned based on what she thought the bride wanted.

**Talk to the co-hosts.** Who is pitching in, and how much? The bride's mother? The groom's mother? Other bridesmaids? Get commitments in writing (a group text works) before any deposit is made. The number one budget conflict at bridal showers comes from one person assuming another person is covering a line item.

**Establish the budget.** Total number, not per-person. Then divide by the number of contributors. Be specific: "We are spending $800 total. Four of us are splitting it equally — $200 each, due before we book the venue." A round number, a clear split, an explicit deadline.

---

### Ten weeks out: book the venue

For anything other than a home shower, venue availability is the first external constraint. Private dining rooms at restaurants, private event spaces, tea rooms, wine bars, cooking studios — all of them can book four to eight weeks in advance for a Saturday afternoon during wedding season. Ten weeks gives you real options.

**Venue types that work well:**

*Home hosting (host's home or family home).* The lowest-cost option and often the most personal. Works well for a guest list under 25. Requires more logistics coordination (catering or potluck, setup, parking, rental chairs if needed) but gives you complete control over the atmosphere.

*Restaurant private room.* The easiest to execute for a first-time planner. Most restaurants handle setup, service, and cleanup. You typically pay a per-person minimum or a room fee. Ask: is there a room minimum? Is there a separate service charge or gratuity? Is the bar included or billed separately? Can we bring our own cake or dessert?

*Tea room or brunch venue.* Popular for showers, often pre-styled with the kind of table setting that reads well in photos. Typically offer a fixed menu rather than à la carte. Book early — these venues are in demand.

*Event rental space or studio.* Maximum flexibility. You bring in your own catering, decor, and setup. Higher effort, but if the bride has a specific aesthetic or you are planning a themed event, the blank canvas is worth it.

**Questions to ask every venue before you book:**

- What is the capacity for a seated, served event?
- Is there a venue rental fee, a food minimum, or both?
- Can we bring our own cake, and is there a cutting fee?
- Is there a dedicated point of contact for the event?
- What is the deposit and cancellation policy?
- Is the parking situation manageable for a guest list that may include older family members?

---

### Eight weeks out: invitations

The rule: invitations go out six weeks before the shower. Eight weeks out, you are writing and ordering them.

**What every invitation needs:**
- Bride's name (and groom's name if relevant for the context)
- Date, start time, and expected end time
- Venue name and full address
- Dress code if any (garden party attire, cocktail, casual)
- RSVP deadline and contact info
- Registry information or "registry information available on request" — registry details belong in the invitation, not the envelope

**Guest list logistics:** The bride should approve the final guest list before invitations go out. Specifically: are you sure which family members are invited? Are the groom's family members on the list? Are there any coworkers the bride would or would not include?

**Digital vs. paper:** Digital invitations (Paperless Post, Evite, or a simple email) are completely acceptable and make RSVP tracking much easier. Paper invitations are traditional and read as more formal. Either is fine; mixing both creates tracking confusion.

---

### Six weeks out: RSVPs, headcount, and menu

Your RSVP deadline should fall at six weeks minus ten days — five weeks and a few days before the shower. That gives you two weeks to chase non-responders before your headcount locks for the venue.

**Chase non-responders personally.** A second invitation email gets ignored. A text or call from the MOH gets an answer. Do not let the headcount slip because you did not want to feel pushy. Chase.

**Lock the headcount at four weeks out.** Most venues need a firm headcount three to four weeks before the event to finalize catering and room setup.

**Menu decisions:**
- *Brunch showers:* Easiest. Mimosas, pastries, quiche, fruit — crowd-pleasers that work for all dietary needs.
- *Lunch showers:* Slightly more formal. Salads, sandwiches, a main, and dessert.
- *Afternoon tea:* Fixed menu, venue-managed, minimal planning.
- *Dinner showers:* Increasingly common for intimate friend-group showers. More expensive, more adult atmosphere.

Confirm dietary restrictions when collecting RSVPs. At least one guest in most groups has a vegetarian, gluten-free, or allergy accommodation. Do not find out at the table.

---

### Four weeks out: decor, theme, and activities

If you are handling your own decor (home shower or rented space), now is when you shop and plan the table styling. The mistake is overplanning decor and underplanning logistics.

**What actually matters for photos:**
- A clean, styled gift table or gift area
- A table centerpiece that reads well in natural light (or whatever light your venue has)
- A backdrop for the bride to sit in front of during gift opening — not elaborate, just intentional

**What guests remember:**
- The food was good
- The conversation was fun
- The games were not painful
- The bride visibly had a great time

Decor that photographs beautifully but is generic does not make a shower memorable. Decor that reflects something specific about the bride does.

**On the question of a theme:** Themes work if they give the planning group a clear decision filter. "Garden party" answers: what goes on the table, what the invitation says, what the food is, what the dress code is. Themes break down when they become a creative constraint that stresses out the planners more than they delight the guests.

---

### Activities and games: what works and what does not

The games that consistently work at bridal showers share three things: they involve information the guests do not already know (which creates genuine surprise), they do not require anyone to perform for the group, and they can flex for a mixed-age guest list.

**Games that work:**

*How well do you know the couple?* Prepare 15 questions about the bride and groom — where they met, who said "I love you" first, where they went on their first date, the groom's opinion on pineapple on pizza. Get the answers from the groom in advance. Read the questions, guests write answers, reveal the right answers. The groom's answers almost always generate the most laughs.

*Wedding bingo.* Give every guest a bingo card with gift items (blender, bath towels, kitchen gadgets, lingerie, etc.) — guests mark off each item as the bride opens it. Whoever gets bingo gets a small prize. Keeps the less-engaged guests engaged during what can be a slow gift-opening stretch.

*Recipe cards.* Leave cards at each table setting asking guests to fill out a favorite recipe for the new couple. These get collected, and the bride ends up with a crowd-sourced recipe collection that is actually personal. No competitive element required.

*Dress the bride in toilet paper.* Works better than it sounds in the right group. Skip it with a mixed-age family-heavy guest list.

**Games that often flop:**

- Team competitions where someone loses visibly
- Anything that requires knowledge of wedding trivia rather than knowledge of the actual bride and groom
- Games that only work with alcohol (many guests are not drinking, and games that require it are awkward for them)

---

### Three weeks out: favors and final logistics

**Favors:** Optional, but if you are doing them, three weeks is the time to order. Custom items (personalized seeds, custom candles, monogrammed items) have production lead times. Generic favors (small candles, a packet of tea, a seed envelope) can be assembled the week of.

The best favors are either consumable (tea, candles, a small plant, a jar of jam) or genuinely useful. The worst favors are cheap plastic items that end up in the back of a junk drawer by the following Tuesday.

**Logistics checklist at three weeks:**
- Venue contract confirmed and deposit paid
- Headcount locked and communicated to venue
- Menu confirmed with venue or catering
- All contributors know their financial commitment
- Cake or dessert ordered (if separate from venue catering)
- Game materials prepared
- Seating chart drafted if formal seating is planned
- Transportation plan for the bride (who is picking her up, when)

---

### One week out: the coordination meeting

If you have co-hosts, have a thirty-minute check-in. Run through the day-of schedule:

- Who is arriving at the venue first and when?
- Who is bringing what (decor, game materials, cake)?
- Who is picking up the bride?
- What time does setup start?
- What is the plan for gift collection and transport at the end?

Day-of logistics always have gaps. The week-of meeting is where you find the gaps before they become problems.

## The shower day: a working schedule

This schedule assumes a noon shower start time. Adjust backward from your actual start time.

**9:00 AM:** Co-host arrival at venue. Setup begins. Table decor, gift table, signage, any A/V if needed.

**10:30 AM:** Food arrives or catering setup begins.

**11:30 AM:** Final walkthrough. Everything in place before guests start arriving.

**11:45 AM:** Pick up the bride (or confirm she is en route if traveling herself).

**12:00 PM:** Guests begin arriving. Mimosas or mocktails available. Ambient music playing.

**12:15–1:00 PM:** Open socializing, drinks, appetizers. Let guests mingle before directing anyone anywhere.

**1:00 PM:** Seated lunch or brunch service.

**1:45 PM:** Gift opening. Seat the bride at the designated gift area. Designate one person to hand gifts, one person to collect ribbons and cards, one person to write down what came from whom (for thank-you notes — this is essential). Take photos.

**2:30 PM:** Cake or dessert. Games if any.

**3:00 PM:** Guests begin departing. No formal end announcement needed — the energy naturally winds down after dessert.

**3:30 PM:** Tear down, gift loading, venue cleanup.

## The gift-tracking system: the thing everyone forgets until it is too late

The thank-you note situation lives or dies on what happens during gift opening. If no one is tracking which guest gave which gift, the bride is going to spend the following month trying to reconstruct it from Instagram and fragmented memory.

Assign one person — not the MOH, who should be sitting with the bride and managing the room — to do only one thing during gift opening: write down each gift and the name of the person who gave it, in order. A simple notebook works. A printed list of guest names works better (just check off and note the gift).

The bride also needs the envelopes from any cards — cards can get separated from gifts in the chaos of opening, and the envelope usually has the return address.

## Budget breakdown by shower type

**Home-hosted shower, 15–20 guests:**
| Line Item | Range |
|-----------|-------|
| Food (catered delivery or home cooking) | $150–350 |
| Beverages | $75–150 |
| Decor | $50–150 |
| Invitations | $30–80 |
| Cake or dessert | $50–100 |
| Games and prizes | $20–50 |
| Favors | $30–80 |
| **Total** | **$405–960** |

**Restaurant private room, 15–20 guests:**
| Line Item | Range |
|-----------|-------|
| Per-person food and drink minimum | $400–800 |
| Room fee (if separate) | $0–300 |
| Cake (bring-your-own or order from venue) | $50–150 |
| Invitations | $30–80 |
| Decor (often minimal at restaurant venues) | $20–75 |
| Favors | $30–80 |
| **Total** | **$530–1,485** |

**Venue rental, 25–40 guests with catering:**
| Line Item | Range |
|-----------|-------|
| Venue rental | $300–800 |
| Catering (per-person for full service) | $600–1,600 |
| Bar and beverages | $200–500 |
| Decor | $100–300 |
| Cake | $100–250 |
| Invitations | $50–120 |
| Favors | $50–150 |
| **Total** | **$1,400–3,720** |
