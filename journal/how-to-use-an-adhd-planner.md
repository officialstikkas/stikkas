# How to actually use an ADHD planner (without abandoning it in three days)

*Why most ADHD planners get abandoned by Wednesday — and the seven small rules that keep one alive.*

**By Oscar Arana · May 13, 2026 · 9 min read**

---

I design ADHD planners for a living, which means I spend a lot of my week reading messages from people who bought one and don't know why it isn't working.

Every May — Mental Health Awareness Month — those messages come in faster. Someone bought an ADHD planner. They were excited. They printed it, set it up on Sunday, used it Monday, used it Tuesday, half-used it Wednesday, forgot about it Thursday, and now it's the following Tuesday and they are emailing me asking if the planner is broken or they are.

Neither is broken. The thing that is broken is the way ADHD planners are sold. They are sold as a system you adopt. They should be sold as a tool you use, the same way a hammer is a tool. You don't adopt a hammer. You don't "stick with" a hammer. You pick it up when there's a nail.

Here are the seven rules I send back when those messages come in. They came out of three years of customer conversations, my own attempts to use the planners I'd designed, and a slow, embarrassing recognition that most of what gets sold as ADHD productivity advice is written for a brain that is not the brain it claims to serve.

## 1. Stop trying to plan the whole week on Sunday

This is the rule that breaks planners faster than anything else. Every ADHD planner you've seen has a beautiful weekly spread, and every YouTuber sets it up Sunday evening with a cup of tea, and the implicit promise is that planning ahead — really, properly, in detail — will save your week.

It will not save your week. Future-you is a stranger, and Sunday-you does not know what Wednesday-you is going to feel like. ADHD brains are particularly bad at forecasting energy. The seven appointments Sunday-you blocked out for Wednesday-you assumed Wednesday-you would have the same focus that Sunday-you had at 4 pm with a clean desk and an empty head. Wednesday-you does not. Wednesday-you slept badly and got distracted by a podcast and now has two hours to do six hours of work.

What works instead: **plan the next day, not the next week.** Open the planner the night before, or first thing in the morning. Write three things — not ten, not a checklist of fifteen, three — that need to actually happen that day. The rest of the week stays empty until its day shows up. The empty page is not a failure. It is the system working.

> A weekly spread filled in on Sunday is a fiction. A daily page filled in the night before is a plan.

If your planner doesn't have a daily page — only weekly spreads — you will need to add one. A blank index card slipped between the pages, or a sticky note stuck to today's date, both work fine. The format matters less than the principle: one page per day, written the day before, three things.

## 2. Three things. Not five. Not "and also."

The first rule says the system. This rule is the discipline that keeps the system honest.

When ADHD brains plan a day, they do one of two things. Either we plan zero things — because the planner is closed and we're winging it — or we plan seventeen things, because once we open the planner the entire backlog of unfinished items comes pouring onto the page. Both of these failures are the same failure. They are the failure of not deciding what actually matters.

So: three things. The exercise is choosing. The choice is the entire point. If you put four things on the list, you've cheated. If you put two things on the list, you've cheated more (you're hoping the third one shows up later — it won't, and you'll feel vague about the day). Three.

One of those three should be tiny — a thirty-second thing, like "text Dad back" — because crossing one off in the first hour gives the brain a hit that makes the next two feel reachable. ADHD planners that don't account for the dopamine economy of crossing things off are designed by people who do not have ADHD.

## 3. The planner does not need to be opened every day

This is the rule that saves planners from getting abandoned permanently after a missed week.

The neurotypical planner culture assumes you'll use it every day, and if you skip a week you've "fallen off" and need to "get back on." That framing is poison for ADHD brains, because we are going to skip weeks. We are going to forget the planner exists for fourteen days, find it on the kitchen counter, feel a wave of shame, and decide to start fresh next Monday — which is six days away, so we'll start in six days, except we won't.

The fix is to reframe what a missed week means. **A missed week is not a failure of the system. A missed week is information.** It tells you the week was busy enough or chaotic enough that you didn't need the planner. Great. Now you're back. Open it today, write three things for today, do not re-plan the missed week. The missed week is gone. The planner is not a debt.

The planners I see succeed long-term are the ones used four days a week on average, not seven. The seven-day-perfect planner is a planner that gets thrown out in March.

## 4. The brain dump is the real magic, and it doesn't belong on the weekly spread

Every ADHD planner worth buying has some version of a brain-dump page. It is the single most useful page in the entire book. It is also the page most people skip, because it isn't structured and it doesn't feel like "real" planning.

The brain dump is the page where you write down — fast, ugly, no order — every single thing rattling around in your head right now. Bills. Birthdays. A weird ache in your knee. The thing you should ask your boss. The thing you should say to your sister. The book you wanted to order. The lightbulb in the hallway.

You are not trying to solve any of these things on the brain dump page. You are getting them out of working memory and onto paper so working memory can stop tracking them. The reason ADHD brains feel exhausted is not because they're thinking about hard things. It's because they're holding ten medium things in active memory at the same time, all day, while also trying to focus. The brain dump is where you put them down.

> The exhaustion isn't from thinking hard. It's from holding ten medium things at once. Put them down.

Do this once a week. Don't try to do it daily — daily is too often, the brain dump turns into a chore. Sunday evening, or first thing Monday morning, twenty minutes, fast and ugly. Then walk away. The next day you'll find that three or four of the things on the brain dump page have already half-resolved themselves just by being written down, and the rest can be triaged into next-day plans.

## 5. Track one habit. Just one. For a season.

ADHD planners love a habit tracker. The grid with thirty boxes for thirty days, twelve habits down the side. It is one of the most aspirational pages in any planner. It is also where most ADHD planners go to die.

What happens is predictable. You set up twelve habits. You hit most of them on Monday. You hit eight on Tuesday. You hit four on Wednesday. You hit zero on Thursday because something happened. The empty boxes on Thursday make you not want to open the tracker on Friday. By the following Monday the grid is half-empty and the planner closes.

The fix: **one habit at a time. For a season. That's it.**

Pick one habit you actually want — not one you think you should want — and track only that, for the entire month. Twelve months a year, twelve habits. That's not slow, that's a year of changed behavior, which is more than most people accomplish trying to fix twelve at once. The trick is that twelve at once is zero, and one at a time is real.

If your planner has a multi-habit tracker built in, use one row. Leave the rest blank. The empty rows are not failure. They are the next eleven months.

## 6. Put the planner where you can't avoid it

This is a physical-world rule and it is more important than any of the page-design rules. The planner you put in a drawer is dead. The planner you leave on the kitchen counter, or the desk, or the nightstand — open, facedown does not count, *open* — is alive.

ADHD brains are object-permanence-poor in a specific way: if we don't see the thing, it does not exist. A closed planner in a drawer cannot remind you of itself. An open planner on the desk reminds you of itself every time you walk by. The reminder is the planner doing half its job. The other half is what you write in it.

This is also why I quietly disagree with most of the iPad-based ADHD planner culture. The iPad version is beautiful, and we sell one, and a lot of people prefer it. But the iPad gets closed. The iPad lives in the same physical space as Netflix and Twitter and email. A paper planner has no other apps. The only thing it competes with for attention is your own ability to walk past it.

If you must keep the planner closed (small house, kids, pets, aesthetic preferences), use a sticky note as a halfway measure. Tear off today's daily page, stick it to the fridge or the monitor or the bathroom mirror. The planner can live in a drawer if today's page lives in the open.

## 7. The planner is a draft, not a record

Last rule, and the one that takes most customers the longest to internalize.

People feel weird about scratching things out, writing in the margins, putting an X over an entire day's plan because the day got hijacked. The planner is supposed to be a clean record of what they did. So they avoid writing in it on bad days, because they don't want the bad days documented. Then they avoid opening it on the day after a bad day, because the gap is there. Then a week is gone.

The mindset that fixed this: **the planner is a draft of the week, not a record of the week.** It is allowed to be ugly. It is supposed to be ugly. The lines should be crossed out. The plans should be rewritten. A day with two things scratched out and one new thing written in pen is a day with three real attempts on it, which is better than a day with one thing perfectly written and then never touched.

The clean-planner aesthetic is for Instagram. The messy planner is for actually getting your week through your week.

## The shape of an ADHD planner that survives

If I had to compress the seven rules into a single sentence: *ADHD planners die when they ask too much of you on Sunday and forgive too little of you on Wednesday.*

The planner that survives in an ADHD life is the one with a daily page (not just a weekly), three slots on that page (not ten), a separate brain-dump page (used weekly, not daily), a habit tracker that's honest about tracking one thing at a time, and a layout that looks fine half-filled-in, because most pages will be half-filled-in.

That's the design brief I worked from when I built ours. It is not the planner most ADHD customers were sold by the bigger brands. It is the one their messages, taken together over three years, asked me to design.

If you've abandoned an ADHD planner before, the planner wasn't the problem. The system sold around it was. Three things. Daily page, not weekly. One habit. Keep it open. Let it be messy. Skip a week and come back. That's the whole thing.

Mental Health Awareness Month is the right month to put this in writing. The shape of executive function is not the shape most productivity tools assume. The tools that survive in an ADHD week are the ones designed by people who took that seriously.

A separate brain-dump page used weekly. And a habit tracker that is honest about tracking one habit at a time. Most ADHD planners sell beautiful weekly spreads and twelve-row habit grids, and that is exactly what kills them by Wednesday.

**What do I do if I have already abandoned a planner before — sometimes more than one?**
