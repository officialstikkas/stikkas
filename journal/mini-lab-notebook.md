# The mini-lab notebook: what 200 prints a day teaches you about color

*Notes from behind the counter at a working LA photo lab — and what photographers actually order.*

**By Oscar Arana · May 12, 2026 · 6 min read**

---

People romanticize the photo lab in a particular way. They picture a darkroom, a safelight, a clothesline of damp prints. They picture me — apparently — in a leather apron, hand-tinting tintypes between sips of espresso. I am very flattered by this. It is not exactly how the day goes.

Most days at the lab are a Frontier printer humming through 4×6 vacation envelopes, a Noritsu film scanner pulling rolls of Portra and Tri-X off a wedding photographer who needs them by Thursday, and a 44-inch Epson laying down a museum-grade exhibition print for an artist who has been emailing me color reference shots for a week. There is a clothesline of damp prints, technically. It is in the back. It is short.

What I want to do here is share some notes from the counter. Not technical notes — there are better blogs for that. Notes on what the work actually looks like, what photographers actually ask for, and the handful of small things that I learned by doing the same job two hundred times a day for years on end. Those notes are the reason I trust myself when I design a printable for Stikkas. The eye is built at the counter.

## What people actually print

Some breakdown of what crosses the counter in a typical week, because the answer surprised me when I first started keeping count:

- **Wedding photographers, in 16×24 and 20×30,** on lustre paper. Not the bride. The photographer, for their portfolio book and studio walls. This is the order that has to be perfect because they're showing it to clients.
- **Real estate photographers, in 8×10,** for staging books. Velvet matte paper, neutral white balance, slight saturation boost. These prints live in agent binders and need to read clean under ugly fluorescent.
- **Working film shooters,** dropping off rolls of 35mm and 120 medium-format. The 120 shooters are 90% wedding and editorial. The 35mm shooters are 70% personal, 30% small commercial.
- **Families,** reprinting grandma's slide carousel into 4×6 envelopes for a memorial. This is the order that makes you slow down.
- **Visual artists,** needing exhibition prints on cotton rag. Two or three a month. Each one a careful conversation.
- **And the rest:** passport photos, school yearbook reprints, the occasional iPhone screenshot somebody really, really wants on paper.

The mix is wider than you'd guess and the standard is higher than you'd guess. People walking into a small lab in 2026 are doing it on purpose. They drove past three CVS photo counters to get here. The bar is "noticeably better than CVS or it doesn't exist."

## The thing 200 prints a day teaches you

You learn, very fast, that color is not a fact. It is a negotiation.

A photograph that looks "neutral" on a calibrated monitor under a north-facing skylight will look slightly warm on the same monitor at 9 p.m. with the desk lamp on. The same photograph printed on lustre and on matte paper will look like two different shots. The same matte print under a living-room warm bulb will look different than under a kitchen LED. You are not printing "a color." You are printing a color, under a specific light, on a specific paper, viewed by a specific eye.

> The job of a colorist isn't to find the "right" color. It's to find the color that survives the journey.

Photographers learn this early. Designers learn it late. Designers who came up on screens — and most of us did — assume the file *is* the image. At the lab you find out the file is the score and the print is the performance. Every paper, every printer profile, every viewer is the orchestra deciding what the score sounds like in this room.

What you take away from doing this two hundred times a day: a quiet skepticism about your own monitor, and a real respect for the people who print for a living.

## What I bring home to my designs

This is the part that connects to why I run Stikkas the way I do.

When I design a wedding planner or a printable journal, I am not designing for a Retina display. I am designing for the moment that file lands on a sheet of inkjet paper in a stranger's kitchen, at 10 p.m., under a yellow lamp, after a long day. The reader is tired, the printer is okay, the paper is whatever was on sale at Target. I have to make sure the page still feels generous and the typography still feels deliberate *at that endpoint.*

Things this changes in practice:

- **I never use a pure white background.** Pure white at the file level becomes "absence of ink" at the print level, and pure absence-of-ink reveals every flaw in the paper. I use a 2–4% warm tint. It prints as a barely-perceptible cream that feels intentional instead of harsh.
- **I never use a pure black headline.** Pure black on a home inkjet over-saturates and the headline bleeds at the edges. I use a 92% ink mix with a warm shift. It prints crisp.
- **I test every accent color in three places:** the screen, a 4×6 sample print on lustre, and a 4×6 sample print on plain inkjet. If the color survives the cheapest of the three, it ships.
- **I design every spread with the margin of a printable in mind.** The reader will print at 100%, no scaling. The page has to look balanced inside that constraint, not designed for full-bleed.
- **I check thumbnails in greyscale,** because some buyers will print on a black-and-white office laser when they're in a rush. If the design is illegible in greyscale, the color was carrying the whole composition. That's bad design.

None of this is fancy. It is just what happens when the person designing the file is also the person who has watched ten thousand pieces of paper come out of a printer.

## What this means for what we sell

The reason I started designing printables in the first place was a small, slightly grumpy observation: the printable planner market on Etsy is enormous, and almost none of it is made by people who have ever worked in a print shop. You can see it on the page. Fonts that don't survive print, color palettes that turn to mud, layouts designed for a phone screenshot of the cover but not for a sheet of paper.

That gap is the entire Stikkas thesis. Make digital things — planners, wedding kits, coloring books — that are designed by someone who actually understands the medium they end up in.

If that is your standard too, this is the right shop to follow. We're shipping new planners and zines every few weeks. Most of them get tested on a Frontier and an Epson before they ever hit the listing.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the biggest thing photographers learn at a photo lab that the rest of us miss?**

That color is not a fact — it is a negotiation. The same photograph looks different on a calibrated monitor in the morning, on the same monitor at 9 p.m. under a desk lamp, printed on lustre paper, printed on matte, under a kitchen LED, and under a warm living-room bulb. The file is the score; the print is the performance. The job is not finding the right color, it is finding the color that survives the journey.

**Why does printing the same image on different papers change it so much?**

Because paper is part of the optical system, not just the substrate. Lustre paper has a slight sheen that lifts highlights and deepens blacks; matte paper absorbs light evenly and flattens the same image into a softer reading. Cotton rag for fine-art prints sits between the two and adds a tactile warmth that screens cannot show. Choosing the paper is half of choosing the print.

**What do you actually print on a Frontier versus an Epson at the lab?**

The Frontier handles the volume — 4x6 envelopes, school portrait reprints, family albums, anything where consistency at scale matters. The 44-inch Epson handles the exhibition prints — fine-art reproductions on cotton rag, large-format gallery work, anything where one print needs to be perfect. Two different machines for two different jobs; mistaking one for the other is the most common cause of disappointment with a print order.

**Why do you design printables for home printers instead of pro labs?**

Because that is where they actually land. Most printable buyers print at home, at 10 p.m., on whatever paper was on sale at Target, on an inkjet that has not been calibrated since it left the box. If the file only looks good on a calibrated monitor, the file fails. So I never use pure white backgrounds (they print as unflattering absence of ink), never pure black headlines (they over-saturate and bleed), and I test every accent color on a cheap inkjet before I ship.

**Should I print my own photos at home or take them to a lab?**

Anything 5x7 and smaller, on photo paper, your home printer does fine. Anything 8x10 and larger, anything for framing, anything you want to last more than five years — bring it to a real lab. The difference is not the resolution; it is the ink chemistry. Lab prints use pigment-based or chromogenic processes that are stable for decades. Home inkjet prints fade noticeably in a sunlit room within two or three years.

**What is the most overlooked thing about making a print look "professional"?**

Paper weight. The single biggest factor in whether something feels like a real print versus a worksheet is the thickness of the paper, not the design on it. Move from 20lb copy paper to 65lb to 110lb cardstock and the same file becomes a different object. Most home printers handle 65lb without complaint; many handle 110lb if you feed the sheets one at a time.
