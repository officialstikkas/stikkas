# How to print a wedding planner that actually looks good at home

*Paper weight, color profiles, and the three printer settings that quietly ruin the page.*

**By Oscar Arana · May 12, 2026 · 8 min read**

---

Every week a customer messages me on Etsy with the same photo: a printable planner page, printed at home, with the muted sage band on the top of the page now looking like a sad pistachio yogurt, the typography slightly fuzzy, and the page curling like a salted chip. They paid eight dollars for a beautiful PDF and they're staring at something that looks worse than the printer manual.

It is almost never the file. I should know — half the files in those messages are mine. The fix is in five small decisions you make before you press print.

I run a mini-lab here in Los Angeles. I develop film, calibrate large-format inkjet, and print photographs every day for working photographers. Then I go home and design printable planners with the same fussiness. The bridge between the two is shorter than it looks: home printing isn't a worse version of pro printing. It is its own medium, with its own rules, and you can get it gorgeous if you respect the rules.

## 1. The paper does most of the work

This is the part nobody wants to hear, because paper is the boring part of the shopping list and the part with the highest dollar cost. But it is also the part that decides whether your printed page reads as "DIY craft fair" or "actual stationery."

For a wedding planner you'll touch and write in for nine months, the sweet spot is **32 lb / 120 gsm bright white** inkjet paper. Standard copy paper is 20 lb / 75 gsm and it is half the weight. You can feel the difference with your eyes closed. 32 lb paper holds ink without bleeding through, takes a fountain pen without ghosting, and folds without instantly creasing.

What to ask for at the store: "Hammermill Premium 32 lb" or "HP Premium Inkjet 32 lb." Both run around $14 for 250 sheets, which is roughly five and a half cents a page. Your wedding planner is 120 pages. That is your paper cost.

If you want the page to feel like a hotel notebook, step up to **presentation paper, 28 lb matte coated**. The matte coating is the difference. It holds color and stops the printer head from over-soaking the fibers, which is the single largest cause of "my sage band looks like yogurt."

> The number-one cause of home-printed planners looking bad is using paper that drinks ink faster than the printer can lay it down.

What to avoid: cardstock heavier than 80 lb (it's too stiff to bind cleanly in a planner cover and home inkjets struggle to feed it), and anything labeled "multipurpose." Multipurpose is code for "lowest spec we could legally call paper."

## 2. The three printer settings nobody changes

Most home printers ship with defaults tuned for a tax return. The defaults are not tuned for a fifty-page editorial PDF. There are three settings, all hidden inside the print dialog, that change the entire result.

### Setting one: media type

This is the most important setting on the entire printer. The print driver uses "media type" to decide *how much ink to lay down and how fast to move the print head.* If you tell it "Plain Paper" and you've loaded matte presentation paper, the printer over-saturates the page and the muted sage band you spent two hours color-grading turns into a flat pistachio puddle.

The fix: open your print dialog, click whatever button reveals the advanced settings (it's almost always called "Show Details" or "Properties"), and find the field that says Media Type or Paper Type. Switch it to **Matte Photo Paper** or **Presentation Paper Matte**, even if you loaded plain Hammermill. The printer will lay down less ink and your colors will land where the designer placed them.

This single setting change is the difference between a wedding planner that looks store-bought and one that looks photocopied.

### Setting two: print quality

"Draft" is the default on a lot of printers because manufacturers want to sell you ink cartridges, and the easiest way to make you re-print is to make the first print look slightly soft. Switch print quality to **High** or **Best**. Yes, it doubles the time per page. A 120-page wedding planner now takes about forty minutes instead of twenty. That is forty minutes of free upgrade.

### Setting three: scale to fit / borderless

Turn both of these off. Every well-designed printable planner has been laid out with a deliberate margin — usually fifteen millimeters — to give the page room to breathe and to leave space for binding. "Scale to Fit" will compress the page by a few percent to force-fit it inside the printable area, which throws every grid alignment and every photograph slightly out of register. "Borderless" will over-print the page edges and waste ink.

The correct setting is almost always **Actual Size / 100% / No Scaling**. The file was designed for letter size. Print it at letter size.

## 3. Color profiles, simplified

Color profiles are the single thing that print professionals talk about most and home printers handle worst. The short version: every device — your screen, your printer, the PDF — speaks a slightly different dialect of color. The profile is the translator.

For home inkjet printing of a printable PDF, you only need to know two things.

First, the PDF you bought is almost certainly in **sRGB**. That is the standard color space for digital things you view on a screen. It is what every well-built printable planner ships in, including ours.

Second, your home printer almost certainly has a setting buried somewhere called "Color Management" or "Printer Color." Set it to **Printer Manages Colors** or **Application Manages Colors → sRGB**. Do not pick "Vivid," do not pick "Photo Enhancement," do not pick any of the AI-named modes added in the last three firmware updates. Those modes are tuned for vacation snapshots, not for a sage-and-cream typographic spread.

If your sage band still prints flat after this, the answer is not a different color profile — it's a different paper.

> Color is the cheapest thing to get right. Buy the right paper and tell the driver the truth about it.

## 4. Binding: the difference between a project and a planner

The final step is the one most people skip and the one that most defines whether the planner becomes part of your week or part of your recycling.

The three good options, in order of cost:

- **Three-hole punch + a half-inch D-ring binder.** Six dollars at any office store. The binder protects the pages, lays flat, and lets you add tabs. Boring, but effective. This is what we recommend for wedding planners — you'll be adding RSVP cards, vendor quotes, and seating diagrams for nine months, and you need something that opens.
- **Spiral / coil binding, done at a copy shop.** About fifteen dollars for a 120-page document at any FedEx Office or Staples copy counter. They'll trim, punch, and coil it while you wait. The planner now feels like a real notebook and lies completely flat on a table. Worth every penny if you'll write in it daily.
- **Saddle stitch or perfect binding** at a real print shop. Forty to sixty dollars. Only worth it if you intend to keep the planner as a keepsake after the wedding. Most clients don't, and that's fine — the planner is a working tool, not a yearbook.

## 5. The one trick we use in the lab

This is the trick I share with every customer who emails me about a planner that didn't print right.

Before you print all 120 pages, print exactly one page. Pick the page with the most color in it — usually the cover, or a section divider with a colored band across the top. Look at it under the lamp where you'll actually use the planner. Not under the harsh white light of your office at midnight. The light where the planner will live.

If the band looks washed out, the answer is almost always paper or media-type setting. If the typography looks fuzzy, switch to High quality. If the colors look right but the page edge is wrong, you left Scale-to-Fit on.

One page. Three minutes. Then you commit the other 119.

This is the part of the wedding planner conversation that the printable-PDF world mostly ignores: you bought a beautiful file and we are pretending the file is the product. The file is half the product. The paper, the printer, and the five minutes you spend on the print dialog are the other half. Honor that half and the planner you bring to the table on your wedding day will look like something you'd be proud to hand to a vendor.

That is the standard we try to design to. It is the standard we encourage you to print to.
