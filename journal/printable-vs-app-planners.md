# Printable planners vs. apps: an honest take from someone who designs both

*When paper wins, when the app wins, and why the answer is usually somewhere in between.*

**By Oscar Arana · May 12, 2026 · 7 min read**

---

I design printable planners for a living. I also design GoodNotes planners — the same content, rebuilt with hyperlinked tabs, for people who plan on an iPad. So my opinion here is biased twice over. I make both. I love both. I sell both. Anything I tell you should be read with that disclosure.

And yet: the question of "should I plan on paper or on a screen" gets answered very badly online. The honest answer isn't "paper is better" or "apps are better." The honest answer is "they do different things, and most people only need one of them, and most reviews online are written by people trying to sell you the other one."

Here's what I tell customers when they email me asking which version of a planner to buy.

## When the printable wins

The printable planner wins anytime *writing it down* is the entire point.

The research on this is unfair to apps. Writing by hand activates more cortical regions than typing. It is slower, which forces you to compress what you're thinking into fewer words. It is physical, which makes it harder to dismiss. The act of putting a pen on a goal page once a week is a different cognitive event than tapping the same words into a notes app. The notes app feels reversible. The paper does not.

So: anywhere planning is also reflection, paper wins. Gratitude journals. Goal-setting kits. Mental health and wellness check-ins. Wedding planning, especially the early-decision phase when you're still figuring out what kind of wedding you actually want. ADHD planners that depend on a daily ritual surviving a chaotic week — paper wins because paper sits open on the desk and refuses to be ignored.

> If the planner is asking you to think, use paper. If the planner is asking you to remember, use the app.

Paper also wins for one practical reason that nobody talks about: it never updates against you. The planner you used in 2018 is the same planner today. The app you used in 2018 has been rebuilt three times, lost half its features, picked up a subscription tier, and now wants to upsell you on AI integration. The printable is a finished object. That stability is part of what you're buying.

## When the app wins

The app planner wins anywhere *looking it up* is the entire point.

Search is the apps' superpower. A printed planner can hold an entire wedding's worth of vendor contacts, but if you can't remember whether the florist's number is on the August or September spread, paper turns hostile fast. The app finds it in three seconds.

Recurrence is another app-only feature. A weekly habit, a monthly bill, a yearly anniversary — none of these belong on paper. You'll forget to write them in next year. The app remembers.

And portability matters more than people admit. If your planner has to be with you all the time — at meetings, at the gym, in the car, in line — then it has to fit in your phone. A planner that lives in a binder on your desk is a planner you'll consult at home, twice a week. That works for some plans and not others.

App-first use cases that I personally would not fight: budgeting and finance (you need running totals, math, history), meal planning (you need a grocery list that updates when you swap a recipe), and any habit-tracking that depends on reminders you'd otherwise miss.

## The case for both, which is what most people actually need

Here is the part the planner-industrial complex doesn't tell you: most working adults use both, on purpose, for different layers of the same year.

The most common setup I see in the messages customers send me:

- **App planner** for the daily / calendar / appointment / reminder layer. Whatever they were already using — Apple Calendar, Notion, Google Calendar, Things. This layer is the "where do I need to be in the next 48 hours" layer.
- **Printed planner** for the weekly / monthly / quarterly reflection layer. Open it Sunday evening, write the week ahead. Open it again Friday to see how it went. This layer is the "who am I trying to be this season" layer.

Those are two genuinely different jobs and they don't compete with each other. The app is the front of the house, dealing with traffic. The printable is the back room, doing the slower work. You don't need one of them to die for the other to work.

> The app is the front of the house. The paper is the back room. Both can be busy.

## What this means for how we ship products

I learned all of this the hard way, by getting customer emails. The first three years I designed planners, I shipped them as PDFs only. Half the customers wanted the iPad version. So I started shipping GoodNotes versions too. Now half the customers want the printable. So every planner we sell ships with both.

The truth is the medium is not the differentiator. The design is. A planner with a thoughtful weekly spread is useful on paper, on an iPad, and on the back of a napkin. A planner with a sloppy weekly spread is useless in all three formats. The argument about printable-versus-app is almost always a distraction from the real question, which is: *is the planner I'm about to buy actually well-designed.*

So here's the more useful set of questions when you're picking a planner — printable or app:

- **Is the weekly spread one page or two?** One-page weekly is more honest. Two-page weekly is usually padded.
- **Does the layout fight you on a busy week?** Some planners give you ten little boxes per day. If your day isn't ten little boxes, that planner doesn't fit your life.
- **Is there room to *write a sentence*, not just a word?** Good planners reserve a few unstructured lines per day for sentences.
- **What happens in the second half of the year, when you stop using it?** Every planner gets abandoned eventually. The good ones survive a two-month break and let you pick them back up. The bad ones make you feel guilty about the gap.
- **Does the designer use it themselves?** Honestly. You can tell. The week-spread layouts designed by people who actually plan their week look different from the layouts designed by people who just thought "weekly spread" sounded right.

## What I'd recommend if you're stuck

If you're choosing for the first time, my actual advice:

If your week is mostly meetings, deadlines, and reminders — start with the app you already use, and skip buying a planner altogether. Add a printed weekly-reflection page. One page. Once a week. That's enough.

If your week is mostly choices — about how you spend your time, what you eat, what you read, who you reach out to — start with paper. The friction is the feature. A printable planner forces you to slow down enough to make the choices on purpose, instead of letting the app's notification queue make them for you.

And if you're somewhere in between — most of us are — buy a printable that ships with a digital companion. Use the digital on the go, write in the printable on Sundays. That's the workflow most of our customers have settled into. We design our planners specifically for that hybrid use.

---

So: not "paper is better." Not "apps are better." Just the older, less-shareable answer that paper and apps do different things and that the goal of a planner — any planner — is to make you slightly more deliberate about your week than you'd otherwise be. Pick whichever format makes you most likely to actually open it on Sunday evening. The rest is design.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are printable planners better than planner apps?**

Neither is universally better — they do different jobs. Paper wins anywhere writing is the point: gratitude journals, goal-setting kits, mental-health check-ins, ADHD planners. Writing by hand activates more cortical regions than typing and forces you to compress what you are thinking into fewer words. Apps win anywhere looking it up is the point: search, recurrence, portability, running totals. If the planner is asking you to think, use paper. If the planner is asking you to remember, use the app.

**Should I use a paper planner or an iPad planner?**

If your week is mostly meetings, deadlines, and reminders, start with the app you already use — Apple Calendar, Google Calendar, Notion — and add a single printed weekly-reflection page once a week. If your week is mostly choices (how you spend time, what you eat, who you reach out to), start with paper. The friction is the feature; paper forces you to slow down enough to make the choices on purpose.

**Can I use both a printable planner and a planner app together?**

Yes, and most working adults do. The most common setup I see is an app planner for the daily appointment-and-reminder layer (the "where do I need to be in the next 48 hours" layer) and a printed planner for the weekly and quarterly reflection layer (the "who am I trying to be this season" layer). The app is the front of the house dealing with traffic. The paper is the back room doing slower work. They do not compete.

**Will an iPad planner like GoodNotes feel as good as paper?**

Different, not equivalent. GoodNotes on a recent iPad with a Pencil gets close to paper for the act of handwriting, and adds searchable text, hyperlinked tabs, and zero ink cost. What you lose is the open-on-the-desk reminder — the iPad has Netflix, Twitter, and email living in the same physical space, and it gets closed. Paper has nothing else on it; the iPad has the entire internet. Choose based on whether you need search and portability more than you need physical persistence.
