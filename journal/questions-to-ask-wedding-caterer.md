# 12 questions to ask your wedding caterer before you sign

*A 25-year wedding DJ on what catering choices make the reception flow versus what tanks it. Stations vs. plated, vendor meal timing, beverage service style, the dessert window, and the catering decisions that quietly decide whether the dance floor lands.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 7, 2026 · 11 min read**

---

I have DJ'd somewhere north of five hundred weddings since 2001. The DJ does not eat the wedding's actual dinner. The DJ eats the vendor meal forty-five minutes before service starts, then stands behind the booth for the next ninety minutes watching the room.

That perspective is what most couples never get. The bride and groom eat their dinner in twelve minutes because they are pulled away for toasts and photographs and a quick visit to every table. The planner eats while standing in the kitchen hallway. The guests see their own plate and the conversation at their table. The DJ sees the entire dinner service from the back of the room — the kitchen-door cadence, the server count, the dish-drop timing, the table that is still waiting for entrées while the next table is being cleared for dessert.

That perspective changes which questions matter to ask a wedding caterer.

A couple interviewing a caterer asks about the menu and the dietary restrictions and the price per head. The caterer gives the tasting and the catalog and the contract. Both parties are honest. The questions that decide whether the reception's dinner block lands or crashes mostly do not get asked, because they are not on the catering blog or the tasting brochure.

Here are twelve that do. Print this list. Bring it to the meeting. Get the answers in writing.

## 1. What is the actual walk-out per-head cost, including tax, service charge, and all add-ons?

The per-person price on the catering brochure is not the price you pay.

The price you pay is the per-person price plus tax (10-12% in most US markets), plus service charge (18-22%), plus the line items that get added during the contract: upgraded china, dietary-restriction meal upcharges, late-night snack station setup, staff overtime, vendor meals, cake-cutting service, tasting fees, beverage corkage, and any vendor that bundles with the venue at a premium.

The walk-out per-head cost is typically 28-32% above the quoted per-person rate.

Ask: *if I have 120 guests, what is the actual total catering cost I will pay, including all taxes, service charges, and standard add-ons?* A great caterer gives you the number on the call. A weak caterer says "let me put together a custom estimate" and then sends you a sales pitch.

You cannot budget the wedding off the per-person rate. You can budget off the walk-out number. Get the walk-out number before you sign anything. The full version of the wedding budget math is at [The Stikkas wedding budget guide](/stikkas/wedding-budget/).

## 2. How long does your service style actually take, minute by minute?

This is the question that determines the entire reception timeline, and almost nobody asks it.

The four common wedding-catering service styles each have a different real-world duration:

- **Plated, three-course service for 120 guests:** sixty to seventy-five minutes from the first salad drop to the last entrée plate cleared. Adds another fifteen to twenty minutes if dessert is plated rather than passed.
- **Buffet service for 120 guests:** forty to forty-five minutes for one buffet line, twenty-five to thirty minutes for two buffet lines on opposite sides of the room. The line bottleneck is real and the DJ has to fill the music gap.
- **Family-style service for 120 guests:** fifty to sixty minutes. Per-table dish drops are simultaneous, so the start is faster, but the table-to-table pacing is uneven.
- **Stations for 120 guests:** practically forty to fifty minutes for the first wave; the long tail can run two hours because guests keep returning. The dance floor competes with the stations the entire night.

Ask the caterer: *for my guest count, minute by minute, walk me through your service style.* A great answer is specific: "salads land at minute 0-12, entrées at minute 35-50, dessert clearing at minute 75." A weak answer is "we work fast." Pick the service style that matches the reception's energy goal — dance-floor-heavy weddings should consider buffet or stations to push the dinner block shorter; elegant evening weddings should consider plated.

The choice ripples through everything: the DJ's first-dance cue, the toast block, the cake-cut window, the dance-floor opening. The full version of how this fits in the reception timeline is at [Wedding Reception Timeline](/stikkas/journal/wedding-reception-timeline/).

## 3. Do you provide vendor meals, and what time are they served?

A working DJ cannot eat the wedding's actual dinner. Same for the photographer, the videographer, the on-site coordinator, the band, and the venue staff. We are on the floor during dinner running the announcements, working the photos, coordinating with the kitchen.

A great wedding caterer includes vendor meals as a $25-$40 per-head line item, served in a separate vendor-meal room thirty to forty-five minutes before the main dinner service. The vendor meal is usually a simplified version of the wedding entrée — a chicken plate instead of a beef tenderloin, a vegetable medley instead of a custom side.

The timing matters. A DJ who eats the vendor meal at 5:30 p.m. is fresh through the 9 p.m. dance-floor energy peak. A DJ who is told to eat after the toasts at 8:30 p.m. is fading through the dance floor. The vendor meal at the right time pays the catering forward in the form of vendor energy for the night.

Ask: *do you include vendor meals, what is the per-head charge, and what time do you serve them?* A caterer who charges full price per vendor meal or skips vendor meals altogether is a caterer who has not worked enough weddings to know why this matters.

## 4. How does the caterer coordinate with the DJ for the cake cut, toasts, and dinner courses?

This is the cousin question to the timeline question, and it tells you everything about whether the caterer plays well with the rest of the vendor team.

A great wedding caterer asks the DJ for the timeline before service starts and works the dish drops around the announcement cues. Entrées are not landing when the best man is mid-toast. The cake cut is coordinated so the dessert plating begins as the cake is being cut, not before. The bread basket is on the table before the salad lands, not after. The water glasses are refilled before the toast block, not during.

A weak caterer drops dishes whenever the kitchen finishes a tray. The DJ has to time the toasts around the kitchen instead of the kitchen timing around the toasts.

Ask explicitly: *how do you coordinate with the DJ and the planner during service?* A great answer is "we walk the kitchen-to-floor cadence with the DJ and the planner before service starts and check in between courses." A bad answer is "we just serve when the food is ready." From the DJ booth, you can hear the difference inside the first ten minutes of dinner.

## 5. What is the catering staff-to-guest ratio?

The per-head price hides the staff count. The staff count decides the service speed.

For plated service, the working ratio is one server per 12-15 guests. For buffet, one staff member per 25-30 guests is enough. For stations or family-style, one server per 15-20 guests.

A caterer who quotes one server per 25 guests for a plated reception is under-staffed for that service style. The entrée course will run thirty minutes long, the timeline will compress, the first dance will push, and the dance floor will start late.

Ask: *how many service staff will be on-site during dinner, and how does that map to a per-table or per-guest ratio?* If the answer is general, the staffing will be general. A great caterer names the number and tells you which staff member is doing what role.

## 6. How does beverage service work, and what is the bar policy?

Beverage service splits the dinner-and-dance economy in two — and the split affects the dance floor more than couples realize.

The four common configurations are:

- **Open bar, catering-provided beverages:** the caterer manages the bar, the bar runs throughout the reception, the per-head price is highest. Simplest to coordinate.
- **Open bar, couple-provided alcohol (corkage):** the catering staffs the bar, the couple supplies the alcohol, the caterer charges $15-$25 per bottle in corkage. Lower total cost, more couple coordination.
- **Cash bar:** guests pay individually. Less common at weddings, often a complaint magnet.
- **Beer-and-wine open bar with a signature cocktail:** middle option. Limits cost while keeping the bar open. Working compromise for budget-conscious weddings.

Ask: *which configurations do you offer, what is the per-head difference, and what time does the bar close?* The bar close should match the contracted end time, not the kitchen close. A bar that closes at 9:30 p.m. while the reception runs until 11 p.m. is a dead dance floor for the last ninety minutes.

The bar policy interacts directly with the DJ's energy peak. The full version is at the [Wedding Reception Timeline](/stikkas/journal/wedding-reception-timeline/).

## 7. Do you have a working plan for dietary restrictions?

A working wedding caterer asks for dietary restrictions thirty days before the wedding, organizes the response by table, communicates to the kitchen with a written allergen sheet, and trains the service staff on which guest at which table gets the modified plate.

A weak caterer says "send us the list and we'll handle it" and then the guest with the gluten allergy gets the salad with croutons.

Ask: *what is your process for dietary restrictions — gluten-free, dairy-free, nut allergies, vegetarian, vegan, kosher, halal — and how is each modified plate identified at the table?* A great caterer answers with a process. A weak caterer answers with reassurance.

This is the catering question with the largest downside if the answer is wrong: a guest with a real allergy who gets served the wrong plate is a 911 call.

## 8. What time is the dessert window?

This sounds petty. It is one of the most underrated catering questions.

Dessert that lands at 9 p.m. on a reception that started at 5 p.m. is dessert that competes with the dance floor's energy peak. Dessert that lands at 8 p.m. is dessert that ends in time for the dance floor to fully activate. Dessert that lands during the cake cut announcement is dessert that confuses the DJ's announcement timing.

A great caterer plates dessert as the cake is cutting and serves it during the cake-cut song, then the dance floor opens. A weak caterer waits until the cake-cut is over, plates dessert after, and serves it during what should have been the first dance-floor song.

The dessert window is forty-five to sixty minutes. Knowing when it lands is the difference between the dance floor opening on time and opening twenty-five minutes late.

Ask: *what time does dessert begin, what time is it cleared, and how does the dessert timing coordinate with the cake cut and the dance-floor opening?*

## 9. Who handles the late-night snack?

Many wedding receptions add a late-night snack station — slider bar, taco bar, pizza, mini grilled cheese, doughnuts. It is the part of the wedding everyone remembers and the catering line item couples often skip on the first pass.

Ask: *does the caterer offer a late-night snack option, what time does it open, what are the per-head price ranges, and is it staffed or self-serve?*

A great late-night snack opens at the dance floor's energy peak (9:30 to 10 p.m. for a typical reception), runs unstaffed for the dance hours, and closes at last call. A great late-night snack also gives the DJ a re-energizing window — the guests refuel on sliders and then come back to the dance floor for the last block.

A weak late-night snack opens at 11:15 p.m. for a reception ending at 11:30 p.m., which means the food goes uneaten and the per-head cost is wasted.

## 10. What happens to the leftovers?

This is the question that surfaces an important contract clause.

Many wedding caterers will not allow leftovers to leave the venue, citing food-safety liability. Others will plate leftovers for the couple to take home. Others will donate leftovers to a local shelter through a wedding-partner network.

Ask: *what is the leftover policy, and can the couple bring containers for the bridal party to take food home?* The answer affects post-wedding logistics — the bride's mother is not eating cold tenderloin at midnight, but if there is enough food left for ten meals, a clear policy is worth $300 in next-week groceries.

## 11. What is the cancellation and force-majeure policy?

Read the contract.

A strong catering contract refunds 90%+ of the deposit if the wedding is cancelled twelve months out, 70% at six months, 50% at three months, and forfeits the deposit inside thirty days. A force-majeure clause should cover natural disasters, governmental orders, and pandemic-era complications.

A weak contract forfeits the entire deposit on cancellation regardless of timing or cause.

The 2020-2021 wedding industry learned this clause the hard way. Read it carefully before signing. The catering deposit is usually 30-50% of the total catering cost — the largest single deposit at the wedding. Treat the cancellation clause with the same care you would treat a real-estate deposit.

## 12. Will you put all of this in the contract?

The twelfth question closes the other eleven.

Every promise the caterer makes in the tasting — the walk-out per-head price, the service style timeline, the vendor meal arrangement, the staff-to-guest ratio, the dietary-restriction process, the dessert window, the leftover policy, the cancellation terms — should be in the contract before you sign.

A great caterer says yes immediately. A caterer who hedges on "we always work with couples on the details" is telling you the details will be re-negotiated at the time the bill arrives.

Get every answer in writing. The catering contract is the second-largest contract of the wedding after the venue. It deserves the same rigor.

## What the answers actually mean

Wedding caterers fall into three categories, and the twenty-minute tasting will sort them.

**The caterer who answers in specifics is the caterer to seriously consider.** "For 120 guests, plated service runs 75 minutes. Vendor meals are $30 per head, served at 5:15 p.m. Walk-out cost for your guest count is $147 per head all-in. We coordinate with the DJ on toast-to-entrée timing." Specifics mean the caterer has rehearsed the answers because they have actually worked the questions.

**The caterer who answers in generalities is the caterer to keep interviewing.** "We're flexible." "Our staff is highly trained." "We work with great DJs." Generalities mean the caterer is selling a brochure they have not internalized as a service.

**The caterer who is annoyed by the questions is the caterer to walk away from.** The twelve questions above are the catering's value proposition. A caterer who is impatient with them is a caterer who will be impatient at 8 p.m. on the wedding night when the kitchen is behind schedule.

The catering is the second-most-expensive line item at most weddings, behind the venue. It also affects every other vendor's timeline. The twelve questions are how you find the caterer that serves the night instead of running it.
