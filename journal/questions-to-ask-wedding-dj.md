# 12 questions to ask a wedding DJ before you book

*A 25-year wedding DJ on the questions that separate a great reception from a flat one. What to ask before you sign the contract, and what the answers actually mean.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 3, 2026 · 10 min read**

---

I have been the DJ at somewhere north of five hundred weddings since 2001. I have worked with planners who run the night like a Swiss watch, and I have worked with couples who picked their DJ off Instagram on a Tuesday because the price was right. There is a pattern in which receptions land and which ones sit politely at their tables waiting for the night to be over. The pattern starts at the meeting before the contract is signed.

Most couples interview a DJ the way they interview a caterer — they ask about price, they ask if the date is available, they look at the package list, and they pick. They do not ask the twelve questions that actually determine whether their reception will be the kind people are still talking about a year later. Those twelve questions are this article.

If you are about to book a wedding DJ, print this list. Bring it to the call. The answers will tell you more than any sample mix on the website.

## 1. Will you be the DJ at my wedding, or will you send someone else?

This is the most important question and almost no one asks it.

Many wedding DJ companies are run as agencies. The person you meet on the call books the date and hands the actual gig to a contractor — sometimes a great one, sometimes a kid two years out of community college doing his fourteenth wedding. If the answer is anything other than a clear yes, ask: *who is the actual DJ, can I meet them, and can I see their wedding reel?*

A great agency will tell you straight: "I'm the agency owner, but the DJ at your wedding will be Maria — here's her reel, here's her bio, here are her three most recent reviews." That is a fine answer. A bad answer is "we'll assign someone closer to the date." That means you do not know who is showing up at your wedding until two weeks before the wedding.

The person at the console is the wedding. Know who that person is.

## 2. How many weddings have you personally done?

Notice the word *personally*. The company may have done 800 weddings. The DJ who is going to show up at yours may have done 12.

You do not need to book the most experienced DJ on earth — there are wonderful DJs with their first season on the calendar. But you do need to know what you are buying. A first-year DJ at a reasonable price is fine. A first-year DJ at top-tier pricing is not.

Ask, also: *how many weddings have you done in the last twelve months?* Wedding DJ is a perishable skill. Someone who did 200 weddings between 2010 and 2018 and twelve since then is rusty. Someone who did 60 in the last year is in shape.

## 3. What happens if you get sick the week of the wedding?

The honest answer is: the DJ has a backup plan, and they will tell you what it is. The dishonest answer is: "I never get sick."

Every working wedding DJ has a network of two or three other DJs who can step in on 48 hours' notice, with the same equipment list, the same music library structure, and a handoff document. If your DJ does not have that network, your wedding has no backup plan, and the day a real flu hits the week of your reception, you are calling around at 2 a.m. trying to find anyone available.

Ask for the backup DJ's name and reel. If they cannot give you either, that is your answer.

## 4. How do you handle the do-not-play list?

This is a culture question. Some DJs treat the do-not-play list as sacred — if it is on the list, it is never played, no matter who requests it, no matter how drunk the request is. Other DJs treat it as a guideline that flexes when grandma asks for the song her late husband loved.

There is no wrong answer to this question. There is only your answer. If you have a song that, for personal reasons, cannot be played at your wedding — an ex's wedding song, a song from a funeral, a song a family member loathes for a private reason — you need a DJ who will not play it under any circumstances. Tell the DJ which way you want it handled. If they hedge, that is your answer.

The full version of how to build a do-not-play list — what to put on it, what to leave off, and the cliché traps every DJ defaults to without one — is at [wedding do-not-play list: what 25 years behind the booth taught me to skip](/stikkas/journal/wedding-do-not-play-list/).

## 5. Do you take requests during the reception, and how?

Some DJs allow open requests. Some DJs let only the wedding party request. Some DJs ask the couple to pre-approve a "request from anyone" list and a "request blocked" list.

The DJs who give you the best dance floor almost always have a system, not an open door. Open requests at a wedding mean the DJ ends up playing "Mony Mony" at 9:15 because someone's uncle leaned over the booth. A wedding floor is fragile — one wrong song at the wrong moment empties the floor for forty minutes. The DJ should have a way of filtering requests, and they should be able to explain it in one sentence.

Mine is: requests go through the wedding party. Anyone else gets "great pick, I'll work it in" and I read the room before deciding.

## 6. What is your read-the-room process?

This is the question that separates working DJs from button-pressers.

Every good wedding DJ has a read-the-room process. Mine is: I watch the dance floor every thirty seconds. When the floor is full, I push the energy. When the floor empties below half, I drop into a safer pocket — a song that nearly everyone there knows, that pulls the medium-engaged dancers back. When I see three couples leaving the floor in a row, I know I am two songs from a problem and I correct hard.

A DJ who cannot explain their read-the-room process in plain English is a DJ who does not have one. They are just running a playlist.

## 7. How do you handle the must-play list?

The trap in must-play lists: a couple gives me eighteen songs they want at the wedding, and twelve of them are the couple's personal favorites that no one else will dance to. If I play all eighteen, I empty the floor twice during the reception. If I skip the ones that will not work, the couple is angry the next morning.

Ask the DJ how they handle the gap between what the couple wants and what the floor wants. The right answer is something like: "I play the must-plays during set moments — first dance, parent dances, cake cutting, any specific dedicated moments — and I work the others in when the energy supports them. If a must-play would kill the floor, I tell you that before the wedding and we decide together."

That is a DJ who is going to fight for your dance floor. The DJ who says "I'll play whatever you ask, you're the boss" is a DJ who is going to make your reception flat.

The two anchor songs for the must-play list are the first dance and the parent dances — both worth picking with real intention. The full picks-by-tempo lists are at [wedding first dance songs: a DJ's curated list of what actually works](/stikkas/journal/wedding-first-dance-songs/) and [father-daughter and mother-son dance songs: 50+ choices that actually work](/stikkas/journal/parent-dance-songs/).

## 8. What is included in your contract — and what is extra?

You would think this is obvious. It is not.

The most common surprises I see on the day of:

- The contract included a microphone for toasts, but not a stand. Stand is $40.
- The contract included one venue setup. The ceremony was outside, the reception was inside, and that is two setups. Second setup is $200.
- Cocktail-hour music was assumed but not written. Add $200.
- The contract ended at 10 p.m. The wedding wanted one more hour. Overtime is $250.
- Ceremony was an hour from the reception. Travel time was not written into the contract. Add $150.

Get every one of these spelled out in writing. The number on the website is rarely the number on the invoice. A good DJ will walk you through every line. A bad DJ will say "we'll work it out" and then bill you for it the morning of the wedding.

## 9. What gear do you bring, and do you have backup?

You do not need to be an audio engineer. You do need to ask: *do you bring backup speakers, backup mixer, backup laptop?*

In twenty-five years I have had a laptop die on me at a wedding once. I had a backup laptop in my truck, swapped it in during the cocktail hour, and no one ever knew. If I had not had a backup laptop, I would have been DJing weddings off my phone for forty-five minutes while I drove to Guitar Center. That is the difference between professional DJ and someone with one laptop.

If the DJ does not have backups, you are paying for a single point of failure. Single points of failure fail at weddings the same way they fail everywhere else.

## 10. Have you worked at my venue?

Not a deal-breaker if no. But if yes, ask about the room. Every wedding venue has a quirk. One has terrible cell signal so the streaming backup does not work. One has a sound system that conflicts with the DJ's setup. One has a power circuit that trips if the DJ and the caterer plug in at the same time.

A DJ who has worked at your venue has already solved those problems. A DJ who has not should still ask you the venue's name and look up the room before the day of.

## 11. How do you coordinate with the planner and the photographer?

The wedding is a team. The DJ is one of the players. The planner runs the timeline, the photographer captures the moments, the DJ holds the energy, the venue holds the room. When those four people coordinate, the wedding flows. When they do not, you get a first dance that starts before the photographer is in position, or a cake cutting that the DJ announces while the planner is still seating the family.

Ask the DJ: *how do you coordinate with the photographer for the first dance, the cake cutting, the toasts, the bouquet toss?* The answer should mention pre-meeting with the photographer the week of the wedding, an explicit handoff during the reception, and a known signal between them.

If the answer is "we just figure it out on the day," your reception is being run by hope.

## 12. Will you walk me through your specific plan for my reception before the day?

This is the closer.

A great wedding DJ has a meeting with the couple two to four weeks before the reception. In that meeting they walk through every minute of the night — arrival music, entrance song, first dance, dinner music, toast queue, parent dances, open floor energy build, late-night cuts, last song. They have a printed run sheet. They ask you who is being introduced and how to pronounce every name. They confirm the do-not-play list one more time. They tell you their plan for if the floor goes flat between 8:30 and 9:30, which is statistically the hardest hour of a reception to hold.

If the DJ does not have a pre-reception meeting in their workflow, do not book them. The reception is not improvised. It is planned. The plan is what you are paying for.

## The questions are the audition

Here is the secret about these twelve questions. The DJ does not just answer them — the DJ either has the answers ready or they do not. A working wedding DJ has heard every one of these questions a hundred times. They will have answers in under thirty seconds each. They will tell you stories from past weddings that illustrate the answer. They will be glad you asked.

A DJ who hedges, who pivots to the package price, who tells you "trust me," who cannot tell you who their backup DJ is — that is not a wedding DJ. That is someone who owns a speaker.

You are about to spend thousands of dollars on the person who runs the energy of the most-photographed night of your life. Spend an hour asking these twelve questions. The answer will tell you everything.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many wedding DJs should I interview before booking one?**

Three is the sweet spot. One is not enough — you have nothing to compare against. Five is too many — the calls blur together and you start making decisions based on price instead of fit. Three DJs lets you compare style, communication, and run-sheet rigor against each other, and gives you a strong sense of what a "normal" answer to each question sounds like.

**When should I book my wedding DJ?**

Eight to twelve months before the wedding for a peak Saturday in May through October. Six months is the absolute minimum for any Saturday in summer; the best working DJs are booked out a full year. For weeknight, Friday, or Sunday weddings, three to six months is usually enough. If you are inside ninety days from the wedding and have not booked, expand your geographic radius rather than lowering your standards.

**What is a normal price range for a wedding DJ?**

In a mid-size US market in 2026, a working wedding DJ runs $1,200 to $3,500 for a six-to-eight-hour reception. Top-tier wedding DJs in major metros run $3,500 to $7,000+. Anything under $800 is almost always a first-year DJ or an agency contractor. The price is rarely the problem; the problem is paying top-tier pricing for an agency-assigned first-year DJ — which is the most common mistake.

**Do I need a separate MC or will the DJ make the announcements?**

Most working wedding DJs are also the MC — they handle the entrance, introductions, parent-dance announcements, toasts, cake-cutting cue, and bouquet toss. Confirm this in writing before booking. A small number of DJs are "DJ-only" and need a separate MC; that is a fine choice if the venue has a dedicated coordinator, but it doubles the coordination burden if it does not.

**Should I give my DJ a must-play list and a do-not-play list?**

Yes to both, but keep the must-play short. Five to eight specific must-plays (first dance, parent dances, cake cutting, last song, plus two or three crowd-pullers you know your people love) is the right size. Eighteen must-plays will empty the floor twice. The do-not-play list should be specific — songs that for personal reasons cannot be played under any circumstances. Tell the DJ exactly how strict the do-not-play is.

**What is the single most important question to ask a wedding DJ?**

Will you be the DJ at my wedding, or will you send someone else? Many wedding DJ companies are agencies — the person you meet on the call books the date and assigns a contractor closer to the wedding. The person at the console is the wedding. Always get the name and reel of the specific DJ who will work your reception, in writing, before signing.
