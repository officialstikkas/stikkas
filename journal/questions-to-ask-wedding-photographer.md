# 11 questions to ask a wedding photographer before you book

*A 25-year wedding DJ on what to ask your wedding photographer before signing the contract. The questions that separate a photographer who serves the night from one who runs over it — written from the DJ booth, the only seat in the room that watches every photographer for the entire reception.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 7, 2026 · 11 min read**

---

I have DJ'd somewhere north of five hundred weddings since 2001. The DJ booth is the one seat in the room that watches the photographer work for the entire night. The bride and groom see the photographer for forty seconds at a time. The planner sees the photographer in fifteen-minute blocks. The DJ booth sees the photographer for nine straight hours — from the first cocktail-hour candid through the last sparkler exit.

I have watched photographers serve the night with such quiet skill that nobody at the wedding noticed they were there until the gallery dropped six weeks later and people cried. I have also watched photographers drain a reception by asking the bride for "just ten more couples shots" in the middle of the entrée, then complain on Instagram about the bad light when their own choices crashed the schedule.

The pattern in which photographers serve the wedding and which ones run over it is mostly set at the meeting before the contract is signed. Most couples interview a wedding photographer the way they interview a caterer — they look at the portfolio, they ask about price, they ask if the date is available, they pick. They do not ask the eleven questions that actually determine whether the photographer will be the kind of vendor everyone is grateful for or the kind everyone is hiding from by 9 p.m.

Here are the eleven questions. Print this list. Bring it to the call. The answers will tell you more than any portfolio.

## 1. Will you be the photographer at my wedding, or will you send someone else?

This is the most important question. Almost no one asks it.

Many wedding photography companies are run as studios. The person you meet on the call books the date and hands the actual wedding to a contractor. Sometimes the contractor is a great photographer with their own portfolio; sometimes the contractor is a second-year shooter who has been assigned three weddings the same weekend.

If the answer is anything other than a clear yes, ask: *who is the actual photographer, can I meet them, and can I see their recent wedding portfolio — not the studio's reel?*

A great studio will tell you straight: "I'm the studio owner, but the photographer at your wedding will be Daniela — here's her last three weddings, here are her reviews under her own name, you can meet her on a separate call." That is a fine answer. A bad answer is "we'll assign someone closer to the date" or "we have a team of equally great photographers." That means you do not know who is photographing your wedding until the morning of the wedding.

The person behind the camera is the wedding. Know who that person is.

## 2. How many weddings have you personally photographed?

Notice the word *personally*. The studio may have shot eight hundred weddings. The photographer who is going to show up at yours may have shot fourteen.

You do not have to book the most experienced photographer on earth. A first-year wedding photographer at a reasonable price is fine and the work is often great. A first-year photographer at top-tier pricing is not.

Ask, also: *how many weddings have you photographed in the last twelve months, and how many of those were in this venue or one like it?* Wedding photography is a perishable skill — venue light, reception pacing, family dynamics — and there is a sharp learning curve. A photographer who shot ninety weddings in 2018 and four since then is rusty. One who shot forty in the last year is in shape.

## 3. What happens if you get sick or injured the week of the wedding?

The honest answer is: *the photographer has a backup network and they will tell you exactly who steps in*. The dishonest answer is some version of "I never get sick" or "I'd never miss your wedding."

Every working wedding photographer has a network of two or three other photographers in the market who can step in on forty-eight hours' notice — same camera bodies, same style sensibility, same workflow for handoff. If your photographer does not have that network, your wedding has no backup plan. The day a real flu hits the week of your reception, you are calling around at 2 a.m. trying to find a wedding photographer in your market who happens to be free.

Ask for the backup's name and a sample gallery. If they cannot give you either, that is your answer.

## 4. How do you handle the timeline?

This is the single question that separates a great wedding photographer from a forgettable one.

A great wedding photographer will not quote you until they have asked you for the reception timeline, the venue floor plan, and the cocktail-hour-to-dinner transition window. They will tell you which parts of your day will fight their light: "your ceremony at 4 p.m. in October will lose direct sun by formals, so we'll need to do formals before the ceremony or use the upper terrace at golden hour." They will tell you exactly how long they need for getting-ready, formals, couples portraits, and the staged reception moments — and they will tell you which of those numbers they will trim if your timeline is tight.

A photographer who asks no timeline questions is a photographer who will shoot the wedding around their own internal clock. From the DJ booth I can see exactly when this is happening: the photographer pulls the couple out of the cocktail hour for "just twenty more minutes," and the cocktail hour is gone. The first dance pushes thirty minutes late because the formals ran over. The cake cut shifts to after the kids' bedtime because the photographer hadn't blocked the right light. Every wedding I have seen run off the rails has had some version of this story at the center.

If you take one question from this list, take this one. *How do you handle the timeline?* The full anatomy of why the timeline matters is at [Wedding Reception Timeline](/stikkas/journal/wedding-reception-timeline/).

## 5. How do you coordinate with the DJ, the planner, and the venue manager?

This is the cousin question to the timeline one, and it tells you everything about whether the photographer plays well with the rest of the vendor team.

A working wedding photographer will tell you they introduce themselves to the DJ and the venue manager within the first hour of the reception, share the timeline they are working off of, and confirm the cue for the first dance, the cake cut, the parent dances, and the exit. They will tell you they coordinate with the DJ on the order of toast announcements so the photographer is positioned for the speaker, not the audience. They will tell you they confirm with the venue manager where they can shoot from during the ceremony.

A wedding photographer who says "I just work alone" is the wedding photographer who blocks the dance floor at the first dance, gets in the DJ's line of sight at the bouquet toss, and misses the cake-cut cue because they were on the patio.

I have worked with the great ones and the terrible ones. The great ones tap me on the shoulder during the cocktail hour and say "what's your cue order for the announcements?" The terrible ones tell me to wait so they can get one more shot. Ask. The answer matters.

## 6. What is your second shooter doing during the reception?

A second shooter at a wedding is most valuable during the getting-ready and ceremony hours, when there are multiple simultaneous reactions to capture. By the reception, the second shooter is either doing real work — capturing the room while the lead is on the dance floor, getting wide reception shots while the lead is on the first dance — or they are eating dinner and chatting with the videographer.

Ask specifically: *what does your second shooter cover during the reception?* A great answer is a list: "candid table coverage during dinner, the dance floor wide while I'm in close on the bride and groom, the bar and the dessert table from the back of the room while I'm on the formal exit." A bad answer is "they help where needed."

If you are paying for a second shooter, you should know exactly what you are paying for during the most expensive hour of the wedding.

## 7. How do you handle reception lighting?

This is the question that separates wedding photographers who have actually worked weddings from photographers who shoot golden-hour content for Instagram and stop.

Wedding receptions are dim. The cocktail hour is dim. The ceremony venue might be dim. A wedding photographer needs a working plan for dim light — bounce flash, on-camera fill, the right lens choices for a moving dance floor at 1/60th of a second.

A great answer is specific: "I shoot the reception with two bodies — one with a 35mm f/1.4 for candid floor coverage and one with a 70-200 f/2.8 for distance shots from the perimeter. I use bounce flash off the ceiling during dinner and dance and switch to direct fill if the ceiling is too high or too dark to bounce." If the photographer hedges or says "natural light is my style," ask what happens when there is no natural light. The answer to that question is the gallery you get from your reception.

## 8. What is your turnaround time for the full gallery?

Get the number in writing in the contract.

A reasonable working wedding photographer turns full galleries in four to eight weeks for peak season, two to four weeks off-season. A "sneak peek" of ten to twenty teaser shots is standard within seventy-two hours of the wedding.

Anything longer than twelve weeks for a full gallery is a yellow flag. The most common reason for a delayed gallery is that the photographer over-booked the season and is now triaging deliverables — your wedding is in line behind another five from earlier in the summer. Ask explicitly: *what is your worst-case turnaround time, what causes it, and what do you do to communicate if it slips?*

## 9. Do I own the photos? Can I print them anywhere?

Read the contract.

Most working wedding photographers grant the couple a personal-use license for unlimited prints and social sharing, keep the commercial copyright themselves, and ask for tagging credit on social media. That is a fine arrangement. What you want to avoid is the contract that restricts you to ordering prints only through the photographer's print lab — sometimes at a 3x markup over a local print shop.

Ask specifically: *can I order prints from any lab I want? Can I have the full-resolution files? What is the licensing language for personal use versus the photographer's portfolio?* Get the answers in writing. The day you walk into your local pro lab — like the one I have run for years — and they tell you the contract prohibits them from making the prints, you have a different problem than you thought you had.

## 10. What happens if I am unhappy with the gallery?

Every great wedding photographer has a plan for this. The plan might be: a free re-edit of any photo you flag, a sit-down review of the gallery within thirty days of delivery, a partial refund tied to a specific delivery failure, or a re-shoot of formals if the conditions allowed.

A wedding photographer who says "that has never happened to me" is hedging. It has happened to every working photographer at some point. The honest answer is the one that names the remedy.

## 11. Will you put this all in the contract?

The eleventh question is the one that closes the other ten.

Every promise the photographer makes on the call — who the actual photographer will be, what the backup plan is, how the timeline works, what the second shooter covers, the turnaround time, the licensing terms, the remedy for an unhappy gallery — should be in the contract before you sign. A great wedding photographer will say yes immediately. A photographer who hedges on "we can sort that out later" or "the contract is standard, but we always work with couples" is telling you that the verbal answers do not have to match the written ones.

Get it in writing. Then sign.

## What the answers actually mean

The eleven questions above have a hundred and ten possible answers, and each answer tells you something. But there are three patterns to listen for.

**The photographer who answers in specifics is the photographer to hire.** "I'll be the photographer." "My backup is Maria, here is her portfolio." "I shoot the reception with these two bodies and this flash setup." "My turnaround is four weeks." Specific answers tell you the photographer has thought about every part of the job and has a system.

**The photographer who answers in generalities is the photographer to keep interviewing.** "We have a team of great photographers." "We've got it covered." "Most couples don't worry about that." General answers tell you the photographer is selling a service they have not internalized — which is the same thing as not knowing how to deliver it.

**The photographer who is annoyed by the questions is the photographer to walk away from.** The great wedding photographers love these questions. They have been waiting for a couple to ask them, because the answers are the photographer's value proposition. A photographer who is impatient with the eleven questions is a photographer who will be impatient with you on the wedding day. From the DJ booth, you can tell the difference inside the first ten minutes of the reception.

The wedding day belongs to the couple and the people who love them. The photographer is the vendor whose job it is to give that day back to the couple in pictures, twenty years from now. The eleven questions are how you find the one who will do that.
