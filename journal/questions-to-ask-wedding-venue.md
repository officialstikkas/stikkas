# 13 questions to ask your wedding venue before you sign

*A 25-year wedding DJ on what venue managers conveniently leave out of the tour. Noise ordinances, load-in restrictions, hidden fees, the last-call surprise, the bar-cannot-move clause — written from the loading dock and the breaker panel, not the showroom.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 7, 2026 · 12 min read**

---

I have DJ'd somewhere north of five hundred weddings since 2001, in roughly four hundred different venues. The DJ does not see the venue from the showroom or the bridal suite. The DJ sees the venue from the loading dock, the breaker panel, the back stairs, the bar storage, and the green-room hallway where the staff hides between the salad course and the entrée.

That perspective changes what you ask the venue.

A couple touring a wedding venue sees the ceiling and the chandeliers and asks about the floor plan and the catering minimum. The venue manager gives the tour they have given a thousand times. Both parties are honest in the way real-estate agents and home buyers are honest at an open house: nobody is lying, and a great many true things are not being said.

The thirteen questions below are the ones the venue manager hopes you do not ask, because each one closes a future excuse for an overage charge or a flat night. I have watched all thirteen of them play out in real weddings, on dance floors that landed and dance floors that did not. Print this list. Bring it to the tour. Ask. Get the answers in writing.

## 1. What time does the noise ordinance shut us down?

This is the most important question and the venue tour almost never volunteers the answer.

Almost every outdoor venue and many indoor venues sit inside a municipal noise ordinance. The ordinance time — usually 10 p.m., sometimes 11 p.m., occasionally midnight, occasionally 9 p.m. for residential areas — is the time the DJ has to drop volume to a level where dinner conversation is the loudest sound in the room. For a dance floor, that is the end of the dance floor, full stop.

A 10 p.m. ordinance and a 6 p.m. ceremony gives you about three hours of dance floor after dinner. A 10 p.m. ordinance and a 5 p.m. ceremony gives you four. The difference between those two reception lengths is the difference between an exhausted dance floor at the last song and a half-empty one.

Ask explicitly: *what is the noise ordinance time, what does enforcement look like, and what does the venue do if someone calls in a noise complaint?* A great venue tells you "10 p.m. for outdoor amplified music, 11 p.m. for indoor with the windows closed, we have never had a complaint when we follow our own rules." A bad venue tells you "don't worry about it." That answer changes the entire timeline. The full timeline math is at [Wedding Reception Timeline](/stikkas/journal/wedding-reception-timeline/).

## 2. Are there hidden fees that don't show up in the base venue price?

The base venue price is usually about 60-70% of the actual venue cost. The other 30-40% comes from line items the contract pulls in once it is being negotiated.

Ask for a full fees-and-charges list before you sign. The most common hidden ones are:

- **Required venue coordinator** — $500 to $1,500. Some venues require their own coordinator on top of the wedding planner you may have hired.
- **Required parking attendant** — $200 to $600. Sometimes city-required, sometimes venue-required.
- **Required security guard** — $40 to $60 per hour for receptions ending after 9 p.m.
- **Cake-cutting fee** — $2 to $5 per slice if the venue catering serves a brought-in cake.
- **Corkage fee** — $15 to $25 per bottle for couples bringing in their own alcohol.
- **In-house catering minimums** — often quoted at the floor; the realistic per-head minimum is 30-50% higher.
- **Generator rental** — $300 to $800 for outdoor receptions without sufficient on-site power.
- **Post-event cleaning fee** — $400 to $1,200, often non-negotiable.
- **Overtime charges** — $250 to $500 per hour past the contracted end time.

A clean venue contract puts all of these on the page before you sign. A venue that "discusses" any of these later is a venue that will discover one more line item the week of the wedding.

## 3. Can I bring in my own DJ, photographer, florist, and caterer or do I have to use the preferred list?

Some venues require you to book vendors from a preferred list. The reason varies: sometimes the venue genuinely knows those vendors respect the building and the timeline; more often, the venue takes a referral fee from the vendors on the list.

A required preferred-vendor list can add twenty to forty percent to the wedding's total vendor cost.

Ask explicitly: *is this a recommended list or a required list?* If required, ask what happens if you want to use a vendor not on the list — sometimes there is an outside-vendor fee ($200-$500), sometimes a vendor-approval process, sometimes a flat no.

If the answer is "required only, no outside vendors," that should change either your venue choice or your budget math. A preferred list as a resource is helpful. A preferred list as a wall is a 30% line item nobody quoted you.

## 4. What time can my vendors load in and load out?

The load-in and load-out window is the vendor team's runway.

A great venue allows a four-hour load-in for the DJ, the florist, the rental company, the caterer, and the photographer's lighting kit. A weak venue allows ninety minutes, then charges an early-access fee.

A great venue allows next-morning load-out for non-perishable equipment — the DJ booth, the rental tables and chairs, the florist's structural pieces. A weak venue requires same-night load-out within two hours of the contracted end time. Same-night load-out means the DJ is breaking down at midnight, the rental company is stacking tables in a dim parking lot at 1 a.m., and the florist is back the next morning anyway to retrieve the structural pieces.

Ask: *load-in window, load-out window, after-hours access policy, and any associated fees for either.* Get the answers in the contract. A venue that allows reasonable load-in and load-out windows is sometimes worth $1,500 more than a cheaper venue with restrictive ones — the savings come back through reduced vendor overtime.

## 5. Does the venue have a backup plan for rain or extreme weather?

This is the question every outdoor venue avoids. Ask it.

A great outdoor venue has a written rain plan — an indoor space the ceremony or reception moves to, or a tent that is either on-site or pre-arranged with a rental partner at no extra cost to the couple. The decision is made by a stated authority (the venue manager or the wedding planner) at a stated time (seventy-two hours out, forty-eight hours out, the morning of) using a stated forecast threshold (30% rain chance or higher; sustained wind above a stated speed; temperature below a stated low).

A weak outdoor venue says "we'll figure it out the morning of." That answer means: the couple is panicking the night before, the rental company has not been alerted, the florist's outdoor structural plan is now wrong, and the DJ's outdoor power configuration is now wrong.

The rain plan is one of the venue's most important deliverables. Ask. Get the trigger criteria and the decision-maker named in the contract.

## 6. What is the venue's power configuration for the DJ and the band?

This is the question a working DJ wishes every couple asked.

A great venue has dedicated 20-amp outlets in the DJ corner of the reception space, separate from the kitchen circuit, separate from the lighting circuit. A weak venue runs the DJ's speakers off the same circuit as the catering's warming trays — which trips the breaker at the entrée course every wedding for three years until the venue manager finally fixes it.

Ask: *where are the dedicated DJ outlets, what amperage are they rated for, what circuit are they on, and is there a backup if the breaker trips during the reception?* If the venue manager does not know the answer, the venue's electrical configuration has not been thought through. That is a fixable problem — the DJ can bring extension cords and request a circuit map ahead of time — but you want to know in advance whether the venue's power is a feature or a problem.

For outdoor venues, ask: *what is the on-site power source, is it generator or grid, and if generator, who provides it and what fuel runtime?* A generator that runs out of diesel at 9:45 p.m. is a real story I have lived through. The full version is in the [Wedding Vendor Tracker Spreadsheet](/stikkas/journal/wedding-vendor-tracker-spreadsheet/) under the venue column.

## 7. What time is last call, and what time does the bar shut down?

The bar timing rules the second half of the reception.

A great venue has a stated last-call time — usually thirty to forty-five minutes before the contracted end time — and a stated bar-close time at the contracted end. A weak venue closes the bar whenever the bartender decides, which is sometimes at 9 p.m. when the wedding still has two hours of dance floor left.

Ask: *what time is last call by venue policy, who makes the call, and can it be moved if the wedding wants the bar open longer?* Most venues will negotiate a thirty-minute extension for an overtime fee. Most venues will not negotiate the dance floor being dry for the last forty-five minutes of the reception, because that is a logistics problem they have not solved.

The bar close also tells the DJ when to plan the energy peak of the night. A 10:30 p.m. bar close and an 11 p.m. last song means the energy peak needs to land at 10 p.m. A 9:30 p.m. bar close pulls everything earlier. Time it before signing the contract.

## 8. Can the bar be moved?

This sounds petty. It is the single most underrated venue question.

The bar's position decides the geography of the cocktail hour and the reception. If the bar is in a corner away from the dance floor, the cocktail hour spreads through the venue and the dance floor activates faster after dinner. If the bar is right next to the dance floor, the cocktail hour stays compact and the dance floor competes with the bar all night.

Many venue bars are built in, and cannot be moved. The venue often does not volunteer this. Ask: *can the bar be moved or is it fixed? If fixed, where is it relative to the dance floor and the dinner tables?* If the bar location does not work for your floor plan, that is sometimes a deal-breaker the venue would have happily not mentioned.

## 9. How does the ceremony-to-reception transition work?

This is the venue's hardest hour and the one the tour glosses over.

A great venue has a clear transition plan: the ceremony chairs are flipped or relocated, the cocktail hour activates in a different room or zone, the reception space is dressed during the cocktail hour, the guests are funneled through a defined path. The venue staff is briefed on the timing.

A weak venue says "we'll handle it." Which often means the cocktail hour is sixty minutes long but the ceremony chairs are still being broken down at minute forty-five, so the guests are loitering by the bar without a place to sit, and the photographer is trying to do family formals in the same space that is being struck.

Ask: *walk me through the ceremony-to-reception transition, minute by minute, with the staff roles named.* If the answer is general, the transition will be general.

## 10. What is the venue coordinator's actual role on the day?

The phrase "venue coordinator" means very different things at different venues.

At one venue, the coordinator is essentially a wedding planner — they run the timeline, coordinate vendors, handle problems, and the couple does not need a separate planner. At another venue, the coordinator unlocks the doors, points the caterer at the kitchen, and disappears. Both are valid roles. They are very different roles.

Ask specifically: *what does your coordinator do on the wedding day, what hours are they on-site, what happens if there is a problem at 9 p.m., and do they coordinate with my outside planner?*

If the venue coordinator's role is "unlock the doors and disappear," you need to budget for a separate wedding planner. If the venue coordinator's role is full-service, your planner budget is smaller. Knowing which is which before signing is worth real money.

## 11. What happens if the venue closes, sells, or goes bankrupt before my wedding?

The 2020-2021 wedding venue closures taught this question to the industry. It still gets skipped.

Read the closure clause in the contract. A strong contract guarantees a full deposit refund if the venue cannot fulfill the date. A weak contract limits liability to the deposit, or only offers a rebooking at the new ownership's discretion.

Most wedding-specific event insurance policies cover this exact scenario for around $200 to $400. Some venues require event insurance as a condition of booking; if yours does not, buy it anyway. The day a venue closure email lands six weeks before the wedding is the day that $300 insurance policy is the best money you spent on the wedding.

## 12. What is the noise ordinance at 8 p.m. and what is it at midnight?

Some municipal noise ordinances step down throughout the night. Outdoor amplified music might be allowed at 95 dB at 8 p.m., then 75 dB at 10 p.m., then 60 dB at midnight. The venue may not volunteer the step-down schedule.

Ask. A step-down ordinance means the DJ's energy peak has to land before the step-down or the dance floor empties on the technical detail of decibel limits. A flat ordinance is easier to plan around than a step-down one — both can work, neither should surprise you on the wedding night.

## 13. Will you put all of this in the contract?

The thirteenth question closes the other twelve.

Every promise the venue manager makes on the tour — the load-in time, the rain plan, the noise ordinance, the fee list, the coordinator's role, the closure clause, the bar position — should be in the contract before you sign. A great venue says yes immediately. A venue that hedges on "the contract is standard, but we always work with couples" is telling you that the verbal answers do not have to match the written ones.

Get every answer in writing. The venue is the largest single contract of the wedding. It deserves the most rigorous one.

## What the answers actually mean

Venue managers fall into three categories, and you can sort them after twenty minutes of touring.

**The venue manager who answers in specifics is the venue to seriously consider.** "Load-in is 1 p.m. for vendors. Noise ordinance is 10 p.m. with windows closed for indoor amplified music. Our preferred vendor list is a resource, not a requirement. The closure clause refunds all deposits." Specifics tell you the venue has rehearsed the answers because they have actually fielded the questions.

**The venue manager who answers in generalities is the venue manager to keep interviewing.** "We're flexible." "We work with all your vendors." "Most couples don't worry about that." Generalities tell you the venue has a different answer for each couple, and yours will arrive on the wedding day as a surprise charge.

**The venue manager who is annoyed by the questions is the venue to walk away from.** The thirteen questions above are the venue's value proposition, the same way the eleven photographer questions are the photographer's. A venue manager who is impatient with them is a venue manager who will be impatient with you at 9 p.m. on the wedding night when something goes wrong.

The venue is the room the wedding happens in. The venue contract is the document the wedding happens under. The thirteen questions tell you whether the room and the contract match.
