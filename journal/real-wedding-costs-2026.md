# Real wedding costs in 2026

*What couples are actually paying in major US metros — by category, with the 2026 numbers most templates get wrong, told from twenty-five years behind a DJ console.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 6, 2026 · 12 min read**

---

I've worked over five hundred weddings since 2001. Most of them have been in and around Los Angeles, but I've done destination work in Palm Springs, San Diego, Napa, Vegas, and a few that flew the whole production out to Hawaii or Cabo. Across all of that, the most consistent thing I've learned about wedding budgets is this: **the gap between what the templates say a wedding costs and what couples actually paid is roughly one full category off.**

The template says "$30,000 average wedding." The check the couple wrote in 2025 says forty-five. The template says "florals are eight percent of budget." The florist's final invoice is thirteen. The template says "venue is one-third." The venue actually consumed half by the time chair upgrades, the bartender fee, the cake-cutting line, and the vendor meal count rolled up.

I want to give you the 2026 numbers that match what couples are actually writing on their checks. Not a template. Not a "national average" that pulled survey data from 2022 weddings and called it a year. The hard, by-category numbers that match what I see when I show up at four o'clock with two crates of speakers and watch the entire production roll past the booth.

I also want to tell you, by category, what you're actually paying for — because once you know that, the budget stops being a guess. It becomes a series of decisions you understand.

## The headline number: $35k to $55k for 100–130 guests, in most metros

Let's pin the range first.

If you are planning a wedding in 2026 for **100 to 130 guests** in a **major US metro** with **professional vendors across the board** and a **standard Saturday-in-season** date, the realistic total is **$35,000 to $55,000**.

- **Coastal metros** — Los Angeles, San Francisco, New York, Boston, DC, Miami — routinely cross **$60,000** for the same guest count. Sixty-five to seventy-five is now common for a Saturday wedding at a real venue in any of those markets.
- **Mid-tier metros** — Phoenix, Charlotte, Nashville, Austin, Denver, Portland, Atlanta — sit at **$30,000 to $45,000**.
- **Smaller metros and rural** — most of the South outside Atlanta/Nashville, most of the Midwest outside Chicago, most of the Mountain West — can land at **$22,000 to $35,000** if the couple is intentional about vendors.

The widely quoted "national average wedding cost" — usually pulled from a 2022 survey and reprinted as if it still applied — is in the **$28,000 to $32,000** range. That number is now off by twenty to fifty percent in real metros. **Couples who plan against the 2022 number get blindsided at the venue tasting.**

What changed?

Two things, mostly. The first is the obvious one: food costs are way up. The per-plate number that used to be $85 in 2022 is routinely $110 to $140 in 2026, and a venue that bundles food, beverage, rental, and staff into one all-inclusive line went from $150 per head to $220 per head over the same window. The second is labor. Every vendor in the wedding supply chain — caterer wait staff, florist delivery teams, hair-and-makeup artists, DJ assistants, photo second-shooters — has raised their hourly rate, and those rate increases compound through every line item.

> The 2026 wedding is not the 2022 wedding with inflation added. It is a different category of cost.

That is the headline. Let's get into the lines.

## Category 1 — Venue and catering (40 to 50 percent of budget)

Venue and catering is the line that consumed half the wedding by the time everything resolved. This is true at almost every wedding I've worked. The line ends up bigger than the couple planned because they tracked the **site fee** during the venue search but didn't track the **all-in number**.

In 2026:

- **Venue site fee alone** (without food, beverage, rentals, staff): **$3,500 to $12,000** in most metros. Coastal premium venues with city views or beachfront access can run $20,000 to $35,000 for the room alone.
- **Full-service venue with food and beverage** for 100 to 130 guests: **$18,000 to $35,000**. Premium venues will cross $45,000.
- **All-inclusive venue** (venue + food + beverage + cake + DJ + photographer in one package): **$22,000 to $40,000**. The DJ and photographer in these packages are almost always the venue's preferred vendors at the low end of the market — fine, but not what you'd hire if you were sourcing each independently.

**What you're actually paying for, line by line:**

- **The room.** This is the site fee — the actual rental of the space for the day, usually six to ten hours of access.
- **Food, plated or buffet.** Per-plate costs that used to anchor at $85 in 2022 are $110 to $140 in 2026 for a standard three-course plated meal with a salad, a protein, and a vegetable side. Premium plates with filet mignon, sea bass, or two-protein offerings cross $160.
- **Bar.** Open bar for 100 guests for four hours runs $4,500 to $9,000 depending on the tier. House beer and wine is the bottom of that range; premium with cocktails and a signature drink is the top.
- **Rentals.** Linens, glassware, chairs, plates, and silverware are sometimes included and sometimes a la carte. When they're a la carte, they usually add **$1,800 to $3,500** that the couple didn't see coming.
- **Service staff.** Wait staff, bartenders, captains. Often a percentage of the food and beverage line — usually 18 to 22 percent — and sometimes a flat hourly rate per server. Either way, it shows up at the bottom of the invoice.
- **Cake-cutting fee.** A real line on a lot of caterer contracts — $1.50 to $3 per slice, which adds $150 to $400 on a 100-guest wedding for a service the caterer is doing in 45 seconds. Negotiate this out at contract time.
- **Vendor meals.** Most caterers require a discounted meal for every vendor working the reception — DJ, photographer, videographer, day-of coordinator, hair-and-makeup if they're staying, sometimes the band's full personnel. Five to ten extra meals at $45 to $65 each is **$225 to $650** of additional food that couples consistently forget.

The line couples consistently underprice: **the bar**. Open bar is the social anchor of the reception, and it is also the most flexible line item in the venue contract. Going from premium open to beer-and-wine-only saves $2,500 to $5,000 on a 100-guest wedding. Most couples don't realize this is an option because the venue led with the premium package.

## Category 2 — Photography and video (10 to 15 percent)

Photography is the line that couples consistently get right. Most couples I've worked with treated photo as a non-negotiable and budgeted accordingly. The line that gets cut, or under-budgeted, is **video**.

In 2026:

- **Wedding photography, 8-hour day, working pro** in a major metro: **$4,500 to $9,000**. This typically includes an engagement session, a second shooter for the wedding day, and a digital gallery delivered within 6 to 8 weeks.
- **Wedding photography, premium tier**: $10,000 to $18,000. You're paying for a name, a 30-page printed book delivered, and a signature editing style.
- **Wedding videography**: $3,500 to $7,500 for a working pro delivering a 4-to-6-minute highlight film plus a longer documentary cut.
- **Drone footage**, when permitted: an add-on of $500 to $1,500.

Below $3,500 for photo or $3,000 for video, you are typically getting a hobbyist or someone building a portfolio. Sometimes that's fine — every working pro started somewhere. But you should know you are buying a portfolio-building shoot, not a polished professional deliverable, and the gallery often takes longer to come back.

**Where couples consistently underprice:** the **getting-ready coverage hour**. Most couples assume the photographer arrives at the venue. Working pros include 60 to 90 minutes of getting-ready coverage in the bridal suite — the dress hanging, the rings on the windowsill, the mother-of-the-bride moment. Cutting this hour saves $500 to $800 and removes about a third of the most-shared photos from your gallery. It's almost never worth the savings.

## Category 3 — Florals and decor (10 to 15 percent)

Florals have moved up the line since 2022. They used to be a 6-to-8 percent budget item and now routinely hit 10 to 15 percent for a couple who wants the wedding to feel floral-anchored.

In 2026:

- **Modest floral package** — bridal bouquet, four bridesmaid bouquets, ten centerpieces, ceremony arch florals: **$4,500 to $7,500** in most metros.
- **Standard floral package** — the above plus aisle decor, boutonnieres, corsages for the parents, a sweetheart-table arrangement: **$6,500 to $10,500**.
- **Lush floral wedding** — installations on the arch, hanging florals, full chuppah or floral wall, bar decor, escort-card arrangement: **$12,000 to $25,000 and up**. This is the wedding you see on Pinterest. It costs what Pinterest doesn't show.

**Why florals moved up:** imported blooms (specifically peonies, garden roses, and dahlias) are more expensive in 2026 than they were in 2022. Floral foam is up. Labor — the team of three to five people who arrive at 7 a.m. to install and stay through breakdown — is significantly more expensive per hour. And the trend toward installation florals (hanging arrangements, full-coverage arches) requires both more labor and more flowers per dollar of visual.

**The "one more arrangement" trap:** at the florist consultation, every couple I've ever talked to said yes to at least one add-on they didn't plan. The escort-card arrangement. The four bud vases for the bar. The petals down the aisle. None of these line items is more than $400 individually. Three of them together is $1,200 that wasn't on the spreadsheet. Walk into the consultation with a hard budget number and a copy of the line items already planned. Decide before you walk in which add-ons you'd say yes to, and which would push you over.

## Category 4 — Music and entertainment (8 to 12 percent)

This is my line. I have stood in this category at five hundred receptions, so I can tell you exactly what couples are actually paying.

In 2026:

- **Working pro wedding DJ**, full day (ceremony, cocktail, reception, ~6 hours coverage): **$2,500 to $5,500** in a major US metro.
- **Tier-B wedding DJ**, reception only: **$1,500 to $2,500**. Equipment is fine, experience is variable. The booking has gone to whoever was available rather than whoever was best.
- **Hobbyist DJ**, less than $1,500: someone working out of a garage with consumer equipment and limited wedding experience. The microphone will hum on the toasts.
- **Live band, four to six pieces, for a reception**: **$8,000 to $14,000**. Premium and name-brand bands cross $20,000.
- **DJ + live musician hybrid** (DJ for most of the night plus an acoustic ceremony musician or sax accompaniment for dance): $3,500 to $7,500.

**Where couples underprice:** the **ceremony coverage line**. Most couples assume "the DJ" means the reception only. If your ceremony is at the same venue as the reception, ask whether the DJ can handle ceremony processional and recessional music, the cocktail hour playlist, and toast-time microphone management. This is usually an add-on of $500 to $1,000 to the reception fee, and it eliminates the need for a separate ceremony musician (string quartet or harpist runs $800 to $1,800).

**The mistake almost every couple makes:** picking the DJ on price alone. The cheapest DJ in your market is almost certainly not the best one. The most expensive isn't necessarily better than the middle tier. Read reviews, ask whether they have a backup DJ if they get sick the day of, and ask whether they bring redundant gear (two laptops, two controllers, backup speakers). Wedding DJing is the most leveraged service-vendor line in your budget — a great DJ makes a $35,000 wedding feel like a $60,000 wedding, and a bad DJ makes the opposite true.

## Category 5 — Attire and beauty (5 to 10 percent)

In 2026:

- **Wedding dress**, off-the-rack designer: **$2,500 to $6,000**. Couture or custom: $8,000 and up.
- **Alterations**: $400 to $1,200. Couples consistently underbudget this.
- **Veil and accessories**: $300 to $1,500.
- **Bridesmaid dresses**: usually paid by bridesmaids themselves, but the bride often covers in the $150-$300 per dress range.
- **Groom's suit or tux**: $400 to $1,500 if buying; $200 to $400 if renting (rentals are increasingly out of fashion — the suit is usually a buy now).
- **Hair and makeup, day of, for bride + 4 bridesmaids + 2 mothers**: **$900 to $2,500** depending on whether the artist comes to the bridal suite.
- **Trial run**: $150 to $300 for each artist.

**Underbudgeted line:** alterations. A $3,500 dress that needs $900 of alterations is a $4,400 dress, and the alterations line almost always appears late — six to eight weeks before the wedding — when the budget is already drawn against. Build alterations as its own line at $1,000 from week one.

## Category 6 — Stationery and signage (2 to 5 percent)

In 2026:

- **Save-the-dates** for 75 households: **$200 to $500** for printed and mailed; $50 to $150 for digital.
- **Invitations + RSVP cards + envelopes** for 75 households: **$500 to $1,500** for printed; less for digital RSVP via website. Premium letterpress or foil-stamped invitations: $1,800 to $4,500.
- **Day-of paper goods** — programs, menus, escort cards, table numbers, signage: **$300 to $1,200** if printed at home from a template, $800 to $2,500 if professionally printed.

This is where a [printable wedding planner kit](/stikkas/journal/print-wedding-planner-at-home/) and a home printer pays for itself. Couples who use professionally printed day-of paper consistently pay 3-5x what they would pay printing high-quality template files at home with proper paper stock. The output looks identical at a 3-foot viewing distance.

## Category 7 — Cake and dessert (1 to 3 percent)

In 2026:

- **Tiered wedding cake** for 100 guests: **$600 to $1,800** depending on tiers, fondant vs buttercream, and detail level.
- **Dessert table or alternative** (cupcakes, donuts, French macarons): **$400 to $1,200**.
- **Late-night snacks** (pizza arrival at 10:30 p.m., taco bar, mini sliders): $400 to $1,500.

Cake almost always gets right-sized to the budget. The line that gets cut, when budgets tighten in week 8, is **late-night snacks**. It probably shouldn't be — the late-night arrival of pizza or tacos around 10:30 p.m. is one of the strongest "best wedding I've been to" guest signals. It's a $400-to-$1,500 line item that buys disproportionate guest memory.

## Category 8 — Everything else (5 to 10 percent — and bigger than you think)

This is the bucket couples never see coming, and it consistently runs to **5 to 10 percent** of total budget. Sometimes more.

- **Marriage license**: $35 to $150 (varies by state and county). Tiny line, just don't forget it.
- **Officiant honorarium**: $250 to $800. Friend-officiants often refuse payment but should receive a gift; professional officiants charge $500 to $1,500.
- **Rehearsal dinner**: $1,500 to $5,000 for 25 to 40 attendees. This is its own mini-wedding and consistently absorbs 5 to 8 percent of the wedding budget couples didn't allocate for it.
- **Transportation**: getaway car for the couple ($300 to $800), party bus for the bridal party ($600 to $1,800), guest shuttles ($800 to $2,500). The shuttle line is the surprise.
- **Day-of coordinator**: $1,200 to $3,500. Increasingly considered non-optional and worth every dollar. The coordinator is the difference between a smooth day and a stressed one.
- **Welcome bags for out-of-town guests**: $5 to $15 per guest.
- **Tips and gratuities**: 10 to 20 percent across every service vendor. For a $40,000 wedding, that's $4,000 to $8,000.

Build "Tips" as its own line at the same level as the categories. A couple I worked with last summer skipped the tips line in their budget and discovered the morning of the wedding that they were $3,200 short on the cash they'd planned to hand out at the end of the reception. They had to put the tips on a credit card. Don't do that.

## The 2026 inflation tax — how much higher than 2022 numbers

If you're working from a budget template that was built in 2022 or early 2023, **scale every category up by 25 to 40 percent** before you trust it. The categories that moved the most:

- **Food and beverage**: +30 to +45 percent.
- **Florals**: +20 to +35 percent.
- **Photography and video**: +10 to +20 percent.
- **DJ and entertainment**: +10 to +15 percent.
- **Transportation**: +20 to +30 percent (fuel + driver labor).
- **Hair and makeup**: +15 to +25 percent.
- **Stationery and printing**: +15 to +25 percent.

The categories that have stayed roughly stable: dress purchase (designers haven't moved as much as you'd expect), marriage license and officiant honoraria, late-night snack pricing.

## The three numbers you actually have to know before booking anything

If I had to pick three numbers a couple has to commit to before they sign a single vendor contract, it would be these:

1. **Your guest count.** This decides 60 percent of the budget. Every 10 additional guests is roughly $1,500 to $2,500 of additional cost — food, beverage, rentals, stationery, transportation, vendor meals, possibly an additional bridesmaid bouquet. Cut guest count by 20 before you cut anything else if the budget is tight.
2. **Your venue + catering all-in number.** Not the site fee. The all-in number including food, beverage, staff, rentals, cake-cutting fee, and vendor meals. This is what consumes half the budget.
3. **Your DJ or band line.** This is the line that decides whether the room dances. Make it real, not aspirational. A $1,200 DJ at a $50,000 wedding is the wrong allocation.

Every other line on the spreadsheet is downstream of these three.

## The two-quote rule (sometimes three)

For every vendor category, get **at least two written quotes**. For venue and catering, get **three**. This is not paranoia — it is how you learn the actual market rate in your specific metro for your specific date.

You will be amazed at the variance. Photographers in the same metro, with similar portfolios, with similar review counts, often quote 30 to 50 percent apart. Florists are even worse — same flower list, two florists, the second quote is consistently $1,200 less than the first or $1,200 more. Without two quotes, you don't know which one is the real market rate.

Caveat: don't pick on price alone. Pick on a combined signal of price, gut feel, and the questions they asked you during the consult. The vendor who asked the most thoughtful questions about your day is usually the one who runs the day well.
