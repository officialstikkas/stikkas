# Teacher Planner 2026-2027: The Complete Back to School Setup Guide

Back to school season starts earlier than most teachers expect — not in August, when the kids show up, but in July, when the planning does. The teachers who walk into their classroom feeling prepared in September started building their systems in July.

This guide covers what an effective teacher planner should contain, how to set it up before the school year starts, and how to use it as a live document rather than a checklist you fill out once and abandon.

---

## What Actually Goes in a Good Teacher Planner

Not all teacher planners are built the same. Many are beautiful but shallow — they look impressive and don't hold up under the load of an actual school year. Here's what separates a functional teacher planner from a decorative one.

### The Non-Negotiables

**Weekly lesson plan template**

This should show every subject or period across the five-day week, with room for objectives, materials, and notes. The format that works: columns are days, rows are time blocks. You need to see the whole week at once — not flip through daily pages.

**Class roster with parent contacts**

Full name, preferred name, parent or guardian names, primary contact number, email, and notes. One row per student. This is your source of truth for the whole year.

**Seating chart grid**

A blank grid (5×6 or 6×7) you can reconfigure throughout the year. Print it on cardstock, put it in a page protector, use a dry-erase marker. Rearrange as needed without reprinting.

**Parent communication log**

For every call, email, or note you send or receive from a parent: date, student name, method of contact, topic, outcome, and follow-up needed. This log is your documentation if anything escalates.

**Goal tracker and year overview**

Annual learning objectives broken into quarters or semesters. When you can see the whole year at once, pacing decisions become obvious.

**Substitute teacher quick-reference**

Your sub should be able to open one page and run your classroom for a day without calling you. Classroom rules, schedule, student notes (who needs extra support, who has medical needs, who the reliable helpers are), and your contact info.

### Nice-to-Haves That Actually Get Used

- **Professional development tracker** — log trainings and PD hours as you complete them; useful at review time
- **Curriculum pacing guide** — map your curriculum to the school calendar, accounting for testing windows and breaks
- **Resource and supply inventory** — what consumables you have at the start of the year vs. what you need

### What to Skip

- Multi-page "reflection" spreads that take 20 minutes to fill in
- Daily mood trackers
- Anything requiring specialized pens or a Cricut

A teacher planner should save you time, not create a secondary art project.

## How to Set Up Your Teacher Planner Before Day One

The August classroom rush is not the time to set up your system. Do this in July, when you still have mental bandwidth.

### Step 1: Block the School Calendar (Week 1 of Setup)

Open your school district's official calendar. Fill in these dates first:

- First and last day of school
- All holidays and school breaks
- Testing windows (especially if you're in a tested grade or subject)
- Professional development days
- Report card and progress report deadlines
- Back-to-school night and parent conference dates

Once these are in, every lesson plan and deadline you write afterward will be realistic.

### Step 2: Build Your Class Roster (Week 1)

You may not have your final roster until August, but you can build the template and pre-fill what you know. When rosters drop:

1. Enter every student name (legal name and preferred name)
2. Note any IEP or 504 flags — not details, just that documentation exists
3. Add primary parent or guardian contact info
4. Note any medical needs in a confidential column you'll reference privately

This takes 45 minutes if you do it once, properly. It saves 10+ hours of scrambling over the year.

### Step 3: Map Your First Unit (Week 2)

Don't plan the whole year in July. Plan the first four to six weeks in detail, and rough-sketch the rest of the semester. Your first unit plan should answer:

- What are the learning objectives?
- What are the day-by-day topics?
- What materials or resources do I need before Day 1?
- What's my assessment for this unit?

Fill this into your weekly lesson plan template so you walk into September with your first month already scaffolded.

### Step 4: Write Your Substitute Quick-Reference (Week 2)

This sounds premature. It's not. Writing your sub quick-reference before the year starts means you do it when you're calm and organized, not at 6 AM when you're calling in sick.

Cover:
- Daily schedule (times, periods, transitions)
- Classroom rules (your 3–5 core rules, simply stated)
- Seating chart (paste or attach a copy)
- The three students who are reliable classroom helpers
- The three students who may need extra support (keep it general — "works best with visual prompts," not full IEP details)
- Emergency procedures (fire drill location, lockdown protocol)
- Your contact info and department head's contact info

### Step 5: Set Up Your Parent Communication Log

Create the log header now: date, student, contact name, method, topic, outcome, follow-up needed. The first entry you'll make is the first time you reach out to a parent — ideally a positive call in the first two weeks.

The communication log pays dividends at conference time and is essential documentation if a situation escalates.

## Using Your Planner as a Live Document

The biggest mistake teachers make with a new planner: using it religiously for three weeks and stopping.

A teacher planner should be touched every day, not every month. Here's how to make that happen.

**The 10-minute Monday morning check-in:**
- Review the week's lesson plan — anything that needs adjustment based on last week's pace?
- Check the parent communication log — any follow-ups due this week?
- Look at the week's upcoming dates — any assessments, events, or deadlines you need to prep for?

**The 5-minute Friday close:**
- Note any lesson modifications ("skipped the group activity — too long; move to Monday")
- Log any parent communications from the week
- Set next Monday's priorities

**The quarterly review:**
- How's pacing vs. the curriculum map?
- What's working in your lesson plan template?
- What needs to change for next quarter?

Teachers who do the Monday 10 and Friday 5 are the ones who say their planner actually changed how their year felt.

## Digital vs. Print: What Works for Teachers

**Go digital if:**
- You carry an iPad and use an Apple Pencil already
- You teach in multiple classrooms or travel between buildings
- You like being able to search your own notes
- You want to duplicate or modify pages without reprinting
- You collaborate with a co-teacher who needs access

**Print if:**
- You think better with a pen in hand
- Your classroom environment is hard on devices (elementary classrooms, labs)
- You want a physical record that doesn't require a charged device

**The hybrid approach (most common):**
- Digital for lesson planning and pacing — easy to update, searchable
- Print for the sub quick-reference, seating chart, and parent log — physical documents are easier to hand off and feel more official

## The First Week Survival Checklist

No matter how prepared you are, the first week of school is chaotic. Use this quick-reference:

**Before students arrive:**
- Class roster printed and reviewed
- Seating chart ready (pencil, not pen)
- Sub quick-reference complete and filed
- Parent communication log set up
- First unit plan in lesson plan template
- Supplies inventoried; any gaps ordered
- Emergency procedures reviewed

**First day:**
- Introduce classroom rules (3–5 only)
- Run the seating chart (expect changes)
- Note any students who need follow-up

**First week:**
- Make one positive parent phone call per day
- Lock in the first unit pace
- Update roster with any new students or changes

**End of week one:**
- Friday 5-minute close: what needs to change for week two?
- First entry in the professional development log

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When does back to school planning start?**
Effective planning starts six to eight weeks before school — mid-July for September starts. The teachers who feel prepared on Day 1 started their planner setup in July.

**What size paper does a teacher planner print on?**
Standard US Letter (8.5×11 inches). Print at "Actual Size" in your printer dialog — never "Fit to Page," which shrinks the layout and makes text harder to read.

**What's the best paper weight for a printed teacher planner?**
65–80 lb cardstock for pages you'll handle frequently. 100 lb for anything you'll laminate (seating charts, schedule cards). Regular 20 lb copy paper is fine for pages you'll tuck into a binder.

**Can I share the digital planner with a co-teacher?**
