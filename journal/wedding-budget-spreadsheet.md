# How to build a wedding budget spreadsheet

*A 25-year wedding DJ on what couples actually spend, what gets cut at the last minute, and how to build a spreadsheet that survives first contact with vendor quotes.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 3, 2026 · 9 min read**

---

I have stood behind a DJ console at maybe five hundred weddings over the last twenty-five years. I am not the planner, I am not the venue, and I am not the bride. I am the guy who shows up at four o'clock with two crates of speakers and watches the entire wedding happen from the angle no one else gets to see — including the angle where you can tell, by 8 pm, which couples are still on budget and which couples are quietly recalculating their honeymoon.

I want to tell you what I've learned about wedding budgets from that angle. Specifically, I want to tell you how to build a wedding budget spreadsheet that survives the first three vendor quotes, what almost always gets cut at the last minute, and which line items the popular templates are pricing wrong for 2026.

This is not a "wedding planning 101" article. This is what I would tell a friend at the bar after two beers.

## The spreadsheet is the wedding plan

The number-one mistake I see is treating the budget spreadsheet like a financial document. It is not. It is the wedding plan.

Every decision you will make for the next nine months — every dress, every vendor, every guest, every flower — is downstream of one number: what you have to spend. And that number, divided into the right categories with the right margins, is the spreadsheet. The couple that opens a Google Sheet on the night they get engaged and types in a real budget with real categories is the couple that, twelve months later, is signing the venue contract with their hands still in the agreed-upon column.

The couple that doesn't is the couple I'm watching at the reception trying to figure out why the florist quote was double what they thought it would be.

> The spreadsheet is not a record. It is the rule. It is the document that decides what is in the wedding and what is not.

So before we talk about line items, agree on this: the budget spreadsheet you build is the first thing you build, and you build it before you book *anything*.

## The eight categories

Most templates I've seen online break a wedding budget into twelve or fifteen categories. That is too many. You will not maintain a fifteen-category spreadsheet for nine months. Nobody does. Three months in you will have stopped updating the "Stationery" tab because there are six line items on it and you can't remember what they were for.

I use eight. They are:

1. **Venue & catering** — the room, the food, the bar, the rentals the venue forces on you.
2. **Photography & video** — the photographer, the videographer, the prints, the album.
3. **Music & entertainment** — DJ, band, ceremony musicians, lighting if it's not in venue.
4. **Florals & decor** — bouquets, centerpieces, ceremony arch, aisle, signage decor.
5. **Attire & beauty** — dress, suit, alterations, shoes, jewelry, hair, makeup.
6. **Stationery & paper** — save-the-dates, invitations, programs, menus, signs, thank-yous.
7. **Cake & sweets** — the cake, dessert table, late-night snacks.
8. **The everything-else bucket** — officiant, marriage license, transportation, gifts for the wedding party, tips, the unexpected hundred-and-fifty-dollar charges that keep showing up.

That is the entire wedding. Eight rows in a sheet. Sub-rows under each one for the actual line items. You will know within forty-five seconds of opening the file where any given dollar belongs.

## The 2026 numbers most templates are pricing wrong

I want to be careful here because every wedding is different and I am not your local market. But I have watched the same line items get sticker-shocked the same way for three years running, and the templates from 2022 that are still floating around have not caught up. Here is where the math has shifted:

**Venue & catering: budget more.** The "$15,000 venue with all-inclusive catering" of 2022 is now $22,000 to $28,000 in most metros. Per-plate costs that used to be $85 are routinely $110 to $140. The templates that quote you "$75 per head" for 2026 are off by a category, not a percentage. If your guest count is 120, your venue and catering line should start at $25,000 and you should be pleasantly surprised if it comes in lower.

**Photography: hold steady, but lengthen the day.** Photographer day rates haven't moved as dramatically, but the *length of coverage* couples are booking has. The 6-hour package was standard in 2022; the 8-to-10-hour package is the new standard. Budget $4,000 to $7,000 for a photographer you'd actually hire, not the cheap ones you'll regret.

**Florals: the line that breaks budgets.** Florists raised prices through every supply-chain bump from 2022 through 2025 and have not lowered them. A "modest" floral package — bridal bouquet, six bridesmaids, ten centerpieces, ceremony arch, ten boutonnières — is $4,500 to $7,000 at the studios couples actually want to book. The under-$2,500 floral templates are a fantasy unless you're doing your own arrangements with grocery-store flowers, which some couples successfully do and you should know going in.

**Music & entertainment: this is my category, so I'll be direct.** A wedding DJ in a major metro who actually knows what they are doing — meaning they have done two hundred receptions, they own their own gear, they show up at 2 pm to set up, and they don't cancel three weeks out — is $1,800 to $3,500 in 2026. The $600 DJ from a directory is the one who shows up at 5:45 for a 6 pm ceremony with one busted speaker and a Spotify playlist. I have watched it happen.

**Stationery: more than you think.** Modern couples sticker-shock here because they think of "invitations" as one item. It is six items: save-the-dates, the invitation suite, RSVP cards, programs, menus, signage. Even at modest per-piece pricing, the full stationery line for a 120-person wedding is $1,200 to $2,200. Templates that line-item "$400 for invitations" are accounting for one of those six items.

**Cake & sweets: hold steady, but read the contract.** Cake pricing per slice has barely moved. What has moved is the *delivery fee*, the *cutting fee* (yes, that is a real thing), and the *late-night dessert table* couples are adding more often. Cake line: $400 to $900 for the cake itself, plus another $200 to $500 for late-night snacks if you're doing them.

## The two columns most spreadsheets are missing

When you open a typical wedding budget template, the columns are: *Item*, *Estimated*, *Actual*. That is the structure of a household expense tracker, not a wedding planning tool. It is missing two columns that the working version of the spreadsheet — the one that actually gets you to the wedding — needs.

The two missing columns are: **Deposit** and **Final due date**.

Every vendor on the wedding takes a deposit. The deposit is usually 25% to 50%, due at booking. The remainder is due somewhere between thirty days before the wedding and the day of, depending on the vendor. The couples who run out of cash three weeks before the wedding are the couples who did not track those final-due dates as a column. Their spreadsheet shows a perfectly balanced "Estimated vs Actual" — and then in the second week of May they get hit with $14,000 in final payments due across six vendors at once, and the credit card comes out.

Make those two columns part of the sheet from day one. Every vendor row gets a deposit (paid, with a date) and a final due (a calendar date in May or June or whenever). Sort the sheet by final-due-date in the last sixty days. That view alone is worth the entire spreadsheet.

## What gets cut at the last minute (it is always the same things)

After enough weddings you start to recognize the pattern. The couple set a budget. The budget creeps. Somewhere around the eight-week mark, they sit down with the spreadsheet on a Sunday morning and they cut. Almost always, the cuts come from the same four places:

1. **Floral scope.** Centerpieces shrink. The aisle decor gets reduced. The arch gets less floral. This is fine; it is the most flexible line on the wedding.
2. **Late-night snacks.** They get crossed off. The guests do not miss them as much as the couple thinks they will, although when they're there they are great.
3. **The favor table.** Often dropped entirely. Most favors get left on the table at the end of the night anyway.
4. **The videographer's add-ons.** The drone pack, the engagement session, the highlight film — they get pruned back to a single-deliverable package.

What rarely gets cut, and shouldn't: photography (you'll regret it forever), the DJ (you'll regret it that night), the food, the dress. Those are the four lines that are doing the heavy lifting of "what the wedding was like" — and the four lines couples instinctively protect when the spreadsheet starts pushing back.

If you build the spreadsheet right at the beginning, you can protect those four lines from day one and let the other lines flex naturally as the quotes come in. That is the whole game.

## Why I use Google Sheets for this and not an app

The wedding planning apps are pretty. They have beautiful charts and pie graphs and they auto-update with progress bars. I get why couples download them.

I do not use them, and I do not recommend them.

Two reasons. First, every vendor I work with sends quotes as PDFs, not as numbers I can plug into an app. The PDF gets emailed to the couple, the couple has to manually re-key the number into the app, and within ninety days the numbers in the app are six weeks out of date because nobody re-keyed the most recent revision. Google Sheets is faster — you open the sheet on your phone in the vendor's lobby and type the number in. No copy-paste from PDF, no app to launch.

Second, the apps lock you into their categories and their math. A spreadsheet lets you add a row whenever a new line item appears. (And new line items always appear. The day-of coordinator. The marriage license. The Uber to the rehearsal dinner. The hairpiece you didn't know you needed.)

A Google Sheet is the right tool because it is the tool that lets you change the shape of the document on the day you need to change it. An app does not let you do that.

## The minimum spreadsheet, written out

If you do nothing else this weekend, open a Google Sheet and put these columns at the top:

`Category` · `Item` · `Vendor` · `Estimated` · `Deposit paid` · `Deposit date` · `Final due` · `Final paid` · `Notes`

Then create eight section headers — the eight categories above — and stub in three to five line items under each one with your best-guess estimates. Total the bottom row. Compare to your max budget. Cry a little. Adjust.

You now have the wedding plan.

You will refine it for the next nine months. You will add rows you didn't know about. You will discover that the venue's "linens included" claim does not include the chair covers, and that's a $400 add-on row. You will swap the budget florist for the next-tier florist after you see the first proposal. The spreadsheet absorbs all of that. It is the document that grows with the wedding.

The couples who end the wedding day on budget are the couples who opened a sheet the week they got engaged and kept it open. The couples who don't are not less smart, less organized, or less in love. They just never built the document.
