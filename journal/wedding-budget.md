# Wedding budget guide: how to plan a wedding that stays under budget

*The four phases of a wedding budget that decide whether you finish at $42,000 like you planned or $58,000 like the credit card statement says — written from twenty-five years behind a DJ console, watching the same patterns repeat at five hundred weddings.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 6, 2026 · 14 min read**

---

I have stood behind a DJ console at somewhere north of five hundred weddings since 2001. The thing I've learned about wedding budgets from that angle — the angle nobody else gets to see, where you watch the entire production roll past for six hours — is that the couples who stay on budget and the couples who blow up their budget make their critical decisions in completely different orders.

The couples who stay on budget build the spreadsheet first and book the vendors second. They walk into every vendor consultation with a hard ceiling number written down before they walk in. They lock the guest list at month 4 and refuse to add anyone without removing someone else. They check the budget against committed dollars at month 6 and again at month 3, and they cut scope when the diagnostic fails.

The couples who blow up the budget do the opposite. They book the dress, the photographer, and the band in week 4 — before the spreadsheet exists. They let the guest list drift up by twenty-five from week 1 to month 8. They sign the venue contract based on the proposal summary, not the full fine-print, and they get surprised at month 3 by line items they didn't see during the tour. They run out of contingency in month 10 and put tips on a credit card the morning of the wedding.

**The difference between those two outcomes is not how much money the couple has. It is the order of the decisions.**

This page is the order of the decisions. It is the framework I would walk a friend through if they asked me — over two beers — how to think about a wedding budget. ## The four phases

A wedding budget happens in four phases. Couples who get the phases in order finish on budget. Couples who skip phases or do them out of order are the ones at the venue at 9:45 pm signing an overtime addendum on the back of a cocktail napkin.

1. **Phase 1: Set the number.** Week 1 of the engagement. Pick the total budget. Build the spreadsheet. Allocate by category. Set the contingency line.
2. **Phase 2: Anchor the big three.** Weeks 6 through 12. Venue + catering, photographer, and DJ-or-band. These three lines anchor 60 to 70 percent of the wedding. Lock them against your category ceilings.
3. **Phase 3: Spend within the spreadsheet.** Months 4 through 9. Every subsequent vendor decision — florals, attire, stationery, cake, transportation, day-of paper — gets made against the spreadsheet's line items. Walk into every consultation with the ceiling number written down before you walk in.
4. **Phase 4: Watch for the four blowout patterns.** Months 6, 3, and 1. Run the diagnostic. Catch guest count creep, the florist upsell, the venue's invisible line items, and the week-of overages before they hit.

Each of these phases has a long-form post on the site that goes deeper than this overview. Use this page as the map; use the posts for the territory.

## Phase 1 — Set the number

The first decision is the number.

In 2026, a wedding for 100 to 130 guests in a major US metro is going to land between $35,000 and $55,000. Coastal metros — Los Angeles, San Francisco, New York, Boston, DC, Miami — routinely cross $60,000 for the same guest count. Mid-tier metros — Phoenix, Charlotte, Nashville, Austin, Denver — sit at $30,000 to $45,000. Below that, you can have a real wedding for $20,000 to $28,000 in smaller metros if you're intentional about every vendor.

**The single most important thing to know about wedding budgets in 2026:** the widely quoted "$30,000 national average" is a 2022 number. Food, florals, and labor are up 20 to 40 percent since then. A couple planning against the 2022 average is planning against the wrong baseline by a full category. Walk into the venue tasting with the right 2026 number or you'll spend the next three months reverse-engineering the budget back up to the real cost.

For the by-category numbers, the deep dive is [Real Wedding Costs in 2026: What 500+ Weddings Actually Spent](/stikkas/journal/real-wedding-costs-2026/). That post gives you the hard 2026 ranges for venue and catering, photography, florals, music, attire, stationery, cake, and the everything-else bucket.

**The eight categories and their typical percentages:**

| Category | Percentage of budget |
|---|---|
| Venue + catering | 40 to 50% |
| Photography + video | 10 to 15% |
| Florals + decor | 10 to 15% |
| Music + entertainment | 8 to 12% |
| Attire + beauty | 5 to 10% |
| Stationery + signage | 2 to 5% |
| Cake + dessert | 1 to 3% |
| Everything else (license, officiant, transport, tips) | 5 to 10% |
| **+ Contingency** | **10%** |

These percentages hold across almost every wedding I've worked. What changes between a $30,000 wedding and a $60,000 wedding isn't the proportions. It's the total. The $60,000 wedding has more expensive everything, in roughly the same shape.

**Build the spreadsheet now.** Don't wait until you've toured venues. Don't wait until the dress shopping happens. Open a Google Sheet the week you get engaged, put your total budget at the top, allocate the eight categories with the percentages above, and add a 10 percent contingency line.

For the spreadsheet template itself — the nine columns I'd recommend, the deposits-vs-final-due structure, why "due-date" matters more than people think — the deep dive is [How to Build a Wedding Budget Spreadsheet (and the 2026 Numbers Most Templates Get Wrong)](/stikkas/journal/wedding-budget-spreadsheet/).

## Phase 2 — Anchor the big three

Three lines anchor the wedding: **venue + catering, photography, and music**. These three together are 60 to 70 percent of the total budget. Lock them first, against the category ceilings you set in phase 1, and everything downstream becomes solvable.

**Venue + catering** is the line that decides half the wedding. Get this one wrong and the budget is structurally broken. Get it right and the rest of the planning is execution against a real number.

The trap in venue + catering is **the difference between the proposal number and the all-in number**. The proposal summary the venue gives you on the tour is usually missing 15 to 25 percent of what the final invoice will say. Chair upgrades, cake-cutting fees, vendor meal counts, service-staff gratuity at 18 to 22 percent of food and beverage, overtime if you run past contract end time, corkage fees, signage stand rentals — none of these are usually in the proposal summary, and all of them appear on the final invoice. Before signing the venue contract, ask for a realistic example invoice from a wedding similar to yours, with names redacted. That number is the actual cost of getting married at this venue.

**Photography** is the next-largest fixed line. Working pros in major metros run $4,500 to $9,000 for an 8-hour day with engagement session, second shooter, and a digital gallery in 6 to 8 weeks. Below $3,500, you're usually getting a hobbyist or a portfolio-builder. Don't cut photography to save the day-of paper line. The day-of paper line is $300; the photography line is $5,000. You can never recover the photos.

**Music and entertainment** is my line. A working pro wedding DJ in a major US metro runs $2,500 to $5,500 for a 6-hour reception with ceremony, cocktail, and reception coverage. Below $1,500 is hobbyist territory and the microphone will hum on the toasts. The cheapest DJ in your market is almost certainly not the best one. A great DJ makes a $35,000 wedding feel like a $60,000 wedding, and a bad DJ makes the opposite true. This is the most leveraged service-vendor line in your budget — pick on combined signal of price, gut feel, and the questions they asked during the consult, not on price alone.

For the full music decision framework — the four music decisions you actually have to make versus the ones that belong to the DJ — see the parallel pillar at [/wedding-music/](/stikkas/wedding-music/).

**The week-4 rule:** Don't book any of the big three in the first four weeks of the engagement. That is the highest-emotion, lowest-discipline window in the planning timeline. Build the spreadsheet first, set the category ceilings, then walk into every big-three consultation in week 6 or later with the ceiling number written down. This single discipline is worth $3,000 to $8,000 in saved budget over the planning timeline.

## Phase 3 — Spend within the spreadsheet

Once the big three are anchored, every subsequent vendor decision gets made against the spreadsheet's line items.

The categories that come next:

- **Florals.** Walk into the consult with a pre-written yes-list — which upsells you'd accept, which you'd skip. The escort-card arrangement, yes. The bud vases on the bar, no. The petal toss, no. The sweetheart-table garland, yes. Decide before, defend during. Florists are good at the consult, which is why the consult eats the budget alive in $200 increments.
- **Attire.** Buy the dress in week 6 of planning, not week 4. Walk into the bridal salon with a hard ceiling number. The $3,500 dress at the ceiling number is a beautiful dress; the $6,000 dress one tier above is also a beautiful dress, but you can't unblow the budget after it's bought. Budget $400 to $1,200 for alterations as its own line — most couples underbudget this and find out late.
- **Stationery and day-of paper.** This is the line where a couple can save the most by being intentional. A $14.99 printable wedding planner kit plus 32 lb inkjet paper plus a home printer turns into the entire wedding's day-of paper — programs, menus, escort cards, signage — for under $80. The professionally printed version of the same set of paper goods is $800 to $2,500. The output looks identical at a 3-foot viewing distance. For the full home-printing setup — paper choice, color profiles, print settings, the three quiet mistakes that ruin the page — the deep dive is [How to Print a Wedding Planner That Actually Looks Good at Home](/stikkas/journal/print-wedding-planner-at-home/).
- **Cake and late-night snacks.** The cake gets right-sized to the budget almost automatically. The line that quietly gets cut is the late-night snacks line. It shouldn't be — the late-night pizza arrival at 10:30 pm is one of the strongest "best wedding I've been to" guest signals, for a $400-to-$1,500 line item. Defend this line.
- **Transportation.** Plan the getaway car, the bridal-party shuttle, and the guest shuttles for out-of-town guests before month 4. Day-of Uber surge pricing for forty guests round-trip is $1,200. A pre-booked shuttle is $900. The math is simple; the planning is what gets skipped.

**Track every committed dollar on the spreadsheet.** Deposit paid + final balance due. Both columns. Update the spreadsheet within 48 hours of every contract signature. Couples who finish on budget treat the spreadsheet as a living document; couples who run over treat it as a one-time exercise in week 2.

## Phase 4 — Watch for the four blowout patterns

By month 6, you can already see whether the budget is heading for a blowout. The four patterns I've watched repeat at almost every wedding are:

1. **Guest count creep** — the list grows by 10 to 25 between week 1 and final RSVP, adding $1,500 to $5,000 of unbudgeted cost.
2. **The florist consult upsell** — the original $5,500 floral package walks out of the consultation at $7,700.
3. **The venue's invisible line items** — chair upgrades, cake-cutting fees, vendor meals, service-staff gratuity, and overtime add 15 to 25 percent to the proposal-summary number.
4. **The week-of and day-of overages** — alteration overage, officiant honorarium upgrade, rehearsal-dinner overflow, day-of coordinator booked late, transportation gap, tips you didn't fully budget.

The full diagnostic — what each pattern looks like in real time, how to catch it at month 6 or month 3, and the three highest-leverage recovery moves when the diagnostic fails — is in [Where Couples Blow Their Wedding Budget: Four Patterns From 500 Receptions](/stikkas/journal/wedding-budget-blowouts/).

**Run the month-6 diagnostic:** sum every dollar committed to date (deposits + finals due) and compare to total budget. If you're already at 80 percent at month 6, you're heading for a blowout. The remaining 20 percent of unbooked vendors plus the late-add line items will run you 20 to 30 percent over.

**Run the month-3 diagnostic:** if you're at 95 percent at month 3, the wedding is on a credit card. Move budget around now or raise the budget cap and tell yourselves about it before the wedding week.

When the diagnostic fails, the three moves with five-figure savings potential:
1. **Cut guest count by 10 to 20** — $1,500 to $5,000.
2. **Move from premium open bar to beer-and-wine only** — $2,500 to $4,000.
3. **Cut floral installation scope** — $3,000 to $8,000 on lush budgets.

Every other cut is small. Those three are where the real money is.

## The contingency line — the single most important number on the spreadsheet

If you take one thing away from this page, take this: **build a 10 percent contingency line on the budget spreadsheet from week one.**

Not five percent. Five is a rounding error and disappears on the first vendor revision. Ten percent of $40,000 is $4,000 of explicit room for the line items that always show up in the final 60 days — rehearsal-dinner overflow, dress alteration overage, day-of coordinator booked late, weather tent that nobody planned for, tips you didn't fully budget.

Don't spend the contingency until month 9. If you make it to month 9 with the contingency intact, you've won — that's $4,000 of buffer for the actual surprises in the final stretch. If you build the budget with no contingency, the contingency is your credit card. That's fine if you have the credit, and terrible if you don't.

Every couple I've ever watched finish a wedding on budget had a contingency line. Every couple I've watched put tips on a credit card at the end of the night did not.

## Where the four phases live in the timeline

The phases overlay onto a wedding planning timeline like this:

| Timeline window | Phase | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| Week 1 to week 4 | Phase 1 | Set the total budget, allocate by category, build the spreadsheet, set the contingency line. **No vendor bookings yet.** |
| Week 6 to month 4 | Phase 2 | Book the big three: venue + catering, photography, music. Each booking happens against the category ceiling, not against the consultation upsell. |
| Month 4 to month 9 | Phase 3 | Book florals, attire, stationery, cake, transportation, day-of paper. Track every committed dollar on the spreadsheet within 48 hours. |
| Month 6 + month 3 | Phase 4 | Run the diagnostic. If committed dollars > 80% at month 6 or > 95% at month 3, cut scope or raise the cap. Watch for the four blowout patterns. |
| Month 9 to wedding day | Final | Defend the contingency line. Spend it on late-add line items as they appear. Pull cash for tips on Thursday. |

This is the framework. It's the order I would walk a friend through. It's also the order the wedding planning posts on this site are arranged in.

## The bottom line

Wedding budgets are not hard. They are just a series of decisions that go wrong in the same four phases at almost every wedding. Get the phases in order — set the number first, anchor the big three second, spend within the spreadsheet third, watch for the blowout patterns fourth — and you'll be in the small fraction of couples who finish the wedding under budget.

The couples who finish under budget are not the couples who spent the least. They are the couples who spent the most intentionally. The framework on this page is the difference between the two.
