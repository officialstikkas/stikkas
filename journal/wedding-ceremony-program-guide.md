# Wedding Ceremony Programs: What to Include, How to Print, and When to Skip Them

Your guests are seated. The music starts. And someone leans over to whisper: *"What order does this go in? Who's the officiant?"*

A ceremony program answers all of that before the question gets asked.

Done right, it's a keepsake. Done wrong, it's a piece of paper that ends up under someone's chair. Here's how to do it right.

---

## Do You Actually Need a Ceremony Program?

Short answer: it depends on your ceremony length and complexity.

**You probably need one if:**
- Your ceremony is longer than 25 minutes
- You have multiple readings, rituals, or cultural traditions guests won't recognize
- You have a large wedding party guests don't all know by name
- You want to acknowledge people (parents, passed loved ones, vendors)

**You can skip it if:**
- Your ceremony is under 20 minutes, civil, minimal
- Your guest list is mostly close family who already know everyone
- Your venue has a screens or projected program (some do)

If you're on the fence, go with a simple bi-fold. It takes 20 minutes to set up and gives guests something to hold during "Love is patient, love is kind" when they've already heard it three times.

## What Goes in a Wedding Ceremony Program

Here's what actually belongs in a program — from a 25-year wedding DJ and event professional who has seen every version of this:

### The Front Cover
- Bride + groom's names (or both first names, however you're framing it)
- Wedding date
- Venue name (optional but grounding)
- A line, illustration, or design that matches your wedding aesthetic

Keep it clean. The cover is the first visual impression. White space is your friend.

### Order of Ceremony

This is the meat of the program. List each moment in sequence:

1. Processional
2. Welcome / Call to Order
3. Opening Words by Officiant
4. Reading(s) — include the reader's name and the reading title or source
5. Ring Exchange / Vows
6. Pronouncement
7. First Kiss
8. Recessional

Add or subtract based on your actual ceremony. Unity candles, sand ceremonies, cultural rituals, musical performances — list them all with a one-line description if guests won't know what they're looking at.

### The Wedding Party
- Officiant
- Maid of Honor / Man of Honor
- Best Man / Best Woman
- Bridesmaids (with relationship — "sister of the bride" reads warmer than a bare name)
- Groomsmen
- Flower girl, ring bearer
- Parents of the couple (especially if escorting or seated in ceremony)

Don't overthink the format. One column, simple, reads clean.

### Back Page — Optional but Good

**Thank-you note.** One paragraph. Thank guests for being there. Mention anyone you want to honor — a late parent, a grandparent, someone who helped make the wedding happen. This is the page guests actually read during the ceremony.

Optional additions:
- Reception info / venue address if different
- QR code to wedding website
- "In memory of" acknowledgment

## How to Format a Ceremony Program

The classic format is a **bi-fold** printed on one 8.5" × 11" sheet of paper (landscape orientation), folded to create a 4-panel 5.5" × 8.5" booklet:

- **Front:** Cover
- **Inside left:** Order of Ceremony
- **Inside right:** Wedding Party
- **Back:** Thank-you note

This format prints on a single sheet, cuts your print cost in half, and folds in seconds.

## Printing Tips (From Someone Who's Done This Professionally)

**Paper stock matters.** Don't print programs on copy paper. Use at least 60–80 lb text weight, or 80 lb cardstock for a stiffer result. At a local print shop, this is a $0.10–0.20 per sheet upgrade that makes a visible difference in how the final product feels.

**Laser beats inkjet for this.** Inkjet can smudge if guests' hands are slightly damp (receptions, outdoor ceremonies in humidity). Laser-printed text stays crisp.

**Print a test fold first.** Before printing 150 copies, print one, fold it, and check that the panels line up correctly. Alignment errors that look tiny on screen get magnified in the fold.

**How many to print:** Print 70–80% of your guest count. Some guests share, some won't pick one up, some will take two. Printing 100% of headcount for a 200-person wedding leaves you with 40 programs on a table.

## Skip the Envelope

Some programs come with an envelope or ribbon wrap. For most weddings: skip it. It adds cost, time, and a thing guests have to undo before they can read anything. The program itself is the delivery mechanism — the wrapping is friction.

---

## What Most Ceremony Programs Get Wrong

**Too much text.** A program is a guide, not a novel. If your descriptions run three sentences, cut to one. Guests are looking up at the ceremony, not studying the program.

**Missing the wedding party's relationships.** "Sarah Johnson" means nothing to half the room. "Childhood best friend of the bride" or "groom's brother" does.

**No back page.** The back is prime real estate. Don't leave it blank when you could use it for a genuine thank-you — guests read it while they're waiting, and it leaves an impression.

**Printing too late.** Programs should be done 5 days before the wedding. Not the night before. If your printer has a line or your home printer runs out of ink, you need that buffer.
