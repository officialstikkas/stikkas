# The wedding day emergency kit that actually saves the day

*Six categories, the items that get used every wedding, the items that look smart on Pinterest but never get touched, and the disasters they actually prevent.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 8, 2026 · 10 min read**

---

Twenty-five years of weddings have given me a strange education in emergency kits. From the DJ booth I don't carry the kit — but I see it in action all day. The maid of honor opens it in the bridal suite when the bride catches her heel on the dress. The mother of the bride opens it in the dining room when somebody spills red wine on the tablecloth. The day-of coordinator opens it in the bridal suite at 8:30 pm when a bridesmaid needs Advil and the bride needs her lipstick.

I have also seen what is missing from kits. The Pinterest-perfect emergency kit on most wedding blogs is a beautiful eighty-item rainbow of supplies organized by color in a clear vinyl pouch. About twelve of those items get used. The other sixty-eight items take up space and weight in a bag that has to move from the bridal suite to the ceremony space to the head table over the course of a day.

This is the field version. Six categories, the items that actually get used, and the specific disasters each item prevents. If your kit has more than about thirty-five items in it, you are carrying extra weight that nobody will touch. If it has less than twenty, you are exposed.

## The structure — six categories, one bag

Build the kit in a clear-pocket toiletry bag, a soft-sided cosmetics organizer, or a small fishing-tackle box. The container has to let you find an item by sight in under five seconds. An opaque bag means searching by feel while the bride waits — exactly wrong.

The six categories are: **fashion fixes, beauty touch-ups, comfort and pain, tech and paperwork, bride-specific, and the disaster items most people forget.** Each one solves a different family of problems. Pack a few items from each category rather than over-stuffing one and skipping another.

## Category 1 — Fashion fixes (the actual lifesavers)

This is the category that earns the kit its keep. About 40 percent of the items in this category will get used at the average wedding. The other 60 percent are there for the one wedding where the dress fails badly.

- **Safety pins, mixed sizes (10–15)** — fix a torn hem, a broken bra strap, a button that pops off the groom's vest, a flower-girl dress that lost a sash. The single most-used item in any wedding kit.
- **Double-sided fashion tape (one full roll)** — keeps necklines from gaping, holds the bride's veil in place when the wind picks up at an outdoor venue, secures the boutonniere ribbon. Use Hollywood Fashion Tape or similar — the cheaper stuff loses its stick after twenty minutes.
- **Hem tape (one full roll, fusible)** — for the dress hem that comes undone during the recessional. Requires an iron to set, but the venue almost always has one in the bridal suite. The iron is in the bridal suite for a reason — the dress will need it.
- **Mini sewing kit** — six colors of thread minimum, two needles, a small pair of scissors. The kit gets opened maybe one wedding in four; when it does, it saves the day.
- **Lint roller (full-size, not travel-size)** — the travel-size lint roller covers about ten passes before it loads up. The full-size handles the whole bridal party.
- **A spare pair of stockings in the bride's exact shade** — runs in stockings happen during the prep, the ceremony, or the first dance. Without a backup the bride walks the rest of the night with a visible ladder.

## Category 2 — Beauty touch-ups (the second-most-used category)

Touch-ups are the constant. Every formal photo session, every dance break, every transition between blocks, somebody in the bridal party wants to touch up.

- **The bride's lipstick — the exact tube she wore for the ceremony** — pack the actual lipstick, not a similar shade. Photographers will ask the bride to reapply between the ceremony and the formal portraits, and again before the grand entrance. Wrong shade is visible in photos.
- **Pressed-powder compact in the bride's shade** — for shine reduction under photo lights and in summer venues.
- **Blotting papers (one full pack)** — faster than powder, doesn't show on cameras, kills shine instantly. The makeup artist will tell you this is more important than the powder.
- **Travel-size hairspray** — for the curl that falls out on the dance floor or the hairpiece that starts shifting after the third hour.
- **Bobby pins (a generous fistful)** — the hairstylist will use about fifteen during the morning prep. Another five to ten will get used during the day for touch-ups.
- **Brush or detangling comb** — for the wind-blown hair after the outdoor ceremony.
- **Mini deodorant (one roll-on, one spray)** — the spray is for the bridesmaid dresses; the roll-on is for the people. Both get used.
- **Mints (one tin)** — for after dinner, before the first dance, before the formal toasts. The DJ booth keeps its own tin for the same reasons.

## Category 3 — Comfort and pain (the quiet medic kit)

Somebody at every wedding gets a headache, a blister, a small cut, or a stomach issue. This category is the medic.

- **Advil or Ibuprofen (10–20 tablets, in original packaging)** — used at every wedding. Always. Bridal-party member, mother of the groom, a vendor who skipped lunch — somebody needs Advil.
- **Tylenol (a smaller backup pack)** — for the person who can't take ibuprofen.
- **Tums (one roll)** — for the rehearsal dinner that disagreed with the bride.
- **Band-Aids in two sizes (10 total)** — for the cut finger, the new-shoe blister, the broken-glass shard in the bridal suite.
- **Blister patches (Compeed or generic)** — different product from a Band-Aid; specifically designed for shoe blisters. A new heel will produce a blister within an hour. The patch is the difference between a comfortable bride and a limping one.
- **Tampons and panty liners** — non-negotiable. The wedding day does not care about cycles. Carry a small supply for the bride and bridesmaids.
- **Eye drops (small bottle)** — for contact-lens wearers, for the bride who has been crying happy-tears all morning, for the smoke from sparkler exits.
- **Tide stain pen** — the catering staff has bigger stain solutions, but the Tide pen handles the small one that's about to be photographed.
- **Travel-size tissues (two packs)** — one for the bride's bag, one for the kit. Both get emptied.

## Category 4 — Tech and paperwork (the underrated category)

This is the category most kits skip and most weddings need. Tech is now central to a wedding day — the photographer texts, the planner coordinates, the DJ takes requests, the videographer syncs with the photographer.

- **The bride's exact phone-charger cable** — not a Lightning if she's USB-C. Not USB-C if she's Lightning. The specific one. Six dollars at any drugstore.
- **A backup phone-charger cable for the groom's phone** — different connector if the couple has different phones. Both phones will need charging during the day.
- **A small battery pack with one full charge** — Anker, RAVPower, or similar. Brand name matters; cheap battery packs fail at 30 percent.
- **A USB wall plug** — most venues have outlets but not always a plug head.
- **The vendor contact sheet (printed)** — phone numbers for every vendor. When the cake delivery is late and the venue's wifi is down, the printed sheet is what you reach for.
- **The day-of timeline (printed)** — the binder version, with everyone's arrival times and the cake-cut moment and the last-song.
- **The marriage license** — if it goes home with the couple after the ceremony, the officiant needs it to sign. I have seen a wedding delay fifteen minutes because the license was in the bride's suite and the officiant was at the altar.
- **A small notepad and two pens** — for the day-of coordinator's running notes and the maid of honor's "tell the bride later" reminders.

## Category 5 — Bride-specific (the personalized layer)

Every bride needs different things here. The categories above are universal; this one is custom.

- **A snack bar (one or two)** — the bride often skips breakfast. By 1 pm she will need food and won't want to eat in front of the photographer. A protein bar in the bridal suite is the fix.
- **A water bottle with a straw** — the straw protects the lipstick. The closed bottle protects the dress. Pack a 24-oz reusable with a flip straw, refill throughout the day.
- **The bride's specific allergy medications or daily prescriptions** — Zyrtec, an inhaler, anything she usually takes. The wedding is not the day to skip a daily medication.
- **A backup pair of flats in her exact size** — the heels are for the ceremony, the formals, and the grand entrance. The flats are for the dance floor by 9:30 pm. Most brides who don't pack flats end up barefoot on the dance floor.
- **The bride's contact lens case and saline** — if she wears contacts, both spare lenses and a backup pair of glasses.
- **A small mirror** — for touch-ups outside the bridal suite.
- **The garter (if relevant)** — packed in the kit because it is the single most-forgotten accessory.

## Category 6 — The disaster items most people forget

This is the category that separates an okay kit from a great one. Each of these items solves a specific problem most kits never account for.

- **White chalk** — for the last-minute mark on the white wedding dress that nobody can wash out before the ceremony. Rubbed gently into the mark, the chalk hides the stain for the rest of the day. Photographs invisibly. A two-dollar stick of chalk has saved more weddings than I can count.
- **Clear nail polish** — applied to the start of a run in stockings, it stops the run from spreading. Also seals the end of a frayed thread. Drugstore item; non-negotiable.
- **Baby powder** — for a grease stain on a white dress, pat baby powder on, let it absorb for ten minutes, brush off. Removes the grease and the visible stain. Also works for makeup on the dress.
- **A small pair of nail clippers** — for the broken nail, the loose thread, the price tag still on the groom's new shoes.
- **A Sharpie in black** — for last-minute changes to the seating chart, signage that needs an addition, name cards that have a typo.
- **Krazy Glue (one tube)** — for the broken shoe heel, the loose stone on the bride's earring, the cake topper that lost its base.
- **A small flashlight** — for the venue with bad lighting in the bridal suite, the search for a dropped earring on the floor, the walk back to the reception area after the sparkler exit.
- **Earring backs (a card of 20)** — earring backs fall off constantly. The bridal party's earrings, the mother of the bride's earrings, the bride's earrings. A card of twenty solves the problem in advance.
- **A small folding scissors (not just the sewing-kit scissors)** — for the dress tag that wasn't removed, the ribbon on a bouquet, the price label on the new groom's shoes.

## What I'd skip — the over-packed kit problems

The Pinterest emergency kits include items that almost never get used at real weddings.

- **A full sewing machine kit** — no. A mini sewing kit covers it. Nobody is going to operate a sewing machine in the bridal suite.
- **Multiple shades of foundation makeup** — the makeup artist handled this in the morning prep. Touch-ups are powder, lipstick, and blotting papers.
- **A full first-aid kit** — overkill. The medic items in Category 3 cover what actually happens at a wedding.
- **A steamer** — every venue's bridal suite has one. Don't carry your own.
- **A backup wedding dress option** — I have actually seen this on a Pinterest list. If your dress fails the answer is alterations, not changing dresses two hours before the ceremony.
- **Spare wedding bands** — no. The rings live with the best man until the ceremony, then with the couple. There is no scenario where a backup ring helps.

## How to actually build the kit

Two weeks before the wedding, list the items by category on paper. One week before, buy what you don't have — most items are drugstore stops, total cost about $40-$60 for a complete kit. Three days before, pack it into the clear-pocket bag. The night before, do one final scan: phone charger for the bride, phone charger for the groom, the marriage license, the lipstick of record, the comfort flats. Those five items are the most-forgotten of the most-needed.

At the rehearsal dinner, hand the kit to the maid of honor. She carries it to the venue the next morning. The kit lives in the bridal suite during prep, moves to the ceremony space in a bag that stays with the bridal party, and ends up at the head table during the reception. If you hired a day-of coordinator, mention the kit at the morning meeting — the coordinator's parallel kit will cover gaps in yours.

Most weddings will use about a dozen items from the kit. One wedding in four will use the safety pins, the hem tape, the chalk, or the nail polish in a way that visibly saves the day. The kit costs sixty dollars and an hour to assemble. That is the cheapest insurance in the wedding budget.
