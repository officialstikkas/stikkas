# Wedding day timeline template

*Why Google Sheets beats every other format — and the eight columns the working version needs.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 12, 2026 · 9 min read**

---

About once a month a couple sends me their wedding day timeline as a Word document and asks if anything looks off. I open it on my laptop at the DJ console — usually the Tuesday before their Saturday wedding — and within forty-five seconds I can tell them three things that are going to break.

The timeline is one of the most important documents on a wedding. It is the difference between a coordinator who is running the room and a coordinator who is running behind the room. It is the difference between a DJ who knows exactly when to call the toasts and a DJ who is waiting in the booth wondering why the bride is still doing photos. It is the document the photographer prints out and tapes to the back of their camera bag.

And nine times out of ten it is in the wrong format.

I want to talk about why the wedding day timeline belongs in a Google Sheet — not a Word document, not a Notes thread, not a screenshot the maid of honor took from the planner. I want to walk through the eight columns the working version of this document needs. And I want to explain why the timeline that gets you to a wedding that starts on time at six is built six weeks out and shared with eight people, not six days out and printed by one person.

This is what I would tell a friend at the bar after two beers — the timeline edition.

## The timeline is a multi-person document

Here is the first thing nobody tells you. The wedding day timeline does not belong to one person. It belongs to eight.

The bride and groom need it because it is *their* day. The coordinator needs it because they are running the room. The DJ needs it because every announcement and every song cue is keyed to a timeline block. The photographer needs it because their entire shot list is built backwards from ceremony, first look, and golden-hour windows. The videographer needs it because they are recording the room and need to know where to be when. The venue manager needs it because they are managing room flips, the kitchen, and the bar. The maid of honor and best man need it because they are answering wedding-party questions all day long and they cannot keep texting the bride.

That is eight people who each need a copy of the timeline forty-eight hours before the wedding. Six of them need a printed version in their hand on the day.

A document that needs to live in eight people's hands cannot be a Word file. Word files are single-author documents. The bride writes one, emails it to the coordinator, the coordinator changes the cocktail-hour timing, emails it back, the bride forgets to re-email the DJ, and the DJ shows up Saturday with a version that has the cocktail hour ending fifteen minutes earlier than reality. I have seen this exact thing happen at least twenty times.

The timeline is a *collaborative* document. The only format built for collaboration is a spreadsheet.

## Why a spreadsheet specifically (and not a PDF)

I will say something controversial here: **the wedding day timeline should not be a PDF until the last 48 hours.**

PDFs are for finished documents. The wedding day timeline is not finished until two days before the wedding. Up until that point it is a living document — the photographer changes their arrival time by twenty minutes, the caterer pushes the buffet open by fifteen, the officiant moves the rehearsal to a different day. Every one of those changes has to ripple through the timeline. A PDF cannot ripple.

A spreadsheet can.

In a Google Sheet, when the photographer texts you at 9 pm on a Tuesday saying "I'm going to arrive at 2:30 instead of 2:00," you open the sheet on your phone, change the cell, and seven other people see the change automatically. There is no email chain. There is no "wait which version is current." There is no question about whether the DJ has the latest copy.

Forty-eight hours before the wedding, you do the conversion. Export the Google Sheet to PDF. Print eight copies. Distribute. From that moment forward the PDF is the source of truth — but only because everyone agreed at hour T-48 to stop editing.

The right pattern is: spreadsheet for the nine weeks of timeline-building, PDF for the final 48 hours.

## The eight columns

Most wedding day timeline templates I've seen online have three columns: *Time*, *Event*, *Notes*. That is the template version. The working version — the one that actually gets the wedding through the day — needs eight.

Here they are.

**1. Start time.** Use 24-hour military time. I know this sounds excessive, but at 9:00 pm versus 21:00 the eye does not have to interpret AM/PM and you cut every ambiguity. The wedding industry runs on 24-hour time. The DJ does. The photographer does. The caterer does. Match the vendor convention.

**2. End time.** This is the column most templates miss. Every block has an end time, not just a start. The cocktail hour starts at 5:00 and ends at 6:00 — both numbers matter. The toasts start at 7:45 and end at 8:00 — both numbers matter. Without end times you cannot calculate buffer.

**3. Event.** What is happening in this block. *Ceremony processional. First look. Cocktail hour. Reception entrance. Cake cut.* Use the language the vendors use. Do not write "Wedding party walks in" when "Reception entrance" is the term every DJ and venue manager already knows.

**4. Location.** Where is this happening. Important when the wedding moves rooms — ceremony room, cocktail patio, ballroom, parking lot for the sparkler send-off. The coordinator needs to know where to be for every block. The photographer needs to pre-position. Skip this column and you get the photographer arriving at the wrong room for the first dance.

**5. Lead.** Who is in charge of this block. Most blocks have a clear owner: the officiant runs the ceremony, the DJ runs the reception entrance, the coordinator runs everything in between, the venue runs the buffet open. Writing the lead in a column means there is no "who's calling this" hesitation on the day. If you put a name in this column for every row, the timeline collapses into a clean question: at any moment, who is currently running the room?

**6. Notes.** The general notes column every template has. Use it for things like "Bride wants flute of champagne ready at 4:55" or "Photographer needs ten minutes alone with the wedding party between blocks 8 and 9."

**7. Vendor cue.** This is the column that turns a timeline into a working DJ document. The vendor cue is what the DJ, the photographer, or the videographer specifically needs to know to do their job at this block. *Cue first dance song "At Last" — Etta James version, not the cover.* *Photographer: get aisle exit shot before grandparents stand.* *Videographer: B-cam on parents' table during the toast.* Without this column the timeline is descriptive. With it the timeline is operational.

**8. Buffer minutes.** Built into every transition: five minutes between major blocks, ten to fifteen between the big ones (ceremony-to-cocktail, dinner-to-toasts, toasts-to-first-dance). The buffer column is the column that makes the timeline survive the actual day. Without it every overrun compounds. With it, the photographer running ten minutes long on family photos does not cascade into a 9 pm dinner. The buffer absorbs it.

Eight columns. That is the entire working timeline.

## What goes wrong without those last two columns

I want to stop and underline something. The two columns most templates miss are **vendor cue** and **buffer minutes**. They are the columns that turn a timeline from "list of times" into "document the wedding actually runs on."

Without vendor cue, the DJ shows up at the booth Saturday at 5 pm with a one-line entry that reads "6:30: First dance." That is descriptive but not operational. The DJ does not know which song. The DJ does not know if the bride wants the radio edit or the full version. The DJ does not know if there is a special microphone moment before the song or if it goes straight in. So the DJ has to find the bride at 6:25 — twenty minutes before the first dance — to confirm. The bride is taking photos. The maid of honor does not know. The coordinator is on the phone with the cake vendor. The first dance starts late and the song is wrong.

Vendor cue prevents that entire chain.

Without buffer minutes, every block is allocated to the minute. *Ceremony processional 5:00–5:15.* *Cocktail hour 5:15–6:15.* *Reception entrance 6:15.* That looks clean on paper. But the processional runs eight minutes long because the flower girl froze. The cocktail hour now starts at 5:23 — which means it ends at 6:23 — which means the reception entrance is now at 6:23, not 6:15. And the DJ has been standing at the microphone since 6:15 wondering whether to make the announcement.

With buffer built in — say five minutes between ceremony and cocktails, ten between cocktails and entrance — the eight-minute overrun gets absorbed and the reception entrance still happens at 6:15. The timeline is not brittle. It bends without breaking.

Both of those columns are non-obvious. Both of them are why the wedding starts on time.

## When to build it

Six to eight weeks before the wedding is the right window.

Earlier than that and you are writing fiction. The photographer has not confirmed exact arrival. The caterer has not committed to buffet timing. The DJ does not know the bridal party intro order because the bride is still deciding who is bringing the dog down the aisle. Filling in a timeline at twelve weeks out is mostly making up numbers that you will erase later.

Later than that — say two weeks out — and you do not have time to circulate the document for vendor review. Every vendor needs at least seven days with the timeline to flag conflicts. The photographer is going to say "I cannot do family photos in fifteen minutes; that is forty-five minutes minimum." The DJ is going to say "You cannot do toasts before dinner is served; the room is not seated yet." The caterer is going to say "We need twenty minutes to flip the room between ceremony and reception." All of those notes come in *during the review window* — which is week six to week three, not the Tuesday before the wedding.

Build the timeline in week six. Circulate to vendors in week five. Final revision in week three. Lock to PDF in week one. That is the workflow.

## Who gets a copy

Eight people, minimum, two days before the wedding. The day-of distribution:

- **Coordinator** — runs the room, holds the master copy
- **DJ** — keeps a copy at the console in a clear sleeve, checks it every ten minutes
- **Photographer** — has a printed copy in their camera bag; references during family photos and golden-hour windows
- **Videographer** — has a copy on their cart or in their phone
- **Venue manager** — runs the kitchen and bar against it
- **Maid of honor** — keeps a copy in the bridal suite for the wedding party
- **Best man** — keeps a copy for the groom and groomsmen
- **Officiant** — references for ceremony block timing

The DJ copy specifically is the working copy. I keep mine taped to the inside of a clear plastic sleeve next to the laptop. Every announcement I make is keyed to a row on the sheet. When the room is moving fast — toasts running into cake cut into parent dances — that sheet is the only thing keeping me on cue.

If a vendor is not getting a copy, you are gambling on that vendor improvising their part of the day. Some vendors are good at improvising. Most are not. Most are professionals who are great when they know what is happening and adequate when they are guessing.

Give every vendor a copy.

## The honest answer about Excel and Numbers

I get asked this enough that I should address it directly. *Can I use Excel? Can I use Numbers?*

You can. You lose one thing: real-time multi-person sync.

Excel can technically be shared via OneDrive but the wedding industry runs in Google. Every coordinator I have worked with has a Gmail address. Every DJ does. Every photographer does. The path of least resistance is the format every vendor already lives in — and that is Google Sheets.

Apple Numbers is worse for this specifically. It does not have a clean share-and-collaborate experience for non-Apple users. The DJ on an Android phone cannot easily edit your Numbers file. The coordinator on a Windows laptop will struggle to open it. Numbers is fine as a personal spreadsheet; it is not fine as a multi-vendor wedding document.

If you start in Excel, plan to convert to a Google Sheet the day you share it with vendors. If you start in Numbers, just save yourself the migration and start in Sheets.

## The eight-people, eight-column rule

This entire article boils down to two numbers. **Eight people, eight columns.** Eight people who each need a copy of the timeline forty-eight hours before the wedding. Eight columns the working version of the document needs — start, end, event, location, lead, notes, vendor cue, buffer.

That is the wedding day timeline.

I have watched five hundred weddings from the DJ console. I have seen the timeline get the wedding through the day, and I have seen the wedding survive *despite* the timeline because the document did not have the right columns or did not get to the right people. The pattern is consistent. The weddings that hum are the weddings where the timeline was built six weeks out, lived in a Google Sheet for five weeks, got reviewed by every vendor in week three, and locked to PDF in week one. The weddings that drag and run late are the weddings where the timeline lived in one person's Word document until Thursday.

Build it in a sheet. Share it with eight people. Use the eight columns. Lock to PDF at T-48. That is the template.
