# Wedding do-not-play list: what 25 years behind the booth taught me to skip

*A working wedding DJ on the document that protects your reception from the songs that will quietly ruin it.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 5, 2026 · 10 min read**

---

The most important music document at a wedding is not the playlist. It is not the must-play list. It is the do-not-play list.

I have been the DJ at over five hundred weddings since 2001. In most of them there was a do-not-play list. In most of those, the list was three or four lines long and written in a hurry the week of the wedding. In the weddings where the list was thoughtful — written in advance, signed by both partners, talked through with the DJ — the music landed differently. The room felt curated, not assembled. The floor stayed full. The couple got to dance to songs they actually loved, in front of guests who were not waiting through three slow songs to get to the one they came for.

A do-not-play list is not a list of songs you mildly dislike. It is the document that protects the reception from the three things that quietly ruin wedding music: personal landmines, style mismatches, and the cliché traps that every DJ defaults to when they run out of ideas.

This is what to put on it, what not to put on it, and how to actually use it on the wedding day.

## What a do-not-play list actually is

The do-not-play list is the only place where the couple can override the DJ in real time without saying anything during the reception.

Here is how it works at a real wedding. It is 9:32 pm. The floor is full. The DJ is reading the room. An uncle leans into the booth and says, "Hey, can you play 'Sweet Caroline'? The whole family loves it." If "Sweet Caroline" is not on the do-not-play list, the DJ has a decision to make in three seconds — and the easy decision, the one that makes the uncle happy and the DJ's job easier, is to play "Sweet Caroline." If "Sweet Caroline" is on the do-not-play list, the DJ has no decision to make. "I'd love to, but the couple specifically asked me not to play that one tonight — sorry." The uncle nods, goes back to the table, and the DJ plays the song they had already cued up.

The do-not-play list is the document that lets the DJ be the bad guy on your behalf. Without it, every uncle, every cousin, every wedding-party member who has had three drinks is making music decisions for you. With it, the DJ holds the line.

## The three categories of do-not-play

Every entry on a do-not-play list falls into one of three categories. If an entry doesn't fit one of these, ask yourself if it actually needs to be on the list.

**Category one: personal.** Songs that are off-limits for private reasons that you do not need to explain to anyone. An ex's wedding song. A song from a parent's funeral. A song from a relationship that ended badly. A song from a chapter of your life you do not want to revisit at your wedding. Two notes here. One: you do not have to explain these to the DJ. "Please do not play 'Wagon Wheel' under any circumstances" is a complete sentence. Two: these are non-negotiable. A great DJ treats personal-category items as inviolable — they are not playing those songs even if your own father walks up and requests one.

**Category two: style.** Genres, artists, or sub-genres you do not want played at your wedding. "No country" is a style ban. "No metal" is a style ban. "No 80s hair-band power ballads" is a style ban. "No explicit hip-hop" is a style ban. Style bans are legitimate and the DJ will work around them. Be specific about what counts — "no country" might mean "no twangy traditional country" to you and "no Taylor Swift" to a strict interpretation, so spell it out.

**Category three: cliché.** The overplayed wedding songs that every DJ has in their back pocket and that you personally cannot stand. These are the songs that exist because they reliably get a wedding floor moving — "Cha Cha Slide," "Cupid Shuffle," "Wobble," "Macarena," "YMCA," "Chicken Dance," "Dancing Queen," "Don't Stop Believin'." There is no shame in banning these. There is also no shame in keeping a couple of them. Just know what you're doing — these are the songs the DJ uses to drag back the medium-engaged dancers. If you ban all of them, you are giving up a floor-filler tool.

## The songs that almost always show up on the list

This is the list I see most often. Some of these are universally hated, some are universally loved, and most are universally polarizing.

**"Cha Cha Slide" — DJ Casper.** Half of couples love it for getting the kids and the older relatives on the floor. The other half cannot stand it. It is the single most-banned and the single most-played wedding song. Make a decision.

**"Cupid Shuffle" — Cupid.** Same category. Same decision.

**"Wobble" — V.I.C.** Same category. Slightly more dated than the other two, but still pulls the floor at the right wedding.

**"YMCA" — Village People.** Universally recognized. Lands hard at weddings with a lot of guests over 50. Reads as dated and corny at weddings with a guest list under 35. If your wedding skews young, ban it.

**"Macarena" — Los del Río.** Almost always on the do-not-play list now. The DJ default is to leave it off the playlist unless explicitly requested.

**"Chicken Dance" — various.** Often on the do-not-play list. If grandparents are present and the couple has a sense of humor about it, keep it for one play in the middle of the night.

**"Sweet Caroline" — Neil Diamond.** Polarizing. Some guest lists go feral for it. Some couples find it unbearable. If you find it unbearable, ban it explicitly — every guest list has at least one person who will request it.

**"Don't Stop Believin'" — Journey.** Reliable floor-filler. Universally known. The most overplayed song in wedding history along with "Sweet Caroline." Ban it if you personally hate it. Otherwise leave it in.

**"Wonderwall" — Oasis.** Surprisingly often on the do-not-play list, usually because one partner hates Oasis or finds the song associated with college house parties. If your partner hates it, ban it.

**"Friends in Low Places" — Garth Brooks.** Always on the list at non-country weddings. Stays in heavy rotation at country weddings.

**"Cotton Eye Joe" — Rednex.** Almost always banned. The DJ default is "don't play unless explicitly requested by the couple in writing."

**"We Are Family" — Sister Sledge.** Surprisingly often banned by couples who want to avoid the overly sentimental moment. The DJ default is to play it during the family dance segment, but skip it if banned.

**"Wagon Wheel" — Old Crow Medicine Show / Darius Rucker.** Polarizing. Sometimes a must-play, sometimes a do-not-play, almost never neutral.

**"Closing Time" — Semisonic.** Often used as the last song of the night. If you don't want the last song to be "Closing Time," put it on the do-not-play list — otherwise the DJ may default to it.

**Anything explicit.** Most working DJs default to clean versions at weddings, but state this in writing anyway. "No explicit versions of any song" is a one-line catch-all that handles a hundred edge cases.

**Anything by an ex's favorite artist.** This is personal-category. Be specific — "no Drake" or "no Ed Sheeran" — and the DJ will work around it.

## What not to put on the list

The do-not-play list works because it is short, specific, and meaningful. Couples ruin their own list by treating it as a venting document for every song they have ever mildly disliked.

**Don't ban entire decades.** "No 80s music" is too broad. It removes "Sweet Child o' Mine," "Take On Me," "Africa," "Don't Stop Believin'," "Livin' on a Prayer," "Like a Prayer," "Time After Time," "Billie Jean," and a thousand other songs the floor will love. If you genuinely hate 80s music, ban specific songs. If what you actually mean is "no cheesy 80s power ballads," say that.

**Don't ban songs you've never heard but heard are bad.** I have had couples ban songs they could not name — "you know that one annoying wedding song, the one with the dance" — which is functionally not a ban at all. If you cannot tell the DJ the title and the artist, you have not actually banned anything.

**Don't ban songs your partner loves to prove a point.** Marriage is long. Wedding music is one night. Pick the battles that matter.

**Don't ban every song your DJ might use to fill a floor.** "Cha Cha Slide," "Cupid Shuffle," "Wobble," "YMCA," "Macarena," "Don't Stop Believin'," "Sweet Caroline," "Mr. Brightside," "I Wanna Dance with Somebody," "September." These are the workhorses of a wedding dance floor. Banning all of them at once leaves the DJ with no reliable floor-fillers. Pick the two or three that you genuinely cannot tolerate and leave the rest.

## The list is a document, not a conversation

Here is the most common mistake I see. A couple has a do-not-play list in their head. They mention three of the songs to the DJ on the phone the week of the wedding. The DJ writes two of them down and forgets the third. At the reception, the DJ plays the third song. The couple is furious. The DJ does not remember the conversation. The night is ruined for one of the three parties.

The do-not-play list has to be a document. It needs to be in writing. It needs to be signed by both partners (because partners sometimes disagree about what's on the list, and the signed document settles it). It needs to be delivered to the DJ at least two weeks before the wedding, and confirmed in the final-week meeting.

The format I recommend: a single page, with three sections — Personal, Style, Cliché. Each entry is a song title and an artist. Each entry has a note column for "strict" or "use judgment." Strict means never play this, no matter who asks. Use judgment means avoid by default but the DJ can play it if the floor genuinely needs it and the right moment comes up.

That two-tier system — strict vs. use judgment — is the key. It lets you ban "Cha Cha Slide" without giving up the ability for the DJ to play it at 9:55 pm when the floor is dipping and a group of cousins has been requesting it for an hour. If you mark it "strict," it never plays. If you mark it "use judgment," it plays only when the DJ thinks the room genuinely wants it.

## The do-not-play meeting

Two weeks before the wedding, the DJ should have a planning meeting with the couple. The do-not-play list is part of that meeting. Print it out. Sit at the table with the DJ. Walk through every entry. The DJ will ask clarifying questions:

- "When you say no country, do you mean no traditional country or also no Taylor Swift and no country-pop crossovers?"
- "When you ban 'Cha Cha Slide,' is that strict or use judgment if the floor goes flat at 10 pm?"
- "When you say no explicit songs, is that the clean version of every song, or is that 'don't play songs that even have a clean version because the meaning is still there'?"
- "When you say no songs by this artist, is the artist's collaborations off too, or just their solo work?"

A great wedding DJ will ask these questions because the answers change what gets played at the wedding. A DJ who does not ask these questions either doesn't have a system or is going to wing it. Either way, that's the moment to find out.

## The list saves the reception in one specific way

There is one moment at every reception where the do-not-play list earns its keep, and it is always the same moment.

Somewhere between 9:00 and 10:30 pm, the floor dips. The dance set has been going for an hour, the energy has plateaued, and the DJ needs to make a decision about what to play next to pull the floor back up. The easiest thing to play, the thing the DJ has been trained to play, is the cliché floor-filler — "Cha Cha Slide," "Sweet Caroline," "YMCA," "Don't Stop Believin'." Those songs work. They always work.

If the couple has not banned them, the DJ will play one of them. The floor will fill up for the duration of that song, and then settle back. The reception will feel like every other wedding the guests have been to.

If the couple has banned them — and the DJ has bought into the ban — the DJ has to find a different way to pull the floor back. They have to read the room more carefully. They have to use a song that is specific to that couple's taste, that lands with that specific guest list, that pulls the floor without falling back on the cliché. The reception ends up feeling like *this* wedding, not every wedding.

That is what the do-not-play list does, ultimately. It forces the DJ to make the reception specific to you.

It is the most underrated music document at a wedding. And it is the one that protects everything else.

## Frequently asked questions

**What should be on a wedding do-not-play list?**

Three categories. (1) Personal — songs tied to an ex, a deceased family member, or a private incident that nobody outside the couple needs to understand. (2) Style — entire genres or artists the couple does not want played, like country or hip-hop or anything explicit. (3) Cliché — the overplayed wedding songs the couple personally hates ("Cha Cha Slide", "Macarena", "YMCA" are the three most common). The do-not-play list should be in writing, signed by both partners, and shared with the DJ at least two weeks before the wedding.

**How long should a wedding do-not-play list be?**

Five to fifteen specific entries is healthy. A list with fewer than five entries usually means the couple hasn't actually thought through the music. A list with more than twenty entries usually means the couple is micromanaging the DJ and will end up frustrated when the DJ has nothing to play that the floor will dance to. Aim for the songs that genuinely cannot be played — not every song you don't personally love.

**Should we put the Cha Cha Slide and the Chicken Dance on our do-not-play list?**

Only if you genuinely hate them. The Cha Cha Slide, the Cupid Shuffle, the Wobble, the Electric Slide, the Chicken Dance, and YMCA are the line-dance pulls that drag the medium-engaged dancers back onto the floor — kids, aunts, the couple's college friends who don't normally dance. Banning all of them is fine; just know you're removing one of the DJ's most reliable floor-filler levers. Most couples ban one or two and keep one or two.

**What's the difference between a do-not-play list and an explicit-only ban?**

Two different documents. A do-not-play list is specific songs (or specific artists or specific genres) that the DJ should not play. An explicit-only ban is a blanket instruction to play clean versions of all songs — no profanity, no slurs, no graphic lyrics. Most working DJs default to clean versions at weddings unless told otherwise, but always state your preference in writing. If you have grandparents, kids, or religious family members at the wedding, the explicit-only ban is non-negotiable.

**Can the do-not-play list include entire genres?**

Yes. "No country" or "no rap" is a legitimate do-not-play instruction. The DJ will work around it — country and hip-hop are big enough categories that removing them leaves plenty of floor-fillers. Be aware that "no rap" usually means "no songs with explicit rap verses or aggressive content" to the DJ, but might also mean "no Beyoncé, no Lizzo, no Bruno Mars songs with rap verses" to a strict interpretation. Be specific about what you mean.

**Should the do-not-play list be enforced if a guest requests one of those songs?**

Yes — that's the entire point of the list. The reason a do-not-play list exists is that without it, a guest request from an uncle at 9:45 pm can override your music vision in two seconds. A good DJ holds the line. Tell the DJ at the planning meeting exactly how strictly to enforce the list — "never play these no matter who asks" is a clear instruction and the DJ will respect it. If you don't make the line clear, an uncle is going to make the night's music decisions for you.
