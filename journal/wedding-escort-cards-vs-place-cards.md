# Escort Cards vs Place Cards: What's the Difference and Which Do You Need?

Most couples treat "escort card" and "place card" as interchangeable. They're not. They solve different problems, go in different locations, and if you mix them up, your seating plan falls apart before the salad course.

Here's the breakdown — clear, practical, from someone who has watched seating logistics unfold (and unravel) at hundreds of weddings.

---

## The Quick Version

| | Escort Card | Place Card |
|---|---|---|
| **What it does** | Tells guests which table they're at | Tells guests which seat at the table |
| **Where it lives** | Entry / cocktail hour space | On the table, at the seat |
| **Guest interaction** | Guest picks it up and carries it | Guest finds it waiting |
| **Required?** | Usually | Only if assigned seats matter |

---

## What Is an Escort Card?

An escort card "escorts" your guest from the entrance to their table. The guest picks it up during cocktail hour (usually from a flat display, alphabetized by last name), reads their table number, and walks to that table.

Escort cards solve the problem of the early-arrival bottleneck — without them, guests wander, ask vendors, or clump awkwardly near the door. With them, everyone self-routes.

**Standard format:**
- Guest name (often "The Smith Family" or "Emma + Guest")
- Table number or table name

**Size:** Typically 3.5" × 2" — business card size. Easy to hold, fits in a pocket, doesn't blow off tables.

**How they're displayed:** Most commonly laid flat in rows on a table, alphabetized. Some couples do hanging cards on a string wall or tucked into envelopes on a frame.

---

## What Is a Place Card?

A place card marks a specific seat at a specific table. Your guest has already found their table (either via escort card or general assignment) and now knows exactly where to sit within that table.

Place cards are usually tent-style cards — printed flat and folded so they stand upright, visible from across the table.

**When you need them:**
- You have a head table with specific seat assignments (MOH next to bride, etc.)
- You're doing a plated dinner with pre-ordered meal choices (the card can indicate the meal)
- Your caterer needs to know who gets what dish delivered where
- You want to separate specific guests (divorced parents, feuding relatives, you know the situation)

**When you can skip them:**
- Round tables where seat-within-table doesn't matter (most casual receptions)
- Buffet receptions where guests plate their own food
- Family-style service where the dish moves around the table

---

## Do You Need Both?

Here's the real talk:

**Most weddings need escort cards. Few need full place cards at every seat.**

The common middle ground:
- Use escort cards to direct everyone to their table
- Use place cards only at the head table where exact positioning matters

If you're doing a plated dinner with pre-selected entrées, your caterer may require seat-level place cards so servers can deliver correctly. Confirm with your caterer before deciding.

---

## How Many Do You Need?

**Escort cards:** One per couple/family unit, not per person. "The Rodriguez Family" gets one card, not four. Count your RSVPs by household, not by head.

For a 120-guest wedding with an average household size of 2, you need roughly 60–70 escort cards.

**Place cards:** One per seat, only for the tables that require it.

---

## Setting Up Escort Cards Without the Chaos

The biggest escort card mistake couples make is not alphabetizing them. Guests spend five uncomfortable minutes searching a display in order of their arrival anxiety level. Always alphabetize by last name.

**Display tips from someone who's been at a lot of receptions:**
- Add two index cards at either end of the display — "A–M" and "N–Z" — so guests self-sort
- Leave 20% more room than you think you need at each section (guests take up space)
- Keep the display table clear of decoration clutter; it's functional, not ornamental
- Station a coordinator near the display for the first 15 minutes of cocktail hour to redirect anyone confused

## Tent Place Cards: The Format That Works

If you're doing place cards at specific tables, the **tent card** format is the easiest to produce and the most functional:

- Print flat on US Letter paper (6 per sheet)
- Score and fold at the center
- Stands upright on its own without a holder

The card reads correctly when a guest sits down and looks at it face-on. Servers can read the table from standing height.

**Avoid flat place cards that lay on the table** — they get covered by centerpiece spread, water glasses, and bread baskets within the first five minutes of service.

---

## Printing Your Escort and Place Cards

**Cardstock weight:** Go with at least 80 lb for escort cards, 90–100 lb for tent cards. Tent cards need enough rigidity to stand without flopping.

**Laser vs inkjet:** Laser holds sharper text at small sizes and doesn't smudge. If you're printing on a home inkjet, let cards dry fully before touching (20 minutes minimum) and avoid high-humidity venues if possible.

**Cut tolerance:** A die-cutter or rotary trimmer gives cleaner edges than scissors. If you're printing 80+ escort cards, a $20 rotary trimmer pays for itself in time saved.

**Outsourcing the print:** For 100+ cards, local copy shops (FedEx Office, Staples) can print on their cardstock and cut to size for $30–80 depending on quantity. Worth it if you're printing a lot.
