# How to build a wedding guest list

*The list isn't a contact list. It's the math that decides the venue, the catering, the budget, and the seating chart. A 25-year wedding DJ on the cuts, the categories, and the RSVP chase.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 3, 2026 · 7 min read**

---

The wedding guest list looks like a list of names. It is not.

The guest list is a number. That number — let's say it is 120 — is the input to almost every other number on the wedding. The venue you can rent. The catering quote per plate. The bar package. The chair count. The favor count. The number of programs the printer charges you for. The size of the photographer's second-shooter agreement. The number of seats at the reception, which decides the number of tables, which decides the floral package the centerpieces are quoted against.

If you build the guest list right, the rest of the wedding has a chassis to hang off. If you build it wrong, you spend the next nine months recalculating every other vendor quote every time a cousin gets added or removed.

I have stood behind a DJ console at maybe five hundred receptions, and I will tell you the moments that go sideways most predictably: the moments where the guest list math was wrong. The empty table at the back nobody planned for. The two chairs missing from table eleven because nobody told catering. The bride finding out at 6 pm that the entire bridesmaid-cousins table didn't show up and the table is dark for the whole reception.

The guest list is the document that prevents those moments. It is worth building correctly the first time.

## The four categories you need on the sheet

The mistake most couples make is starting the list as one long alphabetical column. That structure tells you nothing. It does not let you cut. It does not let you forecast.

Build the list in four categories instead:

1. **Must-attend** — the people without whom the wedding does not happen. Parents. Siblings. Wedding party. Grandparents you actually talk to. This is usually 15 to 30 people.
2. **Close family and best friends** — the people you would be sad to not have there. This is usually 30 to 60 people.
3. **Extended family and broader friend group** — the people you'd like to have but can absorb a "no" from. This is usually another 30 to 80 people.
4. **The "if we have room" list** — coworkers, plus-ones for single friends, friends of parents you've met twice. This is usually another 20 to 50 people.

Now the spreadsheet has a structure. When the venue says "we can hold 130," you know what the 130 looks like: all of category one and two, most of category three, and almost none of category four. When the venue says "we can hold 90," you know what that looks like: all of category one and two, and a hard conversation about category three.

The categories are also how you handle a budget cut. If catering comes in $40 a head higher than you estimated, you don't sit at the kitchen table and try to figure out who to remove. You strike from the bottom of category four upward until the math works.

> The categories are not for the guests. The categories are for the math. The guests will never know which tier they were in.

## The columns the spreadsheet actually needs

The first-draft guest list usually has two columns: name and address. That is not a guest list. That is a Christmas card list.

A working wedding guest list has, at minimum:

`Name` · `Category (1-4)` · `Mailing address` · `Email` · `Save-the-date sent` · `Invitation sent` · `RSVP status (yes/no/pending)` · `Plus-one (yes/no/name)` · `Meal selection` · `Dietary` · `Table assignment` · `Notes`

Twelve columns. They look like a lot at the start; you fill them in over nine months and the document writes itself.

The two columns that catch people off-guard are *Meal selection* and *Dietary*. Most catering contracts now require a per-plate menu selection one to two weeks before the wedding. If you have 120 guests and you ask each one for their entree choice in a single late-stage email, you will get 60 responses and you will be re-emailing the other 60 the night before the deadline. The fix is to put the meal selection on the RSVP card itself, and to enter it directly into the spreadsheet as you receive each RSVP. By the catering deadline, the column is already 95% complete.

## The plus-one question

Every guest list has it. It is the most uncomfortable conversation in wedding planning, and most couples handle it by avoiding the conversation entirely until two weeks before the invitations go out.

Here is the working rule I see real weddings use, in order:

- **Married, engaged, or living-together couples get plus-ones** as a matter of basic respect. Both names go on the invitation.
- **Dating couples of more than a year**, where you actually know the partner, get plus-ones.
- **Single friends without a current partner** — this is where the line gets drawn. The defensible rule is "anyone whose plus-one we have not met or whose relationship is not established does not get one." This is not unkind. It is reciprocity: if your single friend was getting married, they would not be inviting your imaginary plus-one either.
- **Coworkers** — no plus-ones, period. Coworkers attend as themselves or not at all. This is widely understood.

The spreadsheet enforces this. The *Plus-one (yes/no/name)* column captures the decision per row, not as a sweeping rule. You will spend twenty minutes one Sunday going down the list and assigning the plus-one column. After that, the question is closed and the invitations get printed cleanly.

## Save-the-dates, invitations, and the RSVP chase

The mailing sequence is roughly:

- **8 to 10 months out:** save-the-dates to category 1, 2, and 3.
- **3 months out:** formal invitations to everyone the venue can hold, in priority order.
- **6 to 8 weeks out:** RSVP deadline.
- **5 weeks out:** the RSVP chase begins.

The RSVP chase is the part nobody warns couples about. You will send invitations to 120 households. You will receive RSVPs from approximately 65 of them by the deadline. The remaining 55 households require a personal text or call to confirm. This is not optional. The catering deadline is two weeks after the RSVP deadline. You cannot show up with 65 confirmed and tell the venue "the rest is a mystery."

This is where the spreadsheet earns its keep. Sort by RSVP status = pending. That is the call list for the week after the deadline. Make the calls. Update the column as each one comes back. By the catering deadline, the column is 100% known.

## What goes wrong when the list is wrong

When the guest list math is wrong, the wedding day shows it in three places.

**The seating chart.** A bad list means tables you sized wrong. The cousins' table sized for ten with twelve names on it. The friends' table sized for eight with six showing up. The whole room reshuffling at 4 pm because two no-show households were never updated in the spreadsheet.

**The catering count.** Catering is paid for the *guaranteed* count, not the showed-up count. If your guarantee was 130 and 110 came, you paid for 130 meals. If your guarantee was 110 and 130 came, the kitchen ran short and people complained. The guest list, accurately maintained, is what makes the guarantee number defensible.

**The DJ's intro list.** I'll be honest about my own side of this. The intro list for the bridal party, the welcome announcements, the special-dance call-outs — those all read from a document that started as the guest list. When the spreadsheet has dirty data, the DJ's mic at 6:45 pm reflects it. I have stood at a microphone trying to call up a couple who divorced six months ago because nobody updated the list.

The wedding day is a finite number of minutes. Bad guest-list data eats those minutes. Good data lets the minutes go to the things you'll remember.

## The minimum guest list, written out

If you do nothing else this weekend, open a Google Sheet and stub in these columns:

`Name` · `Category` · `Address` · `Email` · `RSVP` · `Plus-one` · `Meal` · `Dietary` · `Table` · `Notes`

Add rows for the people in categories 1 and 2 first — that's the easy part. Total the rows. Compare to your target headcount. Notice how fast you hit the venue cap.

That is the wedding guest list. It will grow for nine months, contract for two more, and finalize the week before. You will love some of the people on it and tolerate a few. That is normal.

The point of the spreadsheet is that you will know exactly where every guest sits, what they're eating, and whether they've confirmed — at any point in the planning, in under five seconds. The couples who have that are the couples who arrive at the wedding with the energy to enjoy it.

The couples who don't are the couples I am watching at 4 pm trying to figure out which table is the missing one.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many people should I invite to my wedding versus expect to show up?**

The working rule for in-state weddings: assume about 85% of invites accept, with another 5% no-show after RSVPing yes. For destination weddings the acceptance rate drops to 60 to 70%. So if your venue holds 130 and you want 120 actual guests, you can comfortably invite 140 to 145 households for an in-state wedding, or 170 to 180 for a destination one. The spreadsheet should let you forecast both numbers from the start.

**How do I decide who gets a plus-one?**

Use four rules in order. Married, engaged, or living-together couples get plus-ones — both names on the invitation, no exceptions. Dating couples of more than a year where you actually know the partner get plus-ones. Single friends without a current established partner do not — this is reciprocity, not unkindness. Coworkers attend as themselves with no plus-ones, period. The spreadsheet captures the decision per row in a Plus-One column so the question is closed before invitations print.

**Should I invite kids to my wedding?**

Three working options. (1) Fully kid-friendly: invite all kids, plan a kids' table or kids' meal, and budget for the chair count. (2) Family-only kids: only nieces, nephews, and the wedding party's children — most couples land here. (3) Adults-only: no kids at all, communicated clearly on the save-the-date so parents have months to plan childcare. Whichever you pick, communicate it once and consistently. The "we mean it" moment is the save-the-date wording, not the invitation.

**When should I send save-the-dates and invitations?**

Save-the-dates go out 8 to 10 months before the wedding for local couples and 10 to 12 months out for destination weddings. Formal invitations go out 3 months before the wedding, with an RSVP deadline 6 to 8 weeks out. That gives you two weeks between the RSVP deadline and the catering's final-count deadline — exactly the window you need for the RSVP chase.

**How do I track wedding RSVPs in a spreadsheet?**

Use an RSVP-status column with three values: yes, no, pending. Sort by pending the week after the RSVP deadline — that is your call list. You will typically receive about half the RSVPs by the deadline; the other half require a personal text or call. This is not optional because the catering deadline is two weeks after the RSVP deadline. Update the column as each response comes back; by the catering deadline, it is 100% known.

**How do I track guests' meal preferences and dietary needs?**

Two columns on the guest list spreadsheet: Meal selection and Dietary. Put the meal choice on the RSVP card itself, and enter each response into the spreadsheet as you receive it. By the catering's final-count deadline, the column is already 95% complete. Common dietary tags worth tracking: vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, nut allergy, kosher, halal, and "kids meal." Send the catering team the totals — not the individual rows — about two weeks out.

**What columns should my wedding guest list spreadsheet have?**

Twelve at minimum: Name, Category (1-4), Mailing address, Email, Save-the-date sent, Invitation sent, RSVP status, Plus-one (yes/no/name), Meal selection, Dietary, Table assignment, Notes. The first-draft list usually has two columns (name and address) and that is a Christmas card list, not a guest list. The full twelve columns sound like a lot at the start; you fill them in over nine months and the document writes itself.
