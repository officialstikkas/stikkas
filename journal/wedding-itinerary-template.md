# Wedding itinerary template: the two itineraries every wedding actually needs

*A 25-year wedding DJ on the difference between the timeline you give the vendors and the itinerary you give the guests — what goes on each, when to send them, and the print specs that make them readable in a low-lit reception hall.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 20, 2026 · 11 min read**

---

The question I get asked most often at the rehearsal dinner, by the parents and the wedding party both, is the same question: *what is happening when.* They have all seen the website. Most of them got a save-the-date eighteen months ago. They are still asking, the night before the wedding, what is happening when.

This is not because anyone is failing to pay attention. It is because the wedding has more moving parts than a normal weekend, the events happen across multiple buildings, and the timing of one thing changes the timing of every other thing. The cure is not more communication. The cure is a *document* — a small one, in their hands, that answers the question.

The trap is that most couples build *one* document and use it for both vendors and guests. That document either becomes too detailed (terrifying for guests) or too vague (useless for vendors). The fix is to build two documents, both short, each pointed at one audience.

I want to walk you through what goes on each, when to send them, and the print specs that make the difference between a welcome bag insert your aunt reads at the hotel and one she leaves on the dresser.

## The two itineraries

There are two documents you actually need. The *vendor timeline* and the *guest itinerary*. (The wedding party gets a third — closer to the vendor timeline than the guest itinerary, but with its own format. I'll get to that.)

### The vendor timeline

This is the internal call sheet. It is owned by the day-of coordinator and shared with every paid vendor — DJ, photographer, videographer, caterer, florist, officiant, transportation, hair/makeup, venue manager. It runs in minute-level detail, usually fifteen-minute blocks across the wedding day, with five-minute precision at hand-off moments (mic hand-off, processional cue, grand entrance, first dance, cake cut).

This document is not for guests. Showing it to guests is overwhelming and creates anxiety they cannot act on. *("Wait, the cocktail hour ends at 6:45? But I haven't gotten a drink yet.")*

The vendor timeline lives in a shared spreadsheet or a printed call sheet handed out the morning of the wedding. The DJ keeps a laminated version on the deck. The coordinator keeps a clipboard version. The photographer keeps a copy in the camera bag.

I cover the structure of the vendor timeline in detail in [How to build a wedding reception timeline that actually works](/journal/wedding-reception-timeline/) — that is the document you need if you are the one building it. For this post, assume it exists.

### The guest itinerary

This is the friendly version. It is what guests carry around the weekend so they know where to go and when. It is block-level — five to seven events maximum across the wedding weekend, each one with six fields and not a field more.

The six fields:

1. **Name of event** (one or two words: *Welcome Dinner*, *Ceremony*, *Reception*, *Farewell Brunch*)
2. **Day and time** (*Friday, June 12 · 6:30 PM*)
3. **Dress code** (one sentence: *cocktail attire*, *beach casual*, *all white*, *come comfortable*)
4. **Location** with full address (the address is non-negotiable — guests use it for Uber)
5. **Transportation** (one line: *shuttles from the Hilton at 5:45*, or *self-drive, valet on-site*, or *no transportation provided*)
6. **One-line note**, if needed (*Note: ceremony is outdoors, bring a wrap*; *Note: brunch is family-style, no reservations needed*)

That's it. Six fields per event. Five to seven events for a weekend wedding. Print it on a single 5×7 card, double-sided if you have to. Welcome bag insert.

## The example: a real wedding weekend itinerary

Here is what a guest itinerary looks like for a Saturday wedding with a Friday welcome dinner and a Sunday brunch. Three events. The kind of weekend wedding that is now the norm for destination and out-of-town weddings.

> **The Garcia–Wong Wedding Weekend**
> *June 12–14, 2026*
>
> **Friday, June 12**
> **Welcome Dinner · 6:30 PM**
> Cocktail attire
> The Olive Grove, 2410 Highland Ave, Pasadena
> Self-drive · valet available
>
> **Saturday, June 13**
> **Ceremony · 5:30 PM**
> Cocktail attire
> Greystone Mansion Garden, 905 Loma Vista Dr, Beverly Hills
> Shuttles from the Hilton at 4:45 PM · return shuttles at 11:30 PM
> *Note: ceremony is outdoors on grass — flat shoes or wedges recommended*
>
> **Sunday, June 14**
> **Farewell Brunch · 10:00 AM – 1:00 PM**
> Casual
> The Garcia family home, 415 N. Catalina Ave, Pasadena
> Self-drive · street parking
> *Drop by anytime — family-style, no reservations needed*

That is the entire document. Three events, each with six fields, fitted onto a single 5×7 card. A guest reads it once at the hotel and knows where to be for the entire weekend.

Now contrast that with what *not* to do. The single most common mistake is the couple who tries to put the ceremony order of service on the itinerary card. They list the processional, the readings, the unity ceremony, the recessional. Suddenly the card has seventeen lines for Saturday alone, and Friday and Sunday have to be tucked into the corners. The whole card becomes unreadable.

The ceremony order of service belongs on the ceremony program. That is a separate document, handed out at the ceremony itself, that lives in the chair pocket or the welcome basket at the ceremony entrance. Keep it off the weekend itinerary.

## The wedding party itinerary

The wedding party needs its own document — closer to the vendor timeline than the guest itinerary, but written for non-professionals. Bridesmaids and groomsmen do not need to know when the wireless mic hand-off happens. They do need to know what time hair and makeup starts, when the bus is leaving the hotel, and the exact moment they need to be in position behind the doors.

Five blocks usually covers it:

1. **Get-ready window** — when hair and makeup start, where, the order of the chairs. Include the photographer's arrival time.
2. **Photo blocks** — when the wedding party portraits happen, where to meet, what to bring (often: bouquets staged with the florist).
3. **Transportation** — bus or shuttle departure times, with a five-minute buffer baked in.
4. **Ceremony positions** — exact time to be in position behind the doors, and which door.
5. **Reception touchpoints** — grand entrance order and pairing, first dance start time, parent dance start times.

I tell wedding parties: assume there will be no group text, no live updates, no one shouting at you across the parking lot. The document on your phone or in your bridesmaid robe pocket is the one source of truth. Print one copy per member of the wedding party. Half of them will lose it. The other half will save the day with it.

## When to send each

The guest itinerary goes out in two phases.

The **digital version** goes on the wedding website two months before the wedding, with the line *please print this for your travel planning*. That timing matters for destination guests booking flights and hotels — they want to know whether they need to arrive Friday morning or Friday evening. Two months out is also when most destination travel becomes non-refundable.

The **printed version** is the welcome bag insert. It is handed out at the hotel check-in the day before the wedding. This is the version that gets read — guests check into the hotel, sit on the bed, open the welcome bag, and want to know what they are doing for the next 48 hours.

Sending a printed itinerary in the mail with the invitation is overkill for most weddings. Worse, it freezes the times nine months out, before the venue has confirmed the ceremony start, before the caterer has nailed down the cocktail hour, before any of the timing has actually been settled. Then when something shifts, half the guests are operating off the printed card and half are off the website, and the parents are texting you to ask which one is right.

The vendor timeline does not get sent to guests. Ever. Some couples post it on the wedding website "for transparency" and the result is guests showing up forty minutes early or, worse, asking the DJ when the cake cut is during the dancing. Keep the vendor timeline inside the vendor circle.

The wedding party itinerary goes out one week before the wedding, with a reminder text the morning of the rehearsal. Print copies at the rehearsal dinner.

## The mistakes I see most often

After 25 years of weddings, four mistakes account for almost all of the day-of confusion that traces back to bad itineraries.

**Mistake 1: One document, two audiences.** The couple builds a single PDF, calls it "Wedding Day Schedule," and tries to share it with both vendors and guests. Either it has too much detail for guests (anxiety) or too little for vendors (chaos). Always two documents.

**Mistake 2: No transportation line.** Guests at destination weddings frequently do not know they are supposed to take the shuttle. They drive themselves, arrive late because they got lost on a dark canyon road, and the ceremony starts twenty minutes behind. Every event needs a one-line transportation note. *Shuttles from the Hilton at 4:45. Self-drive, valet on-site. No transportation provided.*

**Mistake 3: No dress code line.** The wedding website said cocktail attire. The save-the-date said "evening wear." The text the bridesmaid sent said "country casual." Now your aunt is in a sequined gown at a barn wedding and your cousin is in a polo shirt at a black-tie. Print the dress code on the itinerary card for every event. One sentence each.

**Mistake 4: Times that conflict with the vendor timeline.** This is the worst one. The guest itinerary says the ceremony is at 5:30. The vendor timeline says the ceremony starts at 5:45 to give the bridal party fifteen minutes of pre-ceremony photos. The guests arrive at 5:00 to be early, sit in the heat for forty-five minutes, and the wedding starts behind schedule because the guests are visibly cranky. Whoever owns the itinerary needs to talk to the coordinator, *every time the timeline changes*, and update the guest version to match the new start times — usually by rounding down to the guest-facing time and quietly running the actual ceremony five minutes later.

## Print specs

This is the part most templates leave out. The itinerary is a printed object — it lives in a welcome bag at a dim hotel, or on a card table at the venue under low lighting, or in someone's pocket while they walk between two outdoor events. The paper choice and the typography decide whether it survives.

**Paper:** 80–100 lb cover, matte finish, white or cream. Matte over glossy — glossy reflects venue lighting and makes the text unreadable when held at an angle. Cream over white if the wedding palette is warm; pure white if the palette is cool. Skip kraft (brown) for the itinerary — it looks great in photos but the contrast is too low for older eyes.

**Type size:** body copy at 11 pt minimum, 12 pt for venue addresses (the address is the line guests squint at to type into a phone, so make it the most legible thing on the card). Event names can go to 14–18 pt for hierarchy. Skip fonts thinner than book weight — light and ultra-light fonts disappear under low light.

**Layout:** single column on a 5×7 card. Day → Event → Time → Address → Notes, stacked. Resist the urge to put two events side-by-side; the eye loses the second one. If the weekend has more than four events, go to 5×7 folded (essentially a small four-page booklet) before going to a wider format.

**Print at home or print shop:** for fifty or fewer guests, print at home on cardstock and trim with a metal ruler and craft knife — total cost under $15. For more than fifty, the print shop wins on consistency and cost-per-card (Hector at iDgraphx prints 100 cards for around $35; I print most of mine through him because the card edges are clean). For a destination wedding with welcome bags assembled in the hotel room the night before arrival, do the print run at home in your hometown and travel with the cards in a hardcover folder so the corners don't bend.

If you want the deeper print-at-home walkthrough — paper sourcing, printer settings, the trim technique — it lives in [How to print a wedding planner that actually looks good at home](/journal/print-wedding-planner-at-home/). The same paper, settings, and trim approach work for itinerary cards.
