# Wedding planner spreadsheet guide: the four sheets that actually run a wedding

*The four spreadsheet tabs that decide whether a wedding lands — budget, guest list, vendor tracker, seating chart — built from twenty-five years behind a DJ console, watching the spreadsheet-first couples stay calm and the inbox-first couples panic.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 7, 2026 · 15 min read**

---

I have been DJing weddings since 2001. Somewhere north of five hundred receptions, across Los Angeles ballrooms and backyards in Glendale and a vineyard in Temecula and a Polish-American hall in Pomona that holds two hundred and twenty. I have watched the same pattern repeat at every wedding I've worked: the couples who finish wedding-planning calmly and the couples who finish it in a panic make their critical decisions in completely different *documents*.

The couples who stay calm built a single Google Sheet in week one of the engagement. Four tabs. Budget, guest list, vendor tracker, seating chart. Every decision they made for the next nine months — every dollar, every guest, every vendor, every table — got typed into that sheet within forty-eight hours. By month nine, the sheet had the entire wedding's worth of data in one place, sharable with their partner, the day-of coordinator, the maid of honor, and the parents who were contributing.

The couples who panicked kept their wedding in their email inbox. Vendor quotes as PDF attachments. Guest list in a Notes app. Budget in their head. Final-due dates on Post-its on the fridge. They show up to the venue tasting unable to remember whether the photographer's quote was $4,500 or $5,500. They get hit by six final invoices in the same week of the wedding and put tips on a credit card. They redo the seating chart on Wednesday night with paper place cards because the digital version got lost.

The difference between the two outcomes is not budget. It is not taste. It is not even discipline. It is the document.

This page is the guide to the document. The four tabs. The columns each one needs. The sorts that actually pay rent. The shareability that lets every player in the wedding see what they need without you forwarding fourteen emails.

## The four-sheet method

A wedding planner spreadsheet is not one document. It is four documents that share a single file because they need to talk to each other.

**Tab 1 — Budget.** The number that decides every other number. Eight categories. Nine columns per row. Deposit-paid and final-due as the two columns most templates skip and every working couple needs. Sortable by final-due-date in the last sixty days, which is the single most leveraged sort on the entire spreadsheet.

**Tab 2 — Guest list.** The four-category list (must-attend, close, extended, "if we have room"). Twelve columns per guest. RSVP status, plus-one, meal selection, dietary, table assignment. The catering count, the venue size, the chair rental, the stationery print run are all downstream of this tab's row count.

**Tab 3 — Vendor tracker.** Ten to fourteen vendors. Twelve columns per vendor. Contract signed, deposit paid, final balance, final due, day-of arrival time. The document that prevents the photographer from driving to the wrong venue and the cake from arriving an hour late.

**Tab 4 — Seating chart.** Tables and seats. Guest names pulled from the guest list tab (so changes propagate). Combustion notes from both sets of parents. Dance-floor crowd placed near the floor, grandparents in the second ring.

These four tabs together are the wedding planning document. Most couples build one or two and rebuild from scratch in month seven when the missing tabs catch up to them. The structural fix is to build all four in week one — most cells empty, but the columns and tabs in place — so the document grows over time instead of getting rebuilt under pressure.

## Tab 1 — The budget

The budget tab is the document that decides every other number on the wedding.

The 2026 US median for a hundred-guest wedding is around thirty-five thousand dollars. The honest range most couples land in is twenty-five to fifty thousand depending on city, venue type, and headcount. Los Angeles, New York, and Chicago run twenty to forty percent above national median; smaller cities and outdoor venues come in twenty to thirty percent under.

The eight-category structure that survives nine months:

| Category | Typical % of budget |
|---|---|
| Venue & catering | 40-50% |
| Photography & video | 10-15% |
| Florals & decor | 10-15% |
| Music & entertainment | 8-12% |
| Attire & beauty | 5-10% |
| Stationery & signage | 2-5% |
| Cake & dessert | 1-3% |
| Everything else | 5-10% |
| **+ Contingency** | **10%** |

The nine columns each line item needs:

> **Category** · **Item** · **Vendor** · **Estimated** · **Deposit $** · **Deposit paid date** · **Final due date** · **Final paid** · **Notes**

The two columns that most templates miss — and that every working couple needs — are **Deposit paid date** and **Final due date**. Sort the sheet by final-due-date in the last sixty days. That view tells you exactly how much cash flows out of the account in the final stretch and in what order. It is the single most leveraged sort on the spreadsheet. If you build one column from this whole guide, build that one.

## Tab 2 — The guest list

The wedding guest list looks like a list of names. It is not. It is the math that decides every other number on the wedding — the venue capacity, the catering count, the bar package, the chair count, the favor count, the size of the photographer's contract.

The four-category structure that lets you cut without family-drama:

1. **Must-attend** — parents, siblings, wedding party, grandparents you actually talk to. Usually 15-30 people.
2. **Close family and best friends** — the people you'd be sad to not have there. Usually 30-60 people.
3. **Extended family and broader friend group** — the people you'd like but can absorb a "no" from. Usually 30-80 people.
4. **"If we have room"** — coworkers, plus-ones for single friends, friends of parents you've met twice. Usually 20-50 people.

The structure is not for the guests. The guests will never know which tier they were in. The structure is for the math: when the venue says "we can hold 130," you can immediately see which categories fit; when catering comes in $40 higher per plate than estimated, you strike from the bottom of category four upward until the budget works.

The twelve columns the guest list tab actually needs:

> **Name** · **Category (1-4)** · **Mailing address** · **Email** · **Save-the-date sent** · **Invitation sent** · **RSVP status** · **Plus-one** · **Meal selection** · **Dietary** · **Table assignment** · **Notes**

The two columns most templates skip — and most couples eventually wish they had — are **Meal selection** and **Dietary**. Put the meal selection on the RSVP card itself, and enter each response into the spreadsheet as it arrives. By the catering's final-count deadline (typically two weeks before the wedding), the column is already 95% complete instead of requiring an end-of-cycle email chase to sixty people.

## Tab 3 — The vendor tracker

The vendor count almost always surprises couples. Most couples think "venue, photographer, DJ, florist" and stop there. The real count for a full-service wedding of 100-130 guests is ten to fourteen vendors. Each one has a contract, a deposit, a final-due date, a day-of arrival time, and a coordination handoff. Track them all in one tab or you will lose one.

The twelve columns the vendor tracker tab actually needs:

> **Vendor category** · **Business name** · **Contact person** · **Phone** · **Email** · **Contract signed (Y/N)** · **Deposit $** · **Deposit paid date** · **Final balance $** · **Final due date** · **Day-of arrival time** · **Notes**

The column most templates skip — and the column that prevents 80% of day-of disasters — is **Day-of arrival time**. Sort by arrival time on the morning of the wedding. Print the resulting list. Hand it to your day-of coordinator (or your maid of honor if you don't have one). That single printout — ten minutes to prepare — gives them the order vendors arrive, each vendor's contact person and phone, load-in windows, and the notes you've accumulated about each vendor over nine months.

## Tab 4 — The seating chart

I have stood at the front of the reception hall and watched the seating chart go right, and I have stood there and watched it go wrong. When it goes right, the dance floor opens at 8:15 and stays full. When it goes wrong, three tables empty by 9 p.m. and never come back. Couples almost never trace it back to the seating chart, but I usually can.

The seating chart tab is the last tab you fill in, but the first one you need to *set up* — empty tables, empty seats, ready to receive names — because the guest list tab feeds it. When RSVP status flips from "pending" to "yes" in the guest list, that name is now available for table assignment in the seating chart tab. Build the connection in week one (most cells empty), populate it in month nine and ten.

The five rules that survive a 100-guest reception:

1. **Seat by combustion risk, not by relationship.** Ask both sets of parents to mark the names that should not sit at adjacent tables. They will know the history you may have missed.
2. **One anchor per table.** Every table needs one sociable person who knows at least three others at the table and will pull the energy when conversation flags. Distribute the anchors, do not concentrate them at the head table.
3. **Mix strangers, but give every stranger one familiar face.** Strangers seated entirely among strangers go quiet for the first hour and never recover.
4. **Place the dance-floor crowd near the floor.** When the DJ opens the floor, the closest tables get up first. If those tables are the elders, the floor stays empty for three songs. If those tables are the dance crowd, the floor fills by the second song.
5. **Hold a TBD zone for late RSVPs.** Do not assign tables to non-responders until you've chased them. Resolve the TBD zone once, in the final week, after the catering deadline.

## How the four tabs talk to each other

The reason the four tabs live in one file is that they need to share data.

**Guest list count → budget per-head math.** When the guest count changes (and it always changes, by 10-25 between week 1 and the final RSVP), the per-head categories on the budget tab (catering, bar, chair rental, stationery) need to update. Build the formulas so the budget tab pulls headcount from the guest list tab — or at least update the budget number every time you update the guest list.

**Guest list RSVP status → seating chart pool.** Only "yes" RSVPs go into the seating chart tab. Build a filter on the guest list tab that surfaces just confirmed yeses, and use that as the seating pool. Saves you from placing a name that ultimately doesn't show.

**Vendor tracker final-due dates → budget cash-flow sort.** The sort by final-due-date on the budget tab is the same sort that runs on the vendor tracker. Build both, run both — they'll surface the same cash-flow truth from two angles.

**Vendor tracker day-of arrival times → seating chart load-in.** The vendor arrival times decide when the room is ready for guests, which decides when the seating chart goes into effect. The two tabs intersect at the start of the reception block.

These connections are why the spreadsheet beats the app. The four documents *need* to talk to each other in shapes the planner-app schemas don't anticipate. A spreadsheet lets you build those connections (or just hand-update them); an app forces you to navigate four separate sections that don't share data.

## The Google Sheets advantage in four specifics

I have been asked many times why I am so insistent on Google Sheets versus a wedding planner app. Four reasons, in the order they matter.

**Sharing.** A Google Sheet shares four different ways from the same file. Edit access for you and your partner. Read-only for the day-of coordinator. Filtered read-only for the wedding party (just arrival times, no budget numbers). Summary-only for parents who are contributing financially. Apps either share everything or nothing.

**Editing on the move.** You will edit the spreadsheet in vendor lobbies, at the venue tasting, at the bridal salon, in your car after a florist consult. The Google Sheets phone app handles all of this; you type a number, tap "done," and the change syncs. Apps want you to re-key from a PDF, which is fine on a laptop and friction at every other location.

**Schema flexibility.** Every wedding has line items the planner apps don't model. The day-of coordinator booked at month 5. The hairpiece you didn't know you needed. The Uber to the rehearsal dinner. The custom backdrop the photographer asked for. Spreadsheets shrug and absorb new rows. Apps refuse, and you end up with a "miscellaneous" line that hides the truth.

**Permanence.** Your wedding spreadsheet is yours forever. If the planner app's company goes out of business — and several have — your wedding data goes with it. The Google Sheet sits in your Drive whether or not anyone else is still around. Five years from now, you'll show your sister the spreadsheet when she gets engaged. The app would have shut down by then.

## The cash-flow truth, surfaced

The vendor tracker tab and the budget tab together expose a financial truth most couples don't see otherwise: **the wedding is paid in two bursts, not one.**

The first burst is the deposit cycle — months 8 through 4. Every vendor wants 25-50% to lock the date. For a $42,000 wedding, deposits total roughly $12,000 to $18,000 across eleven vendors. That cash needs to be available across the deposit window.

The second burst — the bigger one — is the final-balance cycle, weeks 4 through 1 before the wedding. Caterer final-due (typically 14 days out). Florist final-due (often 7 days out). Photographer final-due (often the wedding day). DJ final-due (often the wedding day). Hair and makeup, paid morning-of. Officiant's honorarium, paid at rehearsal. The final stretch is $20,000 to $28,000 of cash flowing out across thirty days.

The sort by final-due-date in the last sixty days is the view that exposes this burst at month 6 instead of month 1. If the second burst is bigger than you have liquid at month 6, you have time to move money around, take the bonus you were spreading out, or push two final-due-dates by negotiation. By month 1, you have no time.

This is the single most leveraged sort on the spreadsheet. Run it at month 6, run it again at month 3, run it the final time at week 2. Three reads of the same view across nine months will prevent the cash-crisis pattern that hits the couples who never built it.

## What I would build first if I were starting over

If I were starting a wedding plan tomorrow, here is the sheet I would build in the first week, in order:

**Week 1, day 1:** Open a new Google Sheet. Title it "[Last name] wedding planning." Share edit access with my partner. Add four blank tabs labeled "Budget," "Guest list," "Vendor tracker," "Seating chart."

**Week 1, day 2:** Build the budget tab. Add the eight category rows. Set a total budget cell at the top. Add the contingency line (10%). Populate the nine columns. Leave most cells empty. The structure is enough for now.

**Week 1, day 3:** Build the guest list tab. Add the four category headers. Type in the must-attend names you already know (parents, siblings, wedding party). Add the twelve columns. Leave most cells empty.

**Week 2:** Build the vendor tracker tab. Add the twelve columns. Add the fourteen vendor category rows even if you haven't picked any vendors yet — they're placeholders. The structure makes the document real.

**Week 3:** Build the seating chart tab. Add ten or twelve table headers (estimate based on venue capacity ÷ 10). Leave the seats empty. They'll fill in during month 9 and 10.

By week 3, the entire spreadsheet exists, structure-complete and mostly empty. Over the next nine months, you fill it in. By the wedding day, every cell has been touched, every formula has been checked, every sort has been run. The document is the wedding plan in the most literal sense possible.

That is the spreadsheet I would build. That is the spreadsheet we sell.
