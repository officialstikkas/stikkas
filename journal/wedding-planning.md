# Wedding planning guide: how to actually plan a wedding

*The twelve months of decisions that turn an engagement into a wedding day — written from twenty-five years behind a wedding DJ console, where the timeline either lands or it doesn't.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 5, 2026 · 18 min read**

---

I have been a wedding DJ for twenty-five years. I have stood behind a console at somewhere north of five hundred receptions — backyards in Glendale with rented tents, a ballroom at the Biltmore downtown, a vineyard in Temecula, a 200-person Polish-American hall in Pomona, three weddings on the same Saturday in October during the height of the season. I have watched couples plan beautifully and watched couples plan badly. By the time the bridal party is doing the grand entrance, I can usually tell you which one this is going to be.

Wedding planning is not actually one project. It is six or seven projects that all touch each other, run on different timelines, and don't have the same constraints. The mistake most couples make is treating it as a single linear checklist — pick the venue, pick the dress, pick the caterer, done. The truth is that the budget changes the guest list, the guest list changes the venue, the venue changes the food, the food changes the time of day, the time of day changes the music. Pull one string and three others move.

This is the guide I would write to my younger self if I had a wedding to plan today. It is also the guide that maps every long piece of writing we have published in the [Stikkas journal](/stikkas/journal/) into one place, so you can read it as one document or click through to whatever section matters this month.

If you want the planner that holds all of this in one PDF, that is the [Boho Wedding Planner](https://www.etsy.com/listing/4501860507/boho-wedding-planner-printable) we sell on Etsy — but you can plan a wedding without it. The point of this page is the structure, not the file.

## The twelve-month timeline

A real wedding takes twelve to fourteen months from engagement to wedding day. You can do it in six. You can do it in three for an elopement. But the comfortable, low-stress version is twelve months, and it breaks roughly into four phases.

**Months one through three: foundation.** Budget, guest list count (not the actual list yet — just a number), venue, date. The number, the rough crowd, the room, the day. Nothing else matters yet. Couples who try to pick a photographer before they pick a venue end up rescheduling the photographer.

**Months four through six: the big vendors.** Photographer, DJ, caterer if not included in the venue, officiant. These four people decide whether the day feels good or feels rough. Lock them early — the good ones book up six to nine months out.

**Months seven through nine: design and paper.** The aesthetic decisions. Color palette, florals, signage, invitation suite, save-the-dates if you didn't do them earlier, the planner itself, the seating chart starting to form. This is the phase where most couples discover Pinterest and lose two weeks. That's fine — it's part of it. The two weeks of Pinterest is also the two weeks where you figure out what you actually want versus what the algorithm thinks you want.

**Months ten through twelve: logistics and the day-of.** Guest list RSVPs, seating chart finalized, reception timeline locked, hair and makeup trial, day-of binder built, vendor coordination calls, the last hundred small decisions. This is also the phase where everyone tells you to "relax and enjoy" and you can't, because there are still a hundred small decisions.

Each phase has its own document. Months one through three live in the budget spreadsheet. Months four through six live in the vendor tracker. Months seven through nine live in the design brief and the planner. Months ten through twelve live in the day-of binder. Five documents. Each one used at a different time. We'll get to each.

## The budget comes first (and the number is bigger than you think)

The single most important conversation in the first month is the budget conversation. Not the venue. Not the date. The number.

The 2026 US median for a hundred-guest wedding is around thirty-five thousand dollars. The honest range is twenty-five to fifty thousand. In Los Angeles, New York, and Chicago, that range shifts up twenty to forty percent — a comparable LA wedding lands closer to forty-five to sixty thousand. Smaller cities and outdoor venues come in twenty to thirty percent under the national median.

The line items that almost always run over:

- **Catering and bar.** Per-head cost in 2026 is one hundred fifty to two hundred fifty dollars for plated, eighty to one hundred sixty for buffet, plus another forty to eighty per head for the bar. Hundred guests, plated, premium bar: you are at thirty thousand dollars on food and drink alone.
- **Photographer.** Twenty-five hundred to seventy-five hundred is the realistic range for an actual working pro who shoots forty weddings a year. Anyone quoting under fifteen hundred for a full day is a hobbyist or a portfolio-builder; the difference at the editing stage is enormous.
- **Florals.** Three thousand to ten thousand for a hundred-guest wedding with centerpieces, bouquets, and a ceremony arch. This is the line item most couples wildly underestimate. A four-stem bridesmaid bouquet is one hundred fifty dollars wholesale before labor.
- **Stationery and signage.** Five hundred to two thousand if you go custom. Closer to one hundred fifty if you use printable templates and print at home or at a local print shop. We have a [full guide to printing your wedding planner and signage at home](/stikkas/journal/print-wedding-planner-at-home/) — same skills apply to invitation suites and signage.

The structural move that saves more couples than any negotiation: decide which two or three categories actually matter to both of you, and intentionally underspend on the rest. If the food and the music are non-negotiable, the centerpieces can be a single hydrangea per table. If the dress is non-negotiable, the venue can be your friend's backyard. Couples who try to keep every category at the national average end up at an above-average total cost and an average wedding.

## The guest list decides the wedding

The most underrated document in wedding planning is the guest list. Every other number on the budget is downstream of the guest list — the venue size, the catering total, the bar cost, the number of invitations, the number of centerpieces, even the time the reception ends.

The structural rule I have seen work: cut the list before you cut anything else. A wedding for one hundred guests on a forty-thousand-dollar budget is a comfortable wedding. The same forty-thousand-dollar budget for one hundred sixty guests is a tight wedding. Cutting twenty guests saves four to eight thousand dollars without touching the photographer, the dress, or the venue.

The four-tier guest list (Tier 1 is non-negotiable, Tier 4 is "if we end up under capacity"), the plus-one rule that doesn't make anyone mad, and the RSVP-chase nobody warns you about are all in the long version: [how to build a wedding guest list (and why the spreadsheet decides the wedding)](/stikkas/journal/wedding-guest-list-spreadsheet/).

## Vendors: the four that matter, in the order that matters

There are technically twenty wedding vendors. In practice, four of them decide whether the day feels good or feels rough.

**Venue.** Book six to twelve months out. The venue dictates the date, the maximum guest count, the catering structure (in-house vs. brought-in), and often the time of day (most venues lock you into either an afternoon or evening block, not both). Walk-through with a critical eye — does the room hold the guest count *with* the dance floor in it, or only without? Is there a getting-ready room? Is there parking? Where do vendors load in? Couples who book venues by Instagram photos and not by walk-through always discover something painful in month nine.

**Photographer.** Book six to nine months out. The good ones are working every Saturday from May through October. Ask to see two complete weddings (not the highlight reel — every photo from getting-ready through the last dance). The highlight reel tells you what they can do at their best; the full gallery tells you what they actually deliver.

**DJ.** Also book six to nine months out. Almost no couples ask the questions that actually predict whether the DJ will land the reception. The [twelve questions to ask a wedding DJ before you book](/stikkas/journal/questions-to-ask-wedding-dj/) are the ones I would want my own son or daughter to ask if they were getting married. They include things like "how do you handle a flat first hour," "what's your read-the-room process," and the one almost no couple asks: "what happens at the moment of every transition." Print the questions, take them to the meeting.

**Caterer.** If the venue doesn't include catering, this is the third major vendor. Three to six months out. Tasting is mandatory — never book a caterer you haven't eaten food from. Ask specifically about dietary restrictions (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, allergies) and how they handle them, because every wedding has a few and bad handling shows up immediately at table four. The [twelve questions to ask your wedding caterer before you sign](/stikkas/journal/questions-to-ask-wedding-caterer/) — service-style time math, vendor meal timing, alcohol policy, dessert window — are the rigorous version of the catering interview.

For the full vendor framework — the twelve vendors every wedding actually needs, the booking order, the four that matter, the questions for each, and the spreadsheet that holds them all together — read the focused [wedding vendor guide](/stikkas/wedding-vendors/). ## The reception timeline is the document that runs the day

After twenty-five years behind the console, I will tell you the single document that decides whether a reception feels alive at 9 pm is the reception timeline. Not the food. Not the dress. The timeline.

The whole reception is four blocks:

1. **Arrival and cocktail** — forty-five to ninety minutes, low energy, social
2. **Grand entrance through dinner** — ninety minutes to two hours, anchored energy
3. **The bridge** — twenty to forty minutes from dinner ending to dance floor opening
4. **Open dancing** — two to two and a half hours, full energy

The block that ruins receptions is block three. Couples script the entrance, the toasts, the cake cutting, and the first dance. Nobody scripts the connective tissue between dinner ending and the dance floor opening. So there's a forty-minute gap where the DJ is playing dinner music to people who have finished eating, the photographer is changing lenses, the caterer is clearing plates, and the energy of the room drops to zero. By the time the first dance happens, the room is *sitting* and *digesting*, and they don't get up.

The fix is to script the bridge as a single twenty-minute performance — cake cutting, parent dances, bouquet toss, first dance, all flowing into open dancing with no gap. ## The seating chart is the second-hardest document

The hardest is the budget. The second-hardest is the seating chart.

The seating chart is where family dynamics, friend groups, plus-ones, and the physical shape of the room all collide. The five rules that survive a hundred-guest reception:

1. **Tables of eight, not ten.** Eight is the largest table where everyone can hear each other. Ten splits into two conversations and stops being one table.
2. **Mix age groups within tables, not across them.** Don't make a "kids table" that's actually a "young adults table." Mix older couples with young couples.
3. **Put loud people next to loud people.** Soft-spoken guests at the same table as loud ones end up not talking all night.
4. **Solve divorces last and out loud.** The most uncomfortable seating decisions are family ones. Make them in conversation with both parents, not in the spreadsheet.
5. **The bride and groom don't sit at the head table all night.** Plan to be up and moving from grand entrance through dinner, even if the head table is a thing.

## Planner vs. day-of binder: you need both

The wedding planner is a process document. You use it for twelve months. It holds the budget, the guest list, the vendor contacts, the design brief, the contract dates, the deposit schedule.

The day-of binder is an execution document. You use it for one day. It holds the reception timeline, the vendor day-of contacts, the bridal party intros, the must-play and do-not-play songs, the morning-of grab list, the toast order, the seating chart in printed form.

The mistake couples make is trying to use the planner *as* the day-of binder. The planner has too much in it. On the wedding day, at 4 pm, when the maid of honor needs to find the photographer's phone number and the bridal party intros, she should be flipping through ten pages, not eighty. The day-of binder is the planner's short, executable form.

The long version of when you need each is [wedding day binder or full wedding planner?](/stikkas/journal/wedding-day-binder-vs-full-planner/) — most couples need both, at different times.

## Printing things at home (and what to send to a print shop)

A surprising amount of wedding paper ends up being printed at home: the planner itself, the reception timeline, the day-of binder, the seating chart, sometimes the menus and the table numbers.

After thirty years in print shops, I can tell you the three home-printing mistakes that ruin the file:

1. **Printing on twenty-pound copy paper instead of cardstock.** Wedding signage and printables look thin and cheap on copy paper. Sixty-five-pound or one-hundred-pound cardstock costs four times more per sheet and looks ten times more expensive.
2. **Letting the printer auto-scale.** "Fit to page" shrinks the file by two to three percent every time. The bleed disappears, the margins shift, the trim doesn't line up. Print at one hundred percent, with the "actual size" or "no scaling" setting on.
3. **Using inkjet for ceremony programs and saving-the-day prints.** Inkjet on cardstock smudges if anyone touches it before it dries fully (eight hours, not the thirty seconds the printer says). Either use a laser printer or take ceremony programs and signage to a local print shop.

The full version is [how to print a wedding planner that actually looks good at home](/stikkas/journal/print-wedding-planner-at-home/). Worth reading before you load the first sheet.

The things to *not* print at home, and to send to a real print shop instead: invitation suites (color matters, paper choice matters), welcome signs over eleven by seventeen (the home printer can't do that size), and anything you want to look truly professional. Most local print shops will run a small wedding job for fifty to one hundred fifty dollars and the difference is visible at thumbnail size.
