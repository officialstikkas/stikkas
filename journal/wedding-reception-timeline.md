# The wedding reception timeline: a DJ's exact schedule for a 5-hour reception

*Minute-by-minute, from cocktail hour to last call — by a DJ who has worked more than five hundred of them*

**By Oscar Arana · June 23, 2026 · 13 min read**

---

The most common timeline mistake I see at weddings is not in the ceremony. It is in the mental model couples bring to the reception. When most people picture a five-hour reception, they picture five hours of dancing. What a five-hour reception actually contains is roughly ninety minutes of cocktail hour and dinner, ninety minutes of formalities and dinner service, and ninety minutes of open dancing — if everything runs on time, which it never does.

The couples who get real dancing at their reception plan for it explicitly. They know exactly how much time each block takes, they tell their DJ and coordinator in advance, and they make decisions — cut the garter toss, limit toasts to three minutes each, shorten dinner service — to protect the floor time they actually want.

What follows is the timeline I use when I work a five-hour reception from a 5 PM cocktail start. The exact times move depending on ceremony end time, venue, and the couple's priorities — but the structure almost never changes because it is built on how guests actually move, eat, drink, and dance at real weddings.

---

## The working timeline: 5 PM cocktail start, 10 PM end

### 4:45 PM — DJ arrives and opens the room

Guests start arriving before the start time. Always. Someone's mother is the first person anywhere near the venue entrance, fifteen minutes ahead of the announced start, and she is going to hear whatever music is playing. If I arrive at 5:00 PM and the first guest arrives at 4:52 PM, the first eight minutes of the reception sound like soundcheck.

I arrive forty-five minutes before guests. Music is playing, levels are set, the ceremony-to-reception transition playlist is ready, and the monitor near the coordinator station is on. The couple should plan for their DJ to have uninterrupted access to the venue from sixty minutes before guest arrival.

---

### 5:00–5:45 PM — Cocktail hour (venue opens to guests)

The cocktail hour music is background music. Guests are arriving from the ceremony, finding each other, getting their first drink, taking photos. The floor is not a dance floor yet. The correct volume is loud enough to fill the room and create energy, quiet enough that guests can have a conversation without leaning in.

What I play: uptempo jazz, smooth R&B, or whatever genre matches the couple's vibe without being their "real" music. Save the couple's actual playlist for when the couple is in the room.

The mistakes I see during cocktail hour:
- **Music too loud.** People cluster at the edges of the room to talk. Cocktail hour energy dies.
- **First dances started during cocktail hour.** I have seen this. Do not do this. Guests are not seated, the room is not set, and the photographer is not in position.
- **Cocktail hour running long.** Post-ceremony photos are the usual culprit. Every thirty minutes of photo time after the ceremony = thirty minutes of cocktail hour extension = thirty minutes less dancing later. Tell the photographer hard stops in the timeline.

**What the coordinator should be doing:** Room flip from ceremony (if applicable), confirming headcount with the caterer, tracking where the wedding party is, flagging the DJ when the couple is five minutes out.

---

### 5:45–6:00 PM — Grand entrance staging

Fifteen minutes before the grand entrance, the coordinator pulls the wedding party from cocktail hour and lines them up in entrance order. This is the highest-stress fifteen minutes of the evening for the coordinator and the most important fifteen minutes for me. I need:

- Full wedding party list, in entrance order, with phonetic pronunciations
- The couple's chosen entrance song
- Whether the wedding party dances in or walks in
- Whether the couple enters separately or together
- Whether the couple goes straight to the first dance or to the sweetheart table

Every one of these details should be settled at the week-of meeting, not five minutes before the entrance. I have been handed handwritten name lists in the staging area. I have been given phonetic guides written in the dark. Neither is how you set up a clean grand entrance.

---

### 6:00 PM — Grand entrance

If the cocktail hour was background music, the grand entrance is the room's first real moment. Done right, it brings energy that sustains through the first dance and into dinner. Done wrong — wrong song, wrong timing, names mispronounced — the room recovers but it takes twenty minutes.

The entrance order almost always works better when it builds. Groomsmen and bridesmaids first (they get the crowd warm), then the flower girls and ring bearers (crowd goes up), then the couple (crowd at peak). The DJ should be watching the room, not the script, and extending or shortening the couple's walk depending on where the energy is when they cross the threshold.

**Total elapsed time: 5–7 minutes.**

---

### 6:07 PM — First dance

The first dance almost always follows the grand entrance directly. This is the right call. The room is standing, the energy is up, the couple is center floor, the photographer is in position. Moving from the entrance to the first dance without breaking rhythm is the cleanest version.

The first dance runs two to four minutes depending on the song. Most couples ask me to fade the song at the two-minute mark rather than playing the full three-and-a-half minutes — because most people do not want to hold a first dance for three and a half minutes in front of 120 people. If the couple wants the full song, they should practice it at home so they are not standing on the floor at 2:45 with nothing to do.

**Invitation to join:** Some couples invite guests to join the first dance after thirty to sixty seconds. If this is the plan, cue me in advance and I will open the mic to announce it.

---

### 6:12 PM — Parent dances

Father-daughter dance first in most cases, mother-son immediately after. Combined, these run four to six minutes. If both parents are on one side, the dance order can flip — the point is the couple's parents get their moment before dinner service breaks the flow.

Two notes on parent dances that I wish someone had told every couple in advance:

1. If a parent is deceased, the couple often does a brief acknowledgment — a chair with a bouquet, a lit candle, a photo at the sweetheart table — before or after the parent dances. This needs to be communicated to the DJ in advance so the music cues are correct and the mic is ready if anyone speaks.

2. Parent dances where the couple opens the floor to all couples can extend by eight to twelve minutes if the room takes to it. Factor this into your timeline if you want it.

---

### 6:20 PM — Welcome toast

Best man or MOH. Two to three minutes. Mic on, music down to near-zero, guests seated or at least attentive. I announce the toaster by name, bring up the mic, and watch the room. If a toast runs past five minutes, I start watching the caterer — dinner service cannot hold forever for a runaway speech.

The most common mistake with toasts: too many, and no one knows in what order they are happening. If you have four people toasting, give me the list in advance in order, with names and roles, and I will run them clean. If you have four people toasting and I find out at 6:19 PM, someone's introduction will be wrong.

**Recommended maximum:** Three toasts during the first welcome block. Save the rest for mid-dinner if the venue allows standing toasts at tables.

---

### 6:25 PM — Dinner service begins

The caterer should be ready to roll the moment toasts close. The transition from toast to dinner is the most common dead zone in a reception — the DJ brings the music back up, the caterer begins service, and the room needs five minutes to shift gears. Use this time for:

- Dinner music (couple's playlist or curated dinner jazz — something that sounds intentional)
- Coordinator confirming service pace with the caterer
- Any mid-dinner toasts from parents or guests (quick, mic-handheld, two minutes max)

Dinner service typically runs sixty to seventy-five minutes for a plated meal, forty-five to sixty minutes for stations. Family-style can be faster. Buffets are always slower because guests self-direct. Ask the caterer for their realistic service window and add fifteen minutes when planning the timeline.

---

### 7:30–7:45 PM — Cake cutting

After dinner plates clear, before the kitchen crew begins teardown of the main course, is the window for the cake cutting. The exact timing depends on the caterer's service rhythm and the kitchen's need to start slicing.

The cake cutting takes four to six minutes: cue song, couple walks to table, first cut, photographer captures it, couple feeds each other or doesn't, quick round of applause, done. Music continues. Caterer takes the cake back to slice.

I play something slightly up-tempo for the cake cutting — not the slow-dance music of first dances, but not open-dancing volume either. The crowd is mid-evening; something that moves them in the right direction without putting the DJ in front of the moment works better.

**The cake-distribution gap:** From the moment the couple cuts to the moment sliced cake is on tables is typically fifteen to twenty minutes. Do not plan anything formal during this gap. It is dead time and the room knows it. Use it: open the floor, add a mid-dinner toast, let guests mingle.

---

### 7:50 PM — Open dancing begins

This is the block that determines the reputation of the reception. Everything before 7:50 was formality and dinner. What happens from 7:50 to close is why people are still talking about a wedding a year later.

I open the floor with the couple's crowd-starter song — whatever they have told me will bring their specific crowd to the floor. This is not the song I think is a good opener. This is the song that makes their parents, their college friends, their work colleagues, and their cousins all move at the same time. Only the couple knows what that song is. I have seen "Jump" by Kris Kross open a dance floor at 7:50 PM and I have seen it clear one. Know your crowd.

The first fifteen minutes of open dancing is the most important reading time of the night. I watch the floor: who is dancing, who is watching, which songs keep people up and which pull them back to the tables. Every choice I make for the next ninety minutes is based on what I learn in the first fifteen.

**The 70/20/10 rule:** I play 70 percent music the couple has explicitly said they want, 20 percent songs I know from experience work on a floor like this one, and 10 percent real-time reads — songs I decide on in the moment because the floor tells me to. Couples who give me a 150-song must-play list remove the 10 percent and sometimes the 20 percent. The floor suffers.

---

### 8:30 PM — Bouquet toss and garter toss (optional)

If doing them, now is the right window — the floor is warm, energy is up, and there is still an hour left. Pulling this block too early (during dinner) kills the dance floor momentum you have not yet built. Pulling it too late (9:30 PM) means half the single guests have already left.

Some couples skip the garter toss, keep the bouquet toss, or eliminate both. All three are valid. Tell the DJ in advance. A DJ who learns mid-reception that the garter toss is not happening will be fine; a DJ who announces the garter toss to a crowd whose couple is not doing one will not.

**Each takes four to six minutes:** gather single guests, build-up music, toss, applause, transition back to open dancing. Total: ten to fifteen minutes if both are happening.

---

### 8:45–9:45 PM — Peak dancing block

This is the ninety minutes you planned the whole evening around. If the floor is working, the DJ's job is to not break it. No dead-air transitions. No long announcements. No major energy drops. One song feeds the next, and the floor never fully empties.

The signs that this block is going well:
- The floor is at sixty to seventy percent capacity or better
- The couple is dancing
- People who were seated at dinner are now standing at the edge of the floor
- Requests are coming in (a room that feels good makes requests; a room that has checked out has gone back to the bar)

The signs the block is breaking down:
- Songs are running past their energy peak (most songs peak at two to two-and-a-half minutes — experienced DJs transition before the peak breaks)
- The couple is sitting at the sweetheart table
- Guests are leaving for the bar and not coming back to the floor

If the floor is breaking down, I shift genre. If the couple's playlist is skewing older, I drop something younger. If it's skewing too young for the grandparents, I find the crossover song that works for everyone. This is the part of the job that does not go on a playlist.

---

### 9:45 PM — Last call and wind-down

Forty-five minutes to close, I watch the venue coordinator. Most venues start their end-of-night signals here: catering begins teardown of the bar, lighting shifts, room temperature adjusts. The energy naturally softens.

I give the room a verbal signal at 9:45 — "the floor is open for another 45 minutes, make the most of it" or however the couple wants it framed. Some couples want no announcement; some want a clear signal. Decide this in advance.

The last thirty minutes I pull energy back from peak toward something that still feels good but signals wind-down. Not slow-dance music unless the couple specifically wants a slow close. The wrong slow dance at 9:45 empties the floor in five minutes.

---

### 9:55 PM — Last song

The couple should pick the last song. It should be a real choice — not "whatever you think," because the last song is what every guest remembers leaving. It does not have to be the biggest dance song of the night. It can be slow, it can be sentimental, it can be funny. It has to be intentional.

I announce the last song by name. Guests who want to be on the floor for it will be on the floor. The lights come up slightly on the announcement.

---

### 10:00 PM — Send-off (optional)

Sparkler send-offs, flower petals, bubble exits — all require coordination between the couple, the coordinator, the photographer, and the venue. They take five to eight minutes to execute cleanly. If the venue has a hard end at 10:00 PM, a send-off does not work unless the venue coordinator knows about it and has cleared the path in advance.

The DJ's job at the send-off is to play the right song and not compete with the moment. Usually something pulled back, the kind of song the couple might dance to alone in a kitchen at midnight. I fade it out as the couple exits.

## What breaks a timeline (and how to not let it)

**Post-ceremony photos.** The most consistent source of cocktail-hour overrun. Tell the photographer: reception starts at 6:00 PM means photos wrap at 5:45 PM, not 6:10 PM.

**Toasts with no time limit.** Set a limit. Three minutes. Tell the people toasting in advance. If they run long, the coordinator should have a polite signal system.

**Dinner service running long.** Buffets and stations almost always run longer than plated. Ask the caterer for their real estimate, not their optimistic one.

**Family photo gaps.** Many couples schedule "family formals" photos between the ceremony and cocktail hour, which delays the cocktail hour start. Add thirty minutes to the buffer.

**The "just one more song" trap at the end.** Venues have hard stops. "Just one more song" at 10:02 PM in a venue with a 10:00 PM hard cutoff means the venue coordinators are actively doing wind-down procedures while the DJ is playing. Set a real stop time and keep it.

## The run-sheet: how to give your DJ what they need

Every item in this timeline should exist in a written run-sheet that the DJ, the venue coordinator, and the photographer all carry on the night. The run-sheet is not a playlist. It is the working document of the entire evening — names, pronunciations, timing, music cues, notes.

A complete run-sheet for a five-hour reception covers:

- Wedding party list with phonetic pronunciations and roles
- Order of formalities with estimated times
- Music cue for each formality (first dance song, parent dance songs, cake cutting song, last song)
- Toast order with names and roles
- Any special moments (empty-chair acknowledgment, military tribute, guest-specific acknowledgment)
- Photographer's name and how to reach them on the night
- Venue coordinator's name and how to reach them on the night
- Hard venue stop time

The DJ should have a final copy of the run-sheet no later than seven days before the wedding. Any changes after that go to the DJ directly, not through the coordinator.
