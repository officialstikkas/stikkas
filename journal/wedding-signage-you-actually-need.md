# Wedding signage you actually need (and the seven signs nobody reads)

*A 30-year print specialist and 25-year wedding DJ on the signs that earn their square footage*

I've spent thirty years inside print shops and twenty-five years in the DJ booth, which means I've watched more wedding signage get printed *and* used than almost anybody who writes about this for a living. Most of what I read online about wedding signs is written by someone who has never stood at the cocktail-hour-to-reception door and watched 120 people stare at a 12-inch seating chart from 20 feet away.

A wedding sign is a working document, not a decoration. It directs traffic, communicates a rule, or marks a place. If it doesn't do one of those three jobs, it's wall art — which is fine, but call it wall art and budget for it accordingly.

What follows is the field guide. The signs you actually need, the signs that look smart on Pinterest but get walked past, the sizing rules that make a sign readable from across the room, and the print specs that decide whether the sign looks like a wedding investment or a homework printout.

---

## The eight signs that pay their square footage

For most weddings of 80 to 150 guests, eight signs cover everything that needs covering. More than eight and the guests stop reading them; fewer than eight and at least one piece of essential information falls through.

**1. The welcome sign at the entrance.** This is the first thing a guest sees and the only sign that gets photographed by every photographer in attendance. It earns the largest format and the highest production value of any sign at the wedding. Minimum 24 by 36 inches, mounted at eye level on an easel or attached to a sturdy backer. Headline text is the couple's names; sub-copy is the date. That's it. Resist the urge to put a quote on it, a hashtag on it, or a program excerpt on it. The welcome sign's job is to confirm "yes, this is the right wedding" and to anchor a hundred photos.

**2. The ceremony seating sign.** Either a traditional "bride's side, groom's side" or — and this is where I see almost every wedding land in 2026 — a "pick a seat, not a side" sign. The latter is universally readable and signals an inclusive ceremony. Format: 11 by 14 inches on cardstock or 18 by 24 on foam core depending on the ceremony space. Position: at the entry to the ceremony seating, where guests are deciding which way to walk.

**3. The unplugged ceremony sign, if you want one.** The "please put your phones away during the ceremony" sign exists because guests with phones up ruin every aisle photograph. It's not rude to ask. It is rude to write it like an apology — "we know this is unusual, but if you could maybe..." reads as a request the guest can ignore. Write it like a polite directive: "Welcome — please join us in being fully present. Phones away during the ceremony. Photos welcome again at the reception." Eleven by fourteen, on a small easel beside the seating sign.

**4. The bar menu.** If the bar is doing signature cocktails, the bar needs a menu. Not a chalkboard the bartender wrote at five PM — a printed menu, 11 by 17 minimum, that lists the signatures and the standard offerings. A bar with no menu produces three categories of conversation at the bar all night: "what's the signature?" "what do you have?" and "what's in it?" Each conversation is twenty seconds. Multiply by 120 guests times an average of three drink trips and you've added two hours of cumulative bar-line time to the night. A printed bar menu eliminates eighty percent of those questions.

**5. The seating chart.** The single most consequential sign of the entire wedding. The seating chart is the document that determines whether the transition from cocktail hour to reception takes ten minutes or forty. I'll spend more time on this one below — it deserves its own section.

**6. Table numbers.** Five inches tall at minimum, eight inches tall for tables of ten or more. The table number is a wayfinding tool, not a decorative element. If guests cannot read it from the doorway, they wander. Wandering increases the cocktail-hour-to-reception transition by twelve to twenty minutes, which compresses every block of the evening after it.

**7. The guest book or card box sign.** This sign exists because without it, half of the guest book gets ignored and a quarter of the cards end up on the bar. A simple "Please sign our guest book" or "Cards & gifts" sign on the table is enough. Eight by ten inches in a frame or on a small easel. Match it to the welcome sign aesthetically so the whole signage family reads as one wedding, not seven.

**8. The send-off or last-call sign.** Late-night guests look for cues that the night is ending. A "last dance" sign near the bar at the start of the last hour, a "send-off at 10:45" sign near the exit, or both, give the night a shape. Without these signs the band or DJ ends up making three "fifteen-minute warning" announcements over the mic, which deflates the energy of every song after it. A sign lets the announcement happen visually so the music keeps the room moving.

That's the working set. Eight signs, each one doing measurable work for the wedding. Everything beyond this is decoration, and decoration has different rules.

## The seven signs that get walked past

I'll save you the money. These are the signs I see on every wedding Pinterest board and at almost no wedding where they actually got used.

**1. The "in loving memory" sign.** I know. This is hard to put on a list of signs that don't work. But the in-memory sign is a deeply personal acknowledgment, not a guest-directional piece, and most weddings that put it on a sign would be better served by mentioning the people in the program, the toasts, or a candle on the family table. The sign at the entry is usually too far from the seated guests to be read by the people who would want to read it. The fix: put the names where the people who knew them are sitting.

**2. "Eat, drink, and be married" / similar quotes.** These signs are the wedding equivalent of a "live, laugh, love" sign in a kitchen. They make the venue feel less personal, not more. Guests glance and move on. If the wedding has a real piece of family writing, a line from a vow, or a memorable phrase from how the couple actually talks, those can land. Generic quote signs land in the photo and never get a second look.

**3. The Instagram hashtag sign.** Hashtags peaked around 2018 and have been declining as social platforms move away from hashtag-based discovery. By 2026 they're a vestigial wedding artifact. If you want a way for guests to share photos, a simple photo-share app or a shared cloud folder works far better than asking 100 people to remember to type #SmithWedding2026 every time they post.

**4. Multi-quote welcome signs.** "Welcome to the wedding of John and Jane. Today is the first day. Forever begins here. Cheers to love." A welcome sign that includes more than the names and date stops being a welcome sign and becomes a poster. The guest reads two words, sees a wall of text, and moves on. The headline of the welcome sign should be the couple's names. Period.

**5. The cocktail hour menu.** Different from a bar menu. The cocktail hour menu lists the passed apps the caterer is serving. By the time the guest has read what the apps are, the waiter is already past them. Menus belong at the bar (where the guest is making a choice) and at the dinner table (where the menu is a memento). The cocktail hour menu is wall art.

**6. The hashtag rule sign.** "Please don't post photos until after the ceremony." See #3. By 2026 this is over-engineering for a problem that has largely solved itself. The unplugged ceremony sign covers the only version of this rule that still matters.

**7. The thank-you sign.** A "thank you for celebrating with us" sign by the exit reads to guests as: this couple expected me to want one last reminder before I walked out. Almost no guest sees it on the way in (which is when a sign has guest attention) and most guests are tipsy on the way out. The thank-you belongs in a toast and in the thank-you cards the couple writes later. Not on a sign at 10:50 PM.

The pattern: signs that direct traffic, communicate a rule, or mark a place earn their square footage. Signs that communicate sentiment compete with the actual wedding for guest attention and lose.

## The seating chart is the most important sign at the wedding

Of every sign I've watched at every wedding, the seating chart is the one that decides whether the night runs forty-five minutes long. It's worth treating like a working system, not a decorative one.

**Position it where the cocktail hour ends.** The seating chart should not be at the entry, where guests will look at it for ten seconds before walking past it to get a drink. The seating chart should be at the cocktail-hour-to-reception transition — usually a doorway, a hallway, or the entry to the reception space — so guests read it when they are about to go find their seat, not forty-five minutes before.

**Size it to the wedding.** A 100-guest wedding needs a chart that is at least 24 by 36 inches. A 150-guest wedding needs 30 by 40 or two side-by-side charts. The chart needs to be readable by three people at once without anyone elbowing anyone — which means the type needs to be readable from four feet away (so a second guest can read while the first is finishing).

**Alphabetize by last name. Not by table.** This is the single most common mistake on wedding seating charts. A chart organized by table forces every guest to scan every table until they find their own name. A chart organized alphabetically by last name lets the guest find their name in three seconds and read the table number next to it. The first approach takes 60 to 90 seconds per guest; the second takes ten seconds. Multiply by 120 guests at the same doorway and you can do the math on what each layout does to the cocktail-hour-to-reception transition.

**Make the table number bigger than the name.** The guest needs the number. The name is the lookup; the number is the answer. Set the name in 18 to 22 point type, set the table number in 36 to 48 point type. The guest's eye finds the name, jumps right to the number, walks to the table. If the name is bigger than the number the guest has to read every entry to extract the number, which is the same problem as not alphabetizing.

**Light it.** The seating chart belongs in a lit area. If the chart is in a dim entryway, the guest's phone flashlight comes out, the line backs up, and the transition takes twice as long. A single pin spot or a pair of uplights on the chart eliminates the problem.

Treated this way, the seating chart routes 120 guests in under ten minutes. Treated the wrong way, the same chart eats forty.

## The print rules that make a sign readable from across the room

This is the part of wedding signage where my thirty years in print shops actually earns its keep. There are three rules that decide whether a wedding sign looks like a wedding investment or like someone printed it at home, and almost no one writing about wedding signage online seems to know any of them.

**Rule one: build the file at the final print size.** A 24 by 36 welcome sign needs to be built as a 24 by 36 file at 300 DPI. Designers routinely build small files and scale them up at the print shop. Every time, the result is fuzzy edges, jagged type, and a sign that looks like a screenshot blown up. If the file is not built at the final print size, the sign cannot be saved at the print shop. Build it right or build it again.

**Rule two: convert to CMYK with a soft proof.** Screens display RGB. Printers print CMYK. Colors shift between the two — bright blues turn dull, fluorescent magentas turn muddy, rich blacks turn gray. A soft proof is a preview of what the file will look like in CMYK before it prints, and any decent design program (Illustrator, Affinity, even modern Canva exports) can produce one. If the file is sent to the print shop as RGB with no soft proof, the print shop will convert it themselves, and the conversion will surprise you. Colors that were intentional become accidents.

**Rule three: export as PDF/X-1a with embedded fonts.** JPGs are for photographs, not for signs. A JPG of a sign compresses the type and produces visible artifacts at large sizes. A PDF preserves the original vector type and prints crisp. If the fonts aren't embedded, the print shop substitutes a default font and the whole sign changes character. Every print shop accepts PDF/X-1a; every export menu in every design program supports it. Use it.

The signs that look like wedding investments are signs where these three rules were followed. The signs that look like homework printouts are signs where one or more of these rules was skipped. The difference at 24 by 36 inches is visible from across the room.

## The sizing math nobody teaches you

A sign is readable from a distance of about 250 times its cap height. That's an old print-shop rule of thumb, but it works.

- One-inch tall letters are readable from about 25 feet
- Two-inch tall letters are readable from about 50 feet
- Four-inch tall letters are readable from about 100 feet

What this means in practice:

- **Welcome sign:** four-inch tall names, one-and-a-half-inch tall date. Readable from across a parking lot.
- **Seating chart:** half-inch tall name, one-inch tall table number. Readable from four feet (where the next guest in line is reading from).
- **Table numbers:** five to eight inches tall. Readable from the entry to the room.
- **Bar menu:** one-inch tall drink names, half-inch tall ingredients. Readable from the bar.

Most wedding signage I see at real weddings is too small by half on the welcome sign and too small by half on the table numbers. Both are visual furniture that doesn't get used — the welcome sign because guests can't read the headline from where they're being photographed in front of it, the table numbers because guests can't find their table from the doorway.

The fix is just to follow the math.

## Print at home vs. send to a shop

The cutoff is roughly letter size.

**Print at home:** table numbers in frames, bar menus on cardstock, place cards, the guest book sign in a small frame, programs. Anything that fits on letter or legal paper. Use 100lb cover cardstock minimum, set the printer to "best" or "photo" quality, and print on the paper's coated side if it has one. Heavyweight cardstock at 100lb cover or above prints far cleaner than anything thinner.

**Send to a print shop:** welcome sign, seating chart, ceremony signs, any sign larger than 11 by 17. The print shop has access to substrates a home printer cannot use — foam core, gator board, acrylic, plywood, brushed aluminum — and those substrates are what makes a sign read as intentional rather than improvised. A 24 by 36 welcome sign on foam core costs $40 to $60 at most print shops in 2026. The same sign on photo paper from a home printer is impossible to produce in one piece.

If you have a local print shop you trust, build a relationship with one person there for the wedding signage. Bring them all the files at once, get one quote, pick them up in one trip. The cumulative time saved across eight signs is hours.

## The wedding signage timeline

Two weeks before the wedding is the right window to finalize and print the signs. Earlier than that and last-minute changes (RSVPs, seating updates, bar menu tweaks) force a reprint. Closer than two weeks and the print shop's queue can push delivery into the wedding week, which is when you have the least bandwidth to fix anything.

**Six weeks out:** decide which signs you need. Use the eight-sign list above. Sketch each one.

**Four weeks out:** design the signs in the chosen aesthetic family. The welcome sign sets the visual; everything else inherits the typeface, the color palette, and the layout language so the whole set reads as one wedding.

**Two weeks out:** finalize the copy (especially the seating chart, which depends on the final RSVP count). Send the files to the print shop. Confirm pickup date.

**Three days out:** pick up the signs. Inspect every one. The seating chart in particular gets read by every guest — typos on it are the most embarrassing kind of wedding misprint and require an emergency reprint. Read it twice.

**The day before:** signs go to the venue with the rest of the wedding logistics. Welcome sign on its easel in the entry. Seating chart at the cocktail-hour-to-reception transition. Table numbers on each table. Bar menu on the bar. Guest book sign on the guest book table. Send-off sign cued for the late hour.

This timeline is forgiving. The wrong timeline — designing signs the week of the wedding — is how typos and bad sizing make it to the day.

## What this looks like in practice

The wedding signage I see go best at real weddings is a small set, sized correctly, printed well, positioned where the guest is making a decision. The wedding signage I see go badly is a large set, sized incorrectly, printed at home on thin paper, positioned where the guest can't see it or has already decided what to do.

The print specialist's job is to make signs that are *worked*, not signs that are *decorations*. The DJ's job is to call the moment when the sign has done its work and the night moves on. The couple's job is to decide which signs they actually need and to resist the urge to add the ones the Pinterest board suggests.
