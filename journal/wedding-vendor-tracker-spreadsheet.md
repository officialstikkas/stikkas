# Wedding vendor tracker spreadsheet

*The document that prevents the wedding's quiet disasters — the photographer who never confirmed, the caterer whose deposit went unpaid, the DJ who didn't get the venue load-in instructions. A 25-year wedding DJ on the spreadsheet structure that actually holds twelve vendors together.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 7, 2026 · 8 min read**

---

I have been at receptions where the cake arrived an hour late because the baker had the wrong start time written down. I have been at receptions where the photographer drove to the wrong venue because nobody updated her on the day-of address change. I have been at receptions where the florist's crew was still installing the head-table garland when guests started arriving.

None of those problems started on the wedding day. They started six months earlier, when the couple decided to keep vendor information in their email inbox.

The wedding vendor tracker spreadsheet is the document that prevents those problems. Not by being elaborate — by being the one place where every vendor's contract status, deposit, final balance, day-of arrival time, and contact info lives. One sheet. Twelve columns. Updated within forty-eight hours of every signed contract or paid invoice.

This is the spoke that the couples who finish wedding-planning calmly always had, and the couples who finish wedding-planning in a panic always didn't.

## The vendor count almost always surprises couples

Ask most couples how many vendors are at their wedding. The answer is usually "four or five." The real number is closer to twelve.

Here is the typical full-service wedding vendor list for a 100-130 person wedding:

1. **Venue** — the room, the parking, the load-in dock, the venue coordinator.
2. **Catering** — sometimes in-house with the venue, sometimes a separate vendor.
3. **Photographer** — primary day-of coverage, second shooter, deliverables.
4. **Videographer** — if booking, separate from photographer.
5. **DJ or band** — reception music, ceremony sound, MC duties.
6. **Florist** — bouquets, centerpieces, arch, signage decor, installation.
7. **Baker** — wedding cake, dessert table if doing one.
8. **Officiant** — ceremony script, marriage license filing.
9. **Hair stylist** — bride, bridesmaids, sometimes mothers.
10. **Makeup artist** — same crew, sometimes same artist, often different.
11. **Transportation** — wedding party shuttle, guest shuttle if needed, getaway car.
12. **Day-of coordinator** — if hiring (and you should, even part-time).
13. **Rentals** — chairs, linens, tableware, dance floor, sometimes the tent.
14. **Stationery printer** — save-the-dates, invitations, programs, menus, signage.

Fourteen. Even if you fold catering into the venue and skip videographer and rentals, you are still at eleven. Eleven contracts, eleven deposits, eleven final-due dates, eleven day-of arrival times, eleven sets of load-in instructions. You cannot keep eleven of anything in your head for nine months. That is what the spreadsheet is for.

## The twelve columns the tracker actually needs

The minimum viable vendor tracker has twelve columns. Build them on day one — adding columns retroactively five months in is harder than just having them from the start.

> **Vendor category** · **Business name** · **Contact person** · **Phone** · **Email** · **Contract signed (Y/N)** · **Deposit $** · **Deposit paid date** · **Final balance $** · **Final due date** · **Day-of arrival time** · **Notes**

That is the working version. A few of the columns deserve a closer look.

**Day-of arrival time** is the column most templates skip. It is the column that prevents the cake-arrives-late problem. Most vendor contracts specify an arrival window ("4 to 4:30 pm for setup"). Write the start of the window in the column. On the wedding morning, sort the sheet by arrival time — that is your day-of timeline from the vendor side. Cross-check it against your reception timeline and your venue's allowed load-in start. Any conflict shows up in the sort.

**Final balance + final due date** is the cash-flow column most couples wish they had built. Every vendor takes a deposit (25-50% at booking) and the remainder is due somewhere between sixty days and the day before the wedding. The couples who run out of cash in the final stretch are the couples who never sorted by final-due-date — they got hit with six final invoices in the same week and panicked. Sort the sheet by final-due-date in the last sixty days. That view is the spreadsheet's most valuable column combination.

**Contract signed (Y/N)** sounds trivial but isn't. Every two weeks, sort the unsigned list. Walk it. A vendor who emailed a quote three months ago but never sent a signed contract is not a booked vendor — they are a vendor who is also negotiating with two other couples for your date. The sort catches that.

**Notes** is the dumping ground for everything that doesn't fit a column: the photographer's gallery turnaround time, the DJ's overtime rate, the caterer's vegetarian count, the florist's name-spelling preferences for the contract. Anything you'll need at month 8 that you'll forget by then. Write it down.

## The cash-flow truth

A working tracker exposes a financial truth most couples don't see otherwise: the wedding is paid in *two bursts*, not one.

The first burst is the deposit cycle — months 8 through 4. Every vendor wants 25-50% to lock the date. For a $42,000 wedding, deposits total roughly $12,000 to $18,000 across eleven vendors. That cash needs to be available across the deposit window.

The second burst — the bigger one — is the final-balance cycle, weeks 4 through 1 before the wedding. Caterer final-due (typically 14 days out, sometimes 30). Florist final-due (often 7 days out). Photographer final-due (often the wedding day). DJ final-due (often the wedding day). Hair and makeup, paid morning-of. Officiant's honorarium, paid at rehearsal. Venue's overtime allowance, settled day-of. That final stretch is $20,000 to $28,000 of cash flowing out across thirty days.

The tracker is what lets you see the second burst at month 6 instead of month 1. Sort by final-due-date. If the second burst is bigger than you have liquid, you have time at month 6 to move money around, take the bonus you were going to spread out, or push two final-due-dates by negotiation. By month 1, you have no time.

## The arrival-time column is the day-of save

The most useful single column on the day of the wedding is the day-of arrival-time column.

Sort the sheet by arrival time on the morning of the wedding. Hand the printout to your day-of coordinator (or your maid of honor if you don't have one). They now have:

- the order vendors arrive,
- each vendor's contact person and phone,
- load-in window per vendor,
- and the notes (e.g., "caterer needs ice from venue staff," "florist needs water at 3 pm for centerpieces").

That single printout, ten minutes to prepare, prevents 80% of day-of vendor coordination problems. Without it, your maid of honor is taking calls from a baker asking where to deliver the cake — and you are in your makeup chair, not answering your phone.

Build the column from day one. By the time the wedding arrives, it has been filled in for months — not as a panic exercise on Friday afternoon.

## The four highest-leverage uses of the tracker

If you build the spreadsheet right at week 1 and update it every other week, four moments in planning get demonstrably easier.

**1. The month-6 cash check.** Sum every deposit paid plus every final-balance-due. Compare to total budget. If you're already at 80% committed, you're heading for a budget blowout. The tracker is what makes the math visible months earlier than your monthly bank statement would.

**2. The month-4 contract audit.** Sort by "contract signed = No." Walk every row. Either get the contract signed this week or move to a backup vendor while there's still time to find one. Vendors who have been "almost booked" for three months are not booked.

**3. The week-2 final-payment sweep.** Sort by final-due-date in the last fourteen days. This is your week-2 cash flow. Make sure the money is in the account, the credit cards have headroom, and someone is delegated to make each payment. Final-balance-day is not the day to discover that one card got declined.

**4. The morning-of run-sheet.** Sort by day-of arrival time. Print it. Hand it to whoever is running point on the wedding morning. That single sort, that single printout, is the most leveraged document you can hand a day-of coordinator at 6 am.

## Why a spreadsheet instead of a planner app

Three reasons, in the order they matter.

**First, vendors don't speak app.** Every vendor sends quotes as PDFs or emails. Every contract is a signed document. Every revised number arrives as a reply-all from the venue coordinator. Planner apps want you to retype all of that into structured fields, and the moment you stop retyping, the app's data is stale. A spreadsheet absorbs whatever shape the new information comes in.

**Second, your wedding has line items that don't exist in any app's default schema.** The day-of coordinator you booked at month 5. The hairpiece your stylist suggested at the trial. The rehearsal-dinner overflow your in-laws covered. The Uber to the rehearsal. The custom backdrop your photographer asked for. Apps refuse to add new categories; spreadsheets shrug.

**Third, you can share a spreadsheet four ways without giving anybody admin access.** Edit access for your partner. View access for the day-of coordinator. View access (filtered) for the wedding party with just arrival times. View access for the parents with just the budget summary. Apps make all sharing all-or-nothing.

The right tool for a job that involves twelve vendors and fourteen contracts and forty-two payments and four people who need different views of the same data is a Google Sheet. Always.

## The bottom line

Twelve vendors. Fourteen contracts. Forty-two payments across nine months. The spreadsheet is the only document that holds all of that in one place. Build it on day one. Update it every other week. Sort it four different ways at four different moments. The wedding day will be calmer than it would have been without it — and you'll know the vendor's name and phone when the caterer asks at 4 pm where to put the appetizer station.
