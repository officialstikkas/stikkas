    answer: "It depends on whether the vendor owns the business or works for someone else. Contractor vendors — banquet servers, hair and makeup artists working for a studio, drivers — should be tipped 15 to 20 percent of their portion of the bill, the same way you would tip in any service industry. # Wedding vendor guide: how to hire the 12 vendors that actually run your wedding

*The vendor list, the booking order, the four that decide the day, and the questions that separate the vendors who show up from the ones who don't — written from twenty-five years on the loading dock, from the DJ's side of the room.*

**By Oscar Arana · June 8, 2026 · 17 min read**

---

I have worked weddings as a DJ for twenty-five years and loaded into more than four hundred venues. The DJ booth is not where the wedding gets planned. It is, however, the only spot in the room with a clear line of sight to every other vendor for the entire reception — the photographer working the first dance, the videographer covering the parent dances, the caterers wheeling out the buffet at the wrong moment, the florist's runner clearing the ceremony chairs while the toasts are happening on the other side of the room, the bartender getting buried at last call. From the booth you watch the wedding's vendor coordination either work or break, in real time, for five straight hours.

The version that works is the version where the couple did the vendor work in the first nine months — picked twelve vendors against a written list, locked them in the right order, asked the right questions before signing, and held the whole thing together with a tracker spreadsheet that didn't go stale. The version that breaks is the version where vendors were hired one at a time as the couple thought of them, with no spreadsheet, no booking-order discipline, and no shared coordination document — and on the day, three of the twelve don't show up on time, the venue manager doesn't have anyone's contact info, and somebody's mother is texting the photographer at 9 p.m. asking where the family portrait spot is.

The difference between those two weddings is not the budget. It is the vendor process. This guide is the framework I would give a friend planning a wedding from scratch — the twelve vendors, the booking order, the four that matter most, the documents that hold everything together, and the long-form posts behind each one.

## The 12 vendors every full-service wedding actually needs

The count surprises almost every couple I talk to. The starting answer is usually four — venue, photographer, DJ, florist — and the real number is three times that.

The full list, for a wedding of 100 to 130 guests in a US metro in 2026:

1. **Venue** — the building, the grounds, the chairs and tables, the parking, the bathroom infrastructure. Sometimes includes catering, sometimes doesn't. Locks the calendar and the capacity.

2. **Caterer** — food service for the cocktail hour and the reception. In-house at some venues, contracted separately at others. The single most variable expense in the entire wedding.

3. **Photographer** — six to ten hours of coverage, getting-ready photos through the last dance. Delivers the album, the prints, and the photos that will live in your family's hands for the next sixty years.

4. **Videographer** — the second camera. Optional in budget weddings, near-mandatory in coastal-metro weddings. Delivers the highlight reel, the full ceremony, and the long-form film.

5. **DJ or band** — the sound system, the microphone, the music for the ceremony, cocktail hour, and reception. Decides whether the dance floor lives or dies. The single highest variance vendor — a great DJ saves the night, a bad DJ tanks it.

6. **Florist** — the bouquets, the boutonnieres, the centerpieces, the ceremony arch, sometimes the cake florals. Anywhere from a few hundred dollars (DIY-supplemented) to fifteen thousand at a flower-forward wedding.

7. **Baker** — the wedding cake and/or dessert table. Some venues handle this in-house; most do not.

8. **Officiant** — the person who performs the ceremony. A religious officiant from the couple's tradition, a non-denominational officiant hired separately, or a friend ordained online. Wide price range.

9. **Hair stylist** — for the partner getting hair done, plus often the bridal party. Usually on-location.

10. **Makeup artist** — same. Some studios offer both as a package.

11. **Transportation** — the limo, the shuttle, the trolley, or whatever moves the bridal party between the getting-ready location, the ceremony, and the reception. Often skipped at venues where it all happens on-site.

12. **Day-of coordinator** — the person who runs the wedding day. Cues the processional, manages the timeline, deals with whatever goes wrong. Either hired separately or included with the venue.

Optional add-ons, depending on the wedding:

- **Wedding planner** (full-service, not day-of) — runs the entire planning process from booking through wedding day. $4,000 to $12,000.
- **Rentals coordinator** — chairs, linens, tableware, lounge furniture. Sometimes through the venue, sometimes through a separate rental house.
- **Stationery printer** — physical save-the-dates, invitations, programs, escort cards, menus, signage.
- **Calligrapher** — hand-lettered signage, escort cards, envelope addressing.

Pure-services count for a typical wedding: 12. Add a planner and stationery and you're at 14. That is the realistic number — and every one of them needs a contract, a deposit, a final-due date, a day-of arrival time, and a contact person.

## The booking order — why sequence matters more than speed

You cannot book all twelve vendors in the same month. Some of them depend on the others. The booking order is one of the most underappreciated parts of wedding planning — most couples don't realize that the order they hire vendors in determines what each vendor can offer, and at what price.

The order that actually works:

### Wave 1 (12 to 15 months out): the four that decide the day

1. **Venue** — first, always. Every other vendor's pricing, drive time, gear list, and load-in depends on knowing where the wedding is.

2. **Photographer** — second. The top wedding photographers in any market book a full year out for peak Saturdays. Photography is also a vendor you cannot redo — the day happens once, and the photos are the record. The decision deserves the second slot.

3. **DJ or band** — third. Music vendors in the working tier (the people who do this for a living and have the gear) book a year out in major markets. Locking the music vendor early also lets the DJ start the relationship — the music questionnaire, the timeline build, the venue load-in walkthrough — months before the wedding instead of weeks before.

4. **Caterer** — fourth, unless the venue handles catering in-house. The strongest standalone wedding caterers book nine to twelve months in advance for peak dates. Catering is also the largest variable in the budget after the venue, so locking the caterer early lets the rest of the budget firm up.

These four together are typically 65 to 75 percent of the total wedding budget. They are also the four where the difference between a great vendor and a mediocre one is felt the most. If you only deeply vet four vendors, vet these.

- For the photographer: [11 questions to ask a wedding photographer before you book](/stikkas/journal/questions-to-ask-wedding-photographer/)
- For the DJ: [12 questions to ask a wedding DJ before you book](/stikkas/journal/questions-to-ask-wedding-dj/)
- For the caterer: [12 questions to ask your wedding caterer before you sign](/stikkas/journal/questions-to-ask-wedding-caterer/)

They are written from the DJ booth — from somebody who has watched four hundred venues, five hundred photographers, and probably nine hundred caterers actually work, not from a planner who has read about them.

### Wave 2 (6 to 9 months out): the second tier

5. **Florist** — usually books six to nine months out. Some high-end florists book a year out, but the working tier has more capacity than the venue/photographer/DJ tier.

6. **Baker** — six months is usually enough.

7. **Officiant** — depends on the type. Religious officiants from the couple's tradition usually have availability earlier than that; non-denominational officiants and friends-ordained-online book any time.

8. **Hair and makeup** — five to seven months. Trials happen about two months before the wedding.

9. **Transportation** — five months is enough except for niche options (vintage cars, party buses on prom weekend, etc.) that book earlier.

### Wave 3 (3 to 4 months out): the closeout

10. **Rentals** — most rentals quote and book ninety days out.

11. **Day-of coordinator** — three months is enough; some couples wait until two.

12. **Stationery** — invitations should go out eight weeks before the wedding for in-town guests, twelve weeks for destination, so the printer needs to be booked about ten weeks before the invitation send date — typically about four months before the wedding.

The mistake almost every couple makes is collapsing this calendar — booking the florist in month two because they were excited, and then realizing in month nine that the caterer they actually want is gone. Wave 1 is the discipline. The other waves can wait.

## The four vendors that decide the wedding

Of the twelve, four matter more than the others. If you over-budget on four vendors, these are the four. If you over-vet four vendors, these are the four. If you assemble four documents for the day-of binder, these are the four whose contracts and contact sheets go in.

**The venue** is the largest dollar commitment and the most constrained set of decisions downstream of it — the noise ordinance, the capacity, the load-in restrictions, the parking, the kitchen, the bar, the catering policy, the alcohol policy, the music cutoff, the cleanup window. Every other vendor works inside the constraints the venue creates. Pick the venue first and the rest of the vendor list gets easier.

**The photographer** is the only vendor whose product you carry with you for the next sixty years. The wedding lasts six hours. The photos last for the rest of the family's existence. Photography is the wedding's only permanent vendor — every other vendor's work ends when the night ends.

**The DJ or band** decides whether the room hits its peak. A great photographer with a flat dance floor produces a beautiful album of a flat wedding. A great DJ with a mediocre photographer produces a wedding everyone remembers. From the booth, I can tell you which vendor is more replaceable, and it isn't the music vendor.

**The caterer** decides the dinner — and the dinner is the single longest sit-down block of the reception. Bad catering means a 50-minute dead zone at the table. Good catering means the room stays warm through dinner and comes up hot for dancing.

Together these four vendors are 65 to 75 percent of the wedding's budget. They are also the four whose questions deserve the most rigor before signing.

## The supporting cast: the other eight

The remaining eight vendors are real and they matter, but the bar for "good enough" is lower. Pick from the venue's preferred list, get two quotes, check Instagram, and move on.

**The florist** is where the wedding's visual language lives outside the photographs. Florists run the widest price range of any vendor — a friend with a Trader Joe's account can produce competent centerpieces for $300, and a wedding-flower-forward studio can charge $15,000 for the same wedding. Pick a tier that matches the wedding's overall budget; flowers above a $35,000 wedding feel like the wedding is wearing borrowed clothes.

**The baker** is mostly a taste test. The tier that matters is the cake's structural integrity (the cake should survive transport and the cake-cutting moment) and the delivery time. Most working bakers in a metro area can produce a good wedding cake at any of three to five price points.

**The officiant** is the highest-variance vendor on the list, because the choice depends on the couple's religious background and personal preference. A friend-ordained-online officiant is free or near-free and can produce an extraordinary ceremony if the friend writes well. A religious officiant from the couple's tradition costs $200 to $800 and produces the ceremony the couple's family expected. Both are right; pick the one that matches your version of the day.

**Hair and makeup** is a single-day rental of two specialists. The trial run two months out is the real interview. If the trial is wrong, the wedding is wrong. Don't book hair and makeup without a trial.

**Transportation** is mostly a logistics decision, not a creative one. The variable is whether the wedding party arrives together (a shuttle) or whether each person makes their own way (cars). Coastal-metro weddings increasingly use Ubers for the wedding party and a shuttle only for the guests-to-hotel run at the end of the night.

**The day-of coordinator** is the most underrated vendor in the entire twelve. Couples who skip the day-of coordinator end up running the day themselves — and the entire purpose of hiring vendors is so the couple doesn't have to run the day. A good day-of coordinator is $1,200 to $2,500 in most US metros and saves the wedding from itself. If the budget cannot fit one, the maid of honor or best man gets drafted into the role; that is a worse outcome than paying $1,500.

**Rentals** and **stationery** are the cleanup wave. Rentals fill in whatever the venue doesn't provide (chairs, linens, tableware, sometimes tents and lounge furniture); stationery handles the paper side (invitations, programs, escort cards, menus, signage). Both can be sourced in the last 90 days.

## The coordination layer — the spreadsheet that holds everything together

Twelve vendors with twelve contracts, twenty-four deposit and final-due dates, twelve contact people, and twelve day-of arrival times is a hundred-plus data points. No couple holds it in their head. The couples who try end up missing a deposit deadline or a final-due date — and missing a vendor deadline is how a contract gets quietly canceled in month seven.

The fix is a spreadsheet. Twelve columns: vendor category, business name, contact person, phone, email, contract signed (yes/no), deposit amount, deposit paid date, final balance due amount, final paid date, day-of arrival time, notes.

The two columns most templates skip are *day-of arrival time* and *final balance due date.* The first prevents the vendor who shows up at the wrong hour (the cake at 6 p.m. when guests are seated at 5 p.m.; the photographer at 3:30 p.m. when getting-ready started at 2 p.m.). The second prevents the cash-flow crisis three weeks before the wedding when six vendors all want their final payment at once and the couple didn't know they were stacked.
